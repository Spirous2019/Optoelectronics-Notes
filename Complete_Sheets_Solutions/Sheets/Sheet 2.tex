\begin{question}[Question 1]Consider a HeNe laser source of area $A$ that emits 1\,mW of red-light
($h\nu\approx 2.0$\,eV) into a solid angle $\lambda^{2}/A$. Calculate
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
  \item The number of photons emitted per second by this laser source.
  \item The solid angle of emission if $A=80$\,mm$^{2}$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{question}

\subsection*{Solution to Question 1}
% ============================================================

We are given a Helium-Neon (HeNe) laser operating at a power of $P = \SI{1}{\milli\watt}$. The laser emits red light with a photon energy $E_{\mathrm{ph}} = h\nu \approx \SI{2.0}{\electronvolt}$. The beam is emitted into a highly directional solid angle defined by diffraction as $\Omega = \lambda^2/A$, where $A$ is the area of the emitting aperture. We are asked to find the raw photon emission rate and the exact solid angle for a given area.

Power is simply the rate of energy flow. Since light is quantised into discrete photons, the total power $P$ is the number of photons emitted per second, $\Phi$, multiplied by the exact energy of a single photon, $h\nu$. 

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(a) Photon Emission Rate}\quad
Let's first define the energy of a single photon in fundamental SI units (Joules). Since $1 \text{ eV} = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$:
\begin{equation*}
  E_{\mathrm{ph}} = 2.0 \text{ eV} \times \left(1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J/eV}\right) = 3.2 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}
\end{equation*}
The total power $P$ is the photon emission rate $\Phi$ times the photon energy:
\begin{equation*}
  P = \Phi E_{\mathrm{ph}}
\end{equation*}
Dividing both sides by $E_{\mathrm{ph}}$ to isolate the emission rate:
\begin{equation*}
  \Phi = \frac{P}{E_{\mathrm{ph}}} = \frac{1 \times 10^{-3} \text{ W}}{3.2 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
  \boxed{\Phi = 3.125 \times 10^{15} \text{ photons/s}}
\end{equation*}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(b) Solid Angle of Emission}\quad
To find the solid angle $\Omega = \lambda^2/A$, we first need the wavelength $\lambda$. From the photon energy relation $E_{\mathrm{ph}} = hc/\lambda$:
\begin{equation*}
  \lambda = \frac{hc}{E_{\mathrm{ph}}} = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s} \times 3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}}{3.2 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}} \approx 6.21 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m} = 621 \text{ nm}
\end{equation*}
Now, we are given the aperture area $A = 80 \text{ mm}^2 = 80 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}^2$. Substituting this into the diffraction-limited solid angle formula:
\begin{equation*}
  \Omega = \frac{\lambda^2}{A} = \frac{(6.21 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m})^2}{80 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}^2}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
  \Omega = \frac{3.86 \times 10^{-13}}{80 \times 10^{-6}} \approx 4.82 \times 10^{-9} \text{ sr}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
  \boxed{\Omega = 4.83 \times 10^{-9} \text{ steradians}}
\end{equation*}

\noindent\emph{In short:} A standard 1 mW laser outputs roughly 3 thousand trillion photons every second, bunched into an incredibly narrow, diffraction-limited cone of just a few nano-steradians, highlighting the extreme directionality characteristic to laser physics.

\begin{question}[Question 2]Starting from the spectral energy density (J/m$^{3}$-Hz) of the black
body, find an expression of the spectral intensity (watts/m$^{2}$-Hz) emitted by that body.
Then find the photon flux density $\varphi_{\nu}$ (no.\ of photons/m$^{2}$-sec) emitted within
a bandwidth $\Delta\nu$ at frequency $\nu$. Then calculate:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
  \item The number of photons of red light emitted per second by a black body (thermal light
        source) of temperature $T=1000$\,K within a bandwidth of 10\,kHz and a solid angle
        $\lambda^{2}/A$, assuming that it has the same area as the laser source in Problem (1).
  \item The temperature of the black body required to emit the same number of photons per
        second as in Problem (1).
\end{enumerate}
\end{question}

\subsection*{Solution to Question 2}
% ============================================================

We are transitioning from the energy density of a thermal blackbody cavity to calculating the specific directional emission in photons per second. We must derive the relationships between spectral energy density $\rho_\nu$, spectral intensity $I_\nu$, and photon flux $\varphi_\nu$, and then apply them to determine the temperature $T$ a thermal source would need to match the laser from Question 1.

Recall from basic radiometry that the energy density inside an isotropic blackbody cavity is related to the intensity emerging from a small aperture. The flux of energy crossing a unit area in one direction is linked to the density by a factor of $c/4$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Deriving the Expressions}\quad
The spectral intensity $I_\nu$ (radiance integrated over a hemisphere, or outward flux) generated by an isotropic source of spectral energy density $\rho_\nu$ is given by the classic kinetic relation:
\begin{equation*}
  I_\nu = \frac{c}{4} \rho_\nu \quad \text{ [W/(m}^2\cdot\text{Hz)]}
\end{equation*}
To find the photon flux density $\varphi_\nu$ within a narrow bandwidth $\Delta\nu$, we multiply the spectral intensity by the bandwidth to get total intensity, and divide by the single photon energy $h\nu$:
\begin{equation*}
  \varphi_\nu = \frac{I_\nu \Delta\nu}{h\nu} = \frac{c}{4h\nu} \rho_\nu \Delta\nu \quad \text{ [photons/(m}^2\cdot\text{s)]}
\end{equation*}
Notice that $I_\nu$ is the flux emitted over the \emph{entire} forward hemisphere ($2\pi$ steradians). If we want the flux directed strictly into a tiny specific solid angle $\Omega$, we must divide by the Lambertian emission footprint (which adds a factor of $\pi$). The directional intensity (Radiance, $L_\nu$) is $L_\nu = I_\nu/\pi$. Thus, the power emitted into solid angle $\Omega$ by area $A$ is:
\begin{equation*}
  \text{Rate} = \frac{L_\nu \Delta\nu}{h\nu} A \Omega = \frac{c}{4\pi h\nu} \rho_\nu \Delta\nu A \Omega
\end{equation*}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(a) Photon Emission at 1000 K}\quad
Substituting Planck's law for $\rho_\nu = \frac{8\pi h\nu^3}{c^3} \frac{1}{e^{h\nu/k_B T}-1}$ into our Rate equation:
\begin{equation*}
  \text{Rate} = \frac{c}{4\pi h\nu} \left( \frac{8\pi h\nu^3}{c^3} \frac{1}{e^{h\nu/k_B T}-1} \right) \Delta\nu A \left(\frac{\lambda^2}{A}\right)
\end{equation*}
Simplifying algebraically, noting that $A$ cancels out and $c/\nu = \lambda$:
\begin{align*}
  \text{Rate} &= \frac{2\nu^2}{c^2} \frac{1}{e^{h\nu/k_B T}-1} \Delta\nu \lambda^2 \\
  &= \frac{2}{\lambda^2} \frac{1}{e^{h\nu/k_B T}-1} \Delta\nu \lambda^2 \\
  &= \frac{2\Delta\nu}{e^{h\nu/k_B T}-1} \tag{I}
\end{align*}
We are given $h\nu \approx \SI{2.0}{\electronvolt} = \SI{3.2e-19}{\joule}$. At $T=1000\text{ K}$, thermal energy is $k_B T = (1.38 \times 10^{-23})(1000) = 1.38 \times 10^{-20} \text{ J}$. 
The exponent is:
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{h\nu}{k_B T} = \frac{3.2 \times 10^{-19}}{1.38 \times 10^{-20}} \approx 23.19
\end{equation*}
Applying this to equation (I) with $\Delta\nu = 10^4 \text{ Hz}$:
\begin{equation*}
  \text{Rate} = \frac{2(10^4)}{e^{23.19} - 1} = \frac{2 \times 10^4}{1.18 \times 10^{10}}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
  \boxed{\text{Rate}_{1000K} \approx 1.7 \times 10^{-6} \text{ photons/s}}
\end{equation*}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(b) Temperature to Match Laser}\quad
We want the thermal rate to match the laser rate from Question 1 ($\Phi = 3.125 \times 10^{15} \text{ photons/s}$). Because this number is vastly larger than $2\Delta\nu = 20,000$, the denominator $(e^{h\nu/k_B T}-1)$ must be extremely small, meaning the exponent must be tiny.
From (I):
\begin{equation*}
  e^{h\nu/k_B T} - 1 = \frac{2\Delta\nu}{\text{Rate}} \approx \frac{2 \times 10^4}{3.125 \times 10^{15}} = 6.4 \times 10^{-12}
\end{equation*}
Since $e^x - 1 \approx x$ for $x \ll 1$, we can apply the Taylor expansion:
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{h\nu}{k_B T} \approx 6.4 \times 10^{-12}
\end{equation*}
Solving for the temperature $T$:
\begin{equation*}
  T = \frac{h\nu}{k_B(6.4 \times 10^{-12})} = \frac{3.2 \times 10^{-19}}{(1.38 \times 10^{-23})(6.4 \times 10^{-12})}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
  T = \frac{3.2 \times 10^{-19}}{8.83 \times 10^{-35}} \approx 3.62 \times 10^{15} \text{ K}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
  \boxed{T \sim 3.6 \times 10^{15} \text{ K}}
\end{equation*}

\noindent\emph{In short:} A thermal source at $1000$ K is virtually dark in the single-mode red spectrum, emitting less than one photon per week. To match the directional spectral brightness of a standard 1 mW laser, a blackbody would need to be hotter than the core of a star, proving that laser amplification is fundamentally distinct from thermal emission.

\begin{question}[Question 3]Show that the Einstein model of light-matter interaction applies for
broad-band light sources without the need to include the line-shape function. When must the
line-shape function be incorporated?
\end{question}

\subsection*{Solution to Question 3}
% ============================================================

The Einstein light-matter interaction rates (stimulated emission and absorption) are mathematically driven by the spectral overlap between the incident radiation energy density $\rho_\nu$ and the atomic transition lineshape $g(\nu)$. We are asked to justify why we can drop $g(\nu)$ entirely for broadband light sources.

The true, exact stimulated transition rate $W_{12}$ is defined as an integral over all frequencies because both the incident field and the atom possess spectral widths:
\begin{equation*}
  W_{12} = B_{12} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \rho(\nu) g(\nu) d\nu
\end{equation*}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Executing the Broadband Limit}\quad
For a broadband source (like a thermal incandescent lamp or an LED), the spectral radiation energy density $\rho(\nu)$ is essentially flat and constant over the extremely narrow frequency range where the atomic lineshape $g(\nu)$ is non-zero (typically a few GHz).
Because $\rho(\nu)$ varies negligibly within the atomic bandwidth, we can approximate it as a constant $\rho_{\nu_o}$ evaluated at the atomic resonance center $\nu_o$, and pull it safely out of the integral:
\begin{equation*}
  W_{12} \approx B_{12} \rho_{\nu_o} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(\nu) d\nu
\end{equation*}
By its probabilistic definition, the atomic lineshape function is normalized such that its total area is unity:
\begin{equation*}
  \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(\nu) d\nu = 1
\end{equation*}
Therefore, the interaction rate reduces mathematically strictly to:
\begin{equation*}
  \boxed{W_{12} = B_{12} \rho_{\nu_o}}
\end{equation*}
This effectively bypasses the lineshape function entirely, forming the classic, simplified Einstein model originally historically used for macroscopic thermal analysis.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{When to incorporate the line-shape $g(\nu)$?}\quad
The full integral (and thus the lineshape) \emph{must} be retained when the incident light source is \textbf{narrow-band} relative to the atomic transition. A prime example is probing an atom with a highly monochromatic laser. In this case, the radiation spectrum is mathematically closer to a Dirac delta function $\rho(\nu) \approx \rho_0 \delta(\nu-\nu_{L})$. Applying this shifts the integral to collapse onto the lineshape evaluated exclusively at the laser frequency:
\begin{equation*}
  W_{12} = B_{12} \rho_0 g(\nu_L)
\end{equation*}

\noindent\emph{In short:} If the light is broadband, it acts like a uniform bucket of energy over the atom, making the atom's internal spectral preference mathematically irrelevant. If the light is a laser, the atom's exact broadened lineshape dictates whether it "sees" the laser or not.

\begin{question}[Question 4]Show that the line-shape function in the case of inhomogeneous Doppler
broadening may be written in the form
\begin{equation*}
  \bar{g}(\nu)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}g_{\nu_{o}}\!\left(\nu-\nu_{o}\frac{V}{c}\right)P(V)\,dV
\end{equation*}
where
\begin{equation*}
  P(V)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma_{V}^{2}}}\,e^{-V^{2}/2\sigma_{V}^{2}}.
\end{equation*}
Then find an expression of $\bar{g}(\nu)$ in the two limits
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
  \item $\sigma_{V}\to 0$ and
  \item $\Delta\nu\to 0$.
\end{enumerate}
\emph{Hint:} $\lim_{\sigma_{V}\to 0}P(V)=\delta(V)$ and
$\lim_{\epsilon\to 0}\epsilon/(\epsilon^{2}+x^{2})=\pi\delta(x)$.
\end{question}

\subsection*{Solution to Question 4}
% ============================================================

Real laser lineshapes generally suffer from both homogeneous decay (Lorentzian $g_{\nu_o}(\nu)$) and inhomogeneous Maxwellian thermal velocity motion (Doppler shift probability $P(V)$). The result is the convoluted Voigt profile $\bar{g}(\nu)$. We are asked to find the two extreme mathematical limits of this integral.

The Voigt profile convolves the shifted homogeneous lineshape (centered at $\nu_o(1+V/c)$ due to Doppler shifting) with the classical 1D velocity probability distribution:
\begin{equation*}
  \bar{g}(\nu)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}g_{\nu_{o}}\!\left(\nu-\nu_{o}\frac{V}{c}\right)P(V)\,dV
\end{equation*}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(1) Limit as $\sigma_V \to 0$ (No Thermal Motion)}\quad
As the standard deviation of velocity approaches zero (gas temperature approaches absolute zero), the Maxwellian distribution peaks infinitely sharply at $V=0$. The hint reminds us that $\lim_{\sigma_{V}\to 0}P(V)=\delta(V)$. Substituting this into the convolution:
\begin{equation*}
  \bar{g}(\nu) = \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}g_{\nu_{o}}\!\left(\nu-\nu_{o}\frac{V}{c}\right) \delta(V)\,dV
\end{equation*}
By the sifting property of the Dirac delta function, the integral collapses to the remaining integrand evaluated exactly at the spike $V=0$:
\begin{equation*}
  \boxed{\bar{g}(\nu) = g_{\nu_{o}}(\nu)}
\end{equation*}
Physically, taking thermal velocity away simply returns the pure homogeneous Lorentzian profile inherent to the stationary atoms.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(2) Limit as $\Delta\nu \to 0$ (Negligible Homogeneous Broadening)}\quad
If the intrinsic natural homogeneous linewidth $\Delta\nu$ is extremely small (which physically means very long lifetime), the Lorentzian lineshape $g_{\nu_o}$, which is centred at $\nu_o$, collapses to a delta function in its argument. Specifically, for a Lorentzian $g_{\nu_o}(\xi) = (\Delta\nu/2\pi)/((\xi-\nu_o)^2+(\Delta\nu/2)^2)$, using the hint $\lim_{\epsilon\to 0}\epsilon/(\epsilon^2+x^2)=\pi\delta(x)$:
\begin{equation*}
  g_{\nu_o}(\xi) \;\xrightarrow{\Delta\nu\to 0}\; \delta(\xi - \nu_o).
\end{equation*}
Setting $\xi = \nu - \nu_o V/c$ (the argument in the Voigt integral), this becomes:
\begin{equation*}
  g_{\nu_o}\!\left(\nu - \nu_o \frac{V}{c}\right) \to \delta\!\left(\nu - \nu_o\frac{V}{c} - \nu_o\right)
  = \delta\!\left(\nu - \nu_o\!\left(1+\frac{V}{c}\right)\right).
\end{equation*}
To evaluate the integral over $dV$, we use the scaling property $\delta(aV - b) = \frac{1}{|a|}\delta(V - b/a)$. Here the coefficient of $V$ inside the delta is $-\nu_o/c$:
\begin{equation*}
  \delta\!\left( -\frac{\nu_o}{c} V + (\nu - \nu_o) \right) = \frac{c}{\nu_o} \delta\!\left( V - c\frac{\nu-\nu_o}{\nu_o} \right)
\end{equation*}
We substitute this scaled Dirac delta back into the Voigt integral:
\begin{equation*}
  \bar{g}(\nu) = \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} \frac{c}{\nu_o} \delta\!\left( V - c\frac{\nu-\nu_o}{\nu_o} \right) P(V) \,dV
\end{equation*}
Applying the sifting property, we evaluate the velocity distribution $P(V)$ at $V = c\frac{\nu-\nu_o}{\nu_o}$:
\begin{align*}
  \bar{g}(\nu) &= \frac{c}{\nu_o} P\left(c\frac{\nu-\nu_o}{\nu_o}\right) \\
  &= \frac{c}{\nu_o} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma_V^2}} \exp\left[ -\frac{\left(c \frac{\nu-\nu_o}{\nu_o}\right)^2}{2\sigma_V^2} \right]
\end{align*}
We define the Doppler spectral variance as $\sigma_D = \sigma_V \frac{\nu_o}{c}$. Substituting this into the amplitude footprint:
\begin{equation*}
  \boxed{\bar{g}(\nu) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma_D^2}} \exp\left[ -\frac{(\nu-\nu_o)^2}{2\sigma_D^2} \right]}
\end{equation*}

\noindent\emph{In short:} The Voigt convolution gracefully reduces to the purely homogeneous Lorentzian when atoms are "frozen" in place, and perfectly maps to the purely inhomogeneous Gaussian Doppler profile when natural line broadening is negligible.

\begin{question}[Question 5]The Doppler line-shape function of an inhomogeneously broadened laser is
given by
\begin{equation*}
  \bar{g}(\nu)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma_{D}}
  \exp\!\left[-\frac{(\nu-\nu_{o})^{2}}{2\sigma_{D}^{2}}\right],
  \qquad
  \sigma_{D}=\frac{1}{\lambda_{o}}\sqrt{\frac{k_{B}T}{M}}.
\end{equation*}
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
  \item Show that the full-width half maximum (FWHM) of the Doppler linewidth is given by
        $\Delta\nu=\sqrt{8\ln 2}\,\sigma_{D}$.
  \item Consider the $0.6328\,\mu$m transition of Ne in He-Ne laser. If $M=20u$ where
        $u=1.66\times10^{-27}$\,kg is the atomic mass unit, then find the FWHM of the Doppler
        linewidth at $T=300$\,K.
\end{enumerate}
\end{question}

\subsection*{Solution to Question 5}
% ============================================================

We are working with the pure inhomogeneous Gaussian Doppler line-shape function for gas lasers. We need to algebraically derive the full-width at half-maximum (FWHM) of this exponential curve, and then plug in empirical parameters for the prominent red emission line of the internal Neon atoms inside a He-Ne laser tube.

The FWHM represents the absolute frequency separation between the two points where the spectral intensity falls to exactly half of its peak central value.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(a) Mathematical Extraction of Doppler FWHM}\quad
By inspection, the peak of the pure Gaussian lineshape occurs when the exponent goes to zero (at exact resonance $\nu = \nu_o$):
\begin{equation*}
  \bar{g}(\nu_o) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma_D}
\end{equation*}
The FWHM points occur at the specific frequencies $\nu$ where the amplitude drops to half of this maximum:
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma_D} \exp\left[-\frac{(\nu-\nu_o)^2}{2\sigma_D^2}\right] = \frac{1}{2} \left[ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma_D} \right]
\end{equation*}
Canceling out the normalisation coefficient on both sides simplifies the expression immediately:
\begin{equation*}
  \exp\left[-\frac{(\nu-\nu_o)^2}{2\sigma_D^2}\right] = \frac{1}{2}
\end{equation*}
Taking the natural logarithm of both sides to isolate the quadratic distance:
\begin{equation*}
  -\frac{(\nu-\nu_o)^2}{2\sigma_D^2} = \ln(1/2) = -\ln(2)
\end{equation*}
Multiplying by $-2\sigma_D^2$ yields the exact algebraic footprint of the FWHM roots:
\begin{equation*}
  (\nu-\nu_o)^2 = 2\sigma_D^2 \ln(2)
\end{equation*}
Taking the square root grants two symmetric transition points bridging the line central frequency:
\begin{equation*}
  \nu - \nu_o = \pm \sigma_D \sqrt{2\ln(2)}
\end{equation*}
The full span of the width $\Delta\nu$ is the difference between these two bounding roots:
\begin{equation*}
  \Delta\nu = \left(\nu_o + \sigma_D\sqrt{2\ln 2}\right) - \left(\nu_o - \sigma_D\sqrt{2\ln 2}\right)
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
  \boxed{\Delta\nu = \sigma_D \sqrt{8\ln 2} \approx 2.355 \sigma_D}
\end{equation*}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(b) Empirical Computation for Neon-20}\quad
We are given an operating temperature $T=300\text{ K}$, optical wavelength $\lambda_o = \SI{0.6328}{\micro\meter} = 6.328 \times 10^{-7}\text{ m}$, and strict mass $M = 20(1.66 \times 10^{-27}\text{ kg}) = 3.32 \times 10^{-26}\text{ kg}$. The Boltzmann constant is $k_B = 1.38 \times 10^{-23}\text{ J/K}$.
First, we solve for the fundamental Doppler spectral deviation $\sigma_D$:
\begin{equation*}
  \sigma_D = \frac{1}{\lambda_o} \sqrt{ \frac{k_B T}{M} }
\end{equation*}
Substituting our raw values:
\begin{equation*}
  \sigma_D = \frac{1}{6.328 \times 10^{-7}} \sqrt{ \frac{(1.38 \times 10^{-23})(300)}{3.32 \times 10^{-26}} }
\end{equation*}
\begin{align*}
  \sigma_D &= \frac{1}{6.328 \times 10^{-7}} \sqrt{ \frac{4.14 \times 10^{-21}}{3.32 \times 10^{-26}} } \\
  &= \frac{1}{6.328 \times 10^{-7}} \sqrt{ 1.247 \times 10^5 } \\
  &= \frac{353.1}{6.328 \times 10^{-7}} \approx 5.58 \times 10^8 \text{ Hz}
\end{align*}
Applying the FWHM relationship derived above:
\begin{equation*}
  \Delta\nu \approx 2.355 (5.58 \times 10^8 \text{ Hz}) \approx 1.31 \times 10^9 \text{ Hz}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
  \boxed{\Delta\nu_{\mathrm{Doppler}} \approx 1.31 \text{ GHz}}
\end{equation*}

\noindent\emph{In short:} Thanks to thermodynamic velocity profiles, typical low-pressure gas lasers experience inhomogeneous line smearing on the order of gigahertz, making $\Delta\nu_{\mathrm{Doppler}}$ fundamentally wider than the natural homogeneous line lifetime broadening limit.

\begin{question}[Question 6]Show that the change in the energy of emitted photons in a transition
between two energy levels, $E_{1}$ and $E_{2}$, is given by
\begin{equation*}
  \Delta E_{\mathrm{ph}}=\frac{h(1/\tau_{1}+1/\tau_{2})}{2\pi},
\end{equation*}
where $\tau_{1}$ and $\tau_{2}$ are the lifetimes of the two energy levels. Explain how this
change in photon energy conforms with the uncertainty principle. What would $\Delta E_{\mathrm{ph}}$
be if the lower energy level is the ground state? Find the corresponding absorption cross section
at the central (resonance) frequency $\nu_{o}\equiv(E_{2}-E_{1})/h$. Under which conditions is
this absorption cross section maximum? Find an expression of this maximum.
\end{question}

\subsection*{Solution to Question 6}
% ============================================================

This question touches strictly on the foundational quantum uncertainty principle applied to photon emission. Atoms transition from Level 2 ($E_2$, lifetime $\tau_2$) to Level 1 ($E_1$, lifetime $\tau_1$). We must formally prove the energy linewidth $\Delta E_{\mathrm{ph}}$, dissect its implication, evaluate it against a true unbroken ground state, and finally extract the peak absorption cross-section condition.

In fundamental quantum mechanics, it is fundamentally impossible to measure the exact energy of a state if that state only exists for a finite temporal lifetime $\tau$. Heisenberg’s famous relation dictates that $\Delta E \Delta t \ge h/4\pi$. Therefore, the absolute uncertainty in an electronic state’s energy is fundamentally tied exactly to how fast it decays.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{1. Uncertainty in Photon Emission Energy}\quad
By the uncertainty principle, the intrinsic energy uncertainty of any atomic state $i$ is constrained rigidly by its temporal lifetime $\tau_i$:
\begin{equation*}
  \Delta E_i = \frac{\hbar}{\tau_i} = \frac{h}{2\pi \tau_i}
\end{equation*}
When a transitional photon is emitted ($E_2 \to E_1$), the total photon energy uncertainty combines the independent energy uncertainties of both the upper and lower discrete levels:
\begin{equation*}
  \Delta E_{\mathrm{ph}} = \Delta E_1 + \Delta E_2
\end{equation*}
Substituting directly yields the exact natural linewidth equation:
\begin{equation*}
  \boxed{ \Delta E_{\mathrm{ph}} = \frac{h}{2\pi}\left( \frac{1}{\tau_1} + \frac{1}{\tau_2} \right) }
\end{equation*}
Dividing this entire equation by $h$ reveals the fundamental homogeneous spectral linewidth: $\Delta\nu = \frac{1}{2\pi}(1/\tau_1 + 1/\tau_2) = \frac{1}{2\pi\tau_1} + \frac{1}{2\pi\tau_2}$, i.e.\ the sum of the individual decay rates divided by $2\pi$. This conforms mathematically exactly with the uncertainty principle because the shorter the particle stays in a state, the blurrier its corresponding physical energy measurement must become.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{2. Transition to Ground State}\quad
If the lower level $E_1$ represents the absolute ground state, the electron lives there infinitely unless physically perturbed. By definition, $\tau_1 \to \infty$. Therefore, $1/\tau_1 \to 0$. The uncertainty collapses tightly to solely the variance inherent to the upper excited state:
\begin{equation*}
  \boxed{\Delta E_{\mathrm{ph}} \to \frac{h}{2\pi \tau_2} }
\end{equation*}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{3. Maximal Absorption Cross-Section}\quad
The peak absorption cross-section fundamentally relates the macroscopic atomic transitions to the microscopic lineshape evaluated perfectly at resonance $\nu_o$. From the theoretical Einstein equations:
\begin{equation*}
  \sigma(\nu_o) = A_{21} \frac{\lambda^2}{8\pi} g(\nu_o)
\end{equation*}
For a purely homogeneously broadened lifetime transition, the lineshape assumes a Lorentzian profile. Evaluated perfectly at resonance, $\max g(\nu) = 2/(\pi \Delta\nu)$. Substituting this in yields:
\begin{equation*}
  \sigma(\nu_o) = \frac{\lambda^2}{8\pi} A_{21} \frac{2}{\pi \Delta\nu} = \frac{\lambda^2}{4\pi^2} \frac{A_{21}}{\Delta\nu}
\end{equation*}
Under what conditions is this cross section an absolute mathematical maximum?
The cross section maximizes when the total lineshape width $\Delta\nu$ is \emph{as narrow as physically possible}. \subsection*{Solution to Question 7}
% ============================================================

We are working with a homogeneous bulk medium possessing defined metrics: $\tau_r = \SI{230}{\micro\second}$, $\lambda_o = \SI{1.06}{\micro\meter}$, refractive index $n = 1.82$, and an isolated Lorentzian lineshape width $\Delta\nu = \SI{3e12}{\hertz}$. Absorption cross-section maps the "effective shadow area" an atom casts via the expression $\sigma(\nu_o) = \frac{\lambda^2}{8\pi n^2 \tau_r} g(\nu_o)$. The total macroscopic power attenuation parameter $\alpha$ is simply the individual atomic shadowy footprint geometrically scaled across the entire bulk atomic population difference $\Delta N$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(a) Absorption Cross Section at $\lambda_o$}\quad
A normalised Lorentzian profile peaks sharply exactly at resonant frequency $\nu_o$ yielding:
\begin{equation*}
  g(\nu_o) = \frac{2}{\pi \Delta\nu}
\end{equation*}
Applying our massive $3$ THz FWHM:
\begin{equation*}
  g(\nu_o) = \frac{2}{\pi (3 \times 10^{12} \text{ Hz})} \approx 2.12 \times 10^{-13} \text{ s}
\end{equation*}
Now, establishing the in-medium wavelength $\lambda = \lambda_o/n = 1.06/1.82 \approx 0.5824\,\mu\text{m}$ and incorporating the exact cross-section equation:
\begin{equation*}
  \sigma(\nu_o) = \frac{\lambda^2}{8\pi\tau_r} g(\nu_o)

We aggressively substitute our entire operational stack of medium constraints:
\begin{equation*}
  \sigma(\nu_o) = \frac{(0.5824 \times 10^{-6})^2}{8\pi (230 \times 10^{-6})} \times (2.12 \times 10^{-13})

\begin{align*}
  \sigma(\nu_o) &= \frac{3.39 \times 10^{-13}}{5.78 \times 10^{-3}} \times 2.12 \times 10^{-13} \\
  &= (5.86 \times 10^{-11}) \times (2.12 \times 10^{-13})
\end{align*}
\begin{equation*}
  \boxed{\sigma(\nu_o) \approx 1.24 \times 10^{-23} \text{ m}^2}
\end{equation*}

  \item If the average number of photons per state is given by the Bose-Einstein distribution

\subsection*{Formative Question -- Cavity Density of States}
% ============================================================

We are mapping the massive theoretical density of trapped photon energy states $M(\nu)$ inherently locked inside a 3D resonant metal boundary cavity. Using zero-field metal boundary constraints, we trace acceptable spatial k-vectors, build a three-dimensional volume in geometric momentum space, extract spectral density, map it against probabilistic thermal mechanics (Bose-Einstein), and finally compress the entire process down to a 1D fiber-line waveguide parallel limit.

In any standing wave cavity, waves cannot exist uniformly; rather, they form harmonic nodes precisely dictating that exactly an integer multiple of half-wavelengths must mathematically map across the specific dimensional bounds $L$. The number of modes $N$ within frequency $\nu$ equates exactly with the volumetric sphere mapped in continuous k-space.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(a) 3D Optical Cavity Density of States}\quad
For a simple standing cubic box of side limits $L$, the spatial electric field must fall continuously to 0 exactly upon the geometric walls. Consequently, allowed wavevectors are completely discretised:
\begin{equation*}
  k_x = m_1 \frac{\pi}{L}, \quad k_y = m_2 \frac{\pi}{L}, \quad k_z = m_3 \frac{\pi}{L}
\end{equation*}
where $m_1, m_2, m_3$ form exclusively positive integers.
Because photons contain fundamentally two independent polarization spin directions per single mapped axial wavevector, we multiply the resultant geometric modal volumes by an exact factor of 2.
The aggregate amount of states strictly inside a bounded spherical k-space radius $k$ scales rigidly with the total spherical volume encompassing one strict all-positive coordinate octant:
\begin{equation*}
  N(k) = 2 \times \left( \frac{1}{8} \cdot \frac{4\pi}{3} k^3 \right) \times \left(\frac{1}{(\pi/L)^3}\right)
\end{equation*}
Substituting exactly $k = 2\pi\nu/c$ mapping spatial vector into raw optical frequency domain:
\begin{equation*}
  N(\nu) = \frac{\pi}{3} \left( \frac{2\pi\nu}{c} \right)^3 \frac{L^3}{\pi^3} = \frac{\pi}{3} \frac{8\pi^3 \nu^3}{c^3} \frac{V}{\pi^3} = \frac{8\pi V}{3c^3} \nu^3
\end{equation*}
The raw "Density of States" $M(\nu)$ mathematically represents the absolute raw derivative of modes isolated strictly per discrete frequency band per unit geometric physical Volume ($V = L^3$):
\begin{equation*}
  M(\nu) = \frac{1}{V} \frac{dN(\nu)}{d\nu} = \frac{1}{V} \frac{d}{d\nu}\left[ \frac{8\pi V}{3c^3} \nu^3 \right]
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
  \boxed{ M(\nu) = \frac{8\pi \nu^2}{c^3} }
\end{equation*}

The total spatial energy density precisely incorporates:

2. The fundamental probability expectation distribution representing how heavily packed these modes strictly are by photons ($f(\nu)$).

\begin{equation*}

\end{equation*}
Substituting perfectly the geometric states against the Bose-Einstein thermodynamic thermal statistic requirement:
\begin{equation*}

\end{equation*}
This perfectly equates entirely to the historic Planck Blackbody mathematical law!

\medskip

\end{equation*}

\end{equation*}
Substituting $k = 2\pi\nu/c$:

The resultant 1D density metric evaluated isolated per strict 1D unit absolute length $L$ yields:
\begin{equation*}

\begin{equation*}
