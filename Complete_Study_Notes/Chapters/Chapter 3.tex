At the close of the previous chapter, we derived the fundamental macroscopic propagation equation for the photon flux $\Phi$, arriving at the compact result $\Phi(z) = \Phi(0)\,e^{\gamma z}$ where $\gamma = \sigma(N_2 - N_1)$ is the gain coefficient. The key insight was that the exponential growth of the field depends entirely on the \emph{sign} of $\gamma$: if $N_2 > N_1$, the medium amplifies; if $N_2 < N_1$, it absorbs. Everything that follows in this chapter is really an answer to one question: \emph{how do we engineer a real medium to have $N_2 > N_1$, and what are the performance consequences when we do?}

We will build the theory in three stages. First, we identify the physical conditions — population inversion — that make amplification possible in the first place. Second, we write the full rate equations governing the atomic populations and solve them at steady state, which gives us the saturation physics that governs how the gain evolves with signal power. Third, we translate those atomic results into the engineering parameters that characterise a real amplifier: gain $G$, bandwidth, and noise figure. The chapter closes with the Erbium-Doped Fibre Amplifier (EDFA), the technology that directly implements everything we derive here.

Unless explicitly stated otherwise, we work throughout in the \textbf{homogeneous broadening} regime.

% =============================================================
\section{Population Inversion}
% =============================================================

\subsection{What It Is and Why We Need It}

Population inversion is the state of matter in which the upper energy level $E_2$ contains \emph{more} atoms than the lower energy level $E_1$: that is, $N_2 > N_1$. This phenomenon is called inversion because it \textbf{inverts} the natural thermodynamic ordering of the populations. Since $\gamma = \sigma(N_2 - N_1)$, a positive population difference directly makes $\gamma > 0$ — the necessary condition for optical amplification.

The analogy with a water pump is exact: just as you need a pump to move water from a lower reservoir to a higher one against gravity, you need a \textbf{pump} to move atoms from $E_1$ to $E_2$ against the Boltzmann distribution.

\subsection{The Pumping Mechanism}

The key point is that we must create population inversion \emph{before} the signal light enters the medium. The pump must excite atoms from their ground state up to the excited level, and it must do so fast enough to maintain $N_2 > N_1$ continuously.

The pump can in principle be any energy source that atoms can absorb: an electron beam whose electrons collide with atoms and donate kinetic energy, or a separate light source (typically at a different frequency) whose photons are absorbed. In either case, the role of the pump has nothing to do with the signal we want to amplify — the pump operates on the atomic populations before the signal is even present.

We characterise the pump by its \textbf{pumping rate} $R$, defined as the number of atoms extracted from a level (or injected into a level) per unit time per unit volume (units: atoms~m$^{-3}$~s$^{-1}$). Practically, we model the system with \textbf{two independent pumps}:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item $R_1$: the pump that extracts atoms \emph{out of} $E_1$ and sends them down to the ground state $E_0$ below it. By depleting $E_1$, this pump helps achieve $N_1 < N_2$.
  \item $R_2$: the pump that injects atoms \emph{into} $E_2$ from the ground state $E_0$ below it. By filling $E_2$, this pump helps achieve $N_2 > N_1$.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 3/pumping_mechanism.jpg}
    \caption{The Pumping Mechanism: External energy alters populations against the thermal Boltzmann distribution, evacuating the lower state and filling the upper state to achieve inversion.}
\end{figure}

This pumping always originates from the ground state $E_0$, because that is where the overwhelming majority of atoms reside under thermal equilibrium — it makes physical sense to pump from the most densely populated level.

\begin{keyinsight}[Is a Monochromatic Pump Required?]
One might worry: if the pump light must have exactly the photon energy $E_2 - E_0$ to excite atoms from $E_0$ to $E_2$, then the pump itself would need to be a laser — which is circular! In practice, however, real light sources contain a small but finite range of frequencies. Because different energy levels in the medium respond to different frequencies within that range, the pump can excite transitions into several nearby levels, not just $E_2$. As long as the overall effect is that $N_1$ drops and $N_2$ rises, the population inversion condition can still be achieved. The key condition remains that the dominant desired transition is driven efficiently.
\end{keyinsight}

\subsection{The Metastable State Requirement}

There is one more condition that is absolutely necessary for population inversion to be sustainable: the upper level $E_2$ must be a \textbf{metastable state}. A metastable state is an energy level with an unusually long lifetime — much longer than the lifetimes of the other levels involved. Why is this so critical?

After the pump has placed atoms in $E_2$, those atoms will eventually decay back down by spontaneous emission. If the lifetime of $E_2$ is very short, atoms will immediately fall back down before stimulated emission from the signal can exploit them, making it impossible to maintain $N_2 > N_1$. If, on the other hand, the lifetime of $E_2$ is very long (metastable), atoms pile up in $E_2$ and stay there long enough to sustain the inversion.

For sustained population inversion, the physical requirements are therefore clear: we need (1) a strong pump to bulldoze atoms from $E_1$ to $E_2$, and (2) the upper level $E_2$ must be metastable, meaning it intrinsically holds onto its atoms much longer than the other levels involved.

\section{Rate Equations}
% =============================================================

\subsection{Structure of the Rate Equations}

Now we're ready to write the full, general \textbf{rate equations} — the differential equations that describe how $N_2$ and $N_1$ evolve in time. We derive these by taking the modified Einstein model (which gave us stimulated emission and absorption terms) and adding the new pump terms $R_1$ and $R_2$. Note that we have now left the small-signal world entirely; the rate equations are \textbf{general} and apply for any signal power.

Let's first identify all the processes that change $N_2$ and $N_1$, keeping track of the sign of each contribution. For the upper level $E_2$:

\begin{equation*}
  \frac{dN_2}{dt} = \underbrace{R_2}_{\substack{\text{pump into} \\ E_2 \text{ from } E_0}} - \underbrace{\frac{N_2}{\tau_{sp}}}_{\substack{\text{spont. emission} \\ \text{from } E_2}} - \underbrace{\sigma\,\Phi_\nu\,N_2}_{\substack{\text{stim. emission} \\ \text{from } E_2}} + \underbrace{\sigma\,\Phi_\nu\,N_1}_{\substack{\text{absorption} \\ \text{from } E_1 \text{ into } E_2}}
\end{equation*}

where $\tau_{sp}$ is the total spontaneous emission lifetime out of $E_2$, combining both available decay channels:
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{1}{\tau_{sp}} = \frac{1}{\tau_{21}} + \frac{1}{\tau_{20}}
\end{equation*}
Here $\tau_{21}$ is the spontaneous lifetime for $E_2 \to E_1$ and $\tau_{20}$ for $E_2 \to E_0$.

For the lower level $E_1$:
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{dN_1}{dt} = \underbrace{-R_1}_{\substack{\text{pump out of} \\ E_1 \text{ to } E_0}} - \underbrace{\frac{N_1}{\tau_1}}_{\substack{\text{spont. emission} \\ \text{from } E_1}} + \underbrace{\frac{N_2}{\tau_{21}}}_{\substack{\text{spont. emission} \\ \text{from } E_2 \text{ into } E_1}} + \underbrace{\sigma\,\Phi_\nu\,N_2}_{\substack{\text{stim. emission} \\ \text{from } E_2 \text{ into } E_1}} - \underbrace{\sigma\,\Phi_\nu\,N_1}_{\substack{\text{absorption} \\ \text{from } E_1}}
\end{equation*}

where $\tau_1$ is the lifetime of $E_1$ (how fast atoms drain out of $E_1$ by spontaneous emission to levels below it).

\subsection{Important Notes on the Rate Equations}

\textbf{Note 1 — Special case where $E_1$ is the ground state:} If $E_1$ is the lowest level (ground state) and there is no level below it, then atoms in $E_1$ have nowhere to go. There is no pump $R_1$ and the lifetime is $1/\tau_1 = 0$, i.e.\ $\tau_1 \to \infty$. Physically, no energy can be released from the ground state since it has no lower level to transition to. In this case $R_1 = 0$.

\textbf{Note 2 — Forbidden transitions:} In practice, some transitions between energy levels are forbidden by quantum-mechanical selection rules. This simply means that the corresponding spontaneous emission term does not appear in the rate equations for those specific level pairs. The physics of forbidden transitions is beyond our scope here, but it is important to know they exist.

\textbf{Note 3 — How many atoms must the pump move?} To achieve population inversion ($N_2 > N_1$), at least half the atoms originally in $E_1$ must be transferred to $E_2$. That is, the pump must move at least $N/2$ atoms (where $N$ is the total atom density) upward before inversion occurs.

\textbf{Note 4 — Three-level and four-level lasers:} Depending on how many energy levels are actively involved in the pumping and lasing cycle, we classify lasers as follows.

\textbf{Three-level laser:} Here the pumping excites atoms from $E_1$ (the ground state) up to a third level $E_3$. Atoms then quickly decay from $E_3$ down to the metastable level $E_2$ via non-radiative emission (releasing heat, not light). The lasing transition is then from $E_2$ down to $E_1$. In this scheme, since $E_1$ is the ground state, we always have $R_1 = 0$. The difficulty with a three-level laser is that the lower lasing level \emph{is} the ground state, which is heavily populated, so a large pump power is needed to invert the population.

\textbf{Four-level laser:} Here the pumping excites atoms from $E_1$ (the ground state) up to a fourth level $E_4$. Atoms quickly decay from $E_4$ to the metastable level $E_3$ (the upper lasing level). The lasing transition is from $E_3$ down to $E_2$, and atoms then rapidly drain out of $E_2$ back to $E_1$. Since $E_2$ empties quickly (short $\tau_2$), the lower lasing level stays nearly empty and population inversion is achieved much more easily than in the three-level scheme. This is why most practical lasers use the four-level design.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 3/three_vs_four_level_systems.jpg}
    \caption{Three-Level vs. Four-Level Systems: The four-level architecture is vastly superior because its terminal lasing level empties instantly, making population inversion practically effortless compared to the three-level design.}
\end{figure}

An important observation about both designs: the photons produced by stimulated emission have a \emph{lower} frequency (longer wavelength) than the photons absorbed by the pump. This is physically sensible — the pump drives atoms up to a higher level, and the atoms then lase on a smaller gap (between intermediate levels), releasing less energy per photon than was pumped in.

\textbf{Note 5 — Transparency condition:} If $N_2 = N_1$ exactly, then $N_2 - N_1 = 0$ and therefore $\gamma_0 = 0$. This means the medium neither absorbs nor amplifies the signal — light passes through completely unchanged. This phenomenon is called the \textbf{transparency condition}. It is the boundary between the absorbing regime ($N_2 < N_1$) and the amplifying regime ($N_2 > N_1$). Achieving transparency is a prerequisite for crossing into gain.

\section{The Three-Level Laser System}

Up to this point we have described the pump in terms of two abstract rates $R_1$ and
$R_2$ — we never asked where these rates come from or how they depend on the system
parameters.  Now we turn to the most practically important laser architecture —
the \textbf{three-level laser system} — and work through its rate equations
explicitly.  This is not just an academic exercise: the Erbium-doped fibre amplifier
(EDFA) that we will meet at the end of this chapter is a three-level system.

\subsection{System Architecture}

The three-level laser has three energy levels $E_1 < E_2 < E_3$.  The level
assignments are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $E_1$: the \textbf{ground state} (lower lasing level), with population
    density $N_1$.
    \item $E_2$: the \textbf{metastable upper lasing level}, with population
    density $N_2$ and spontaneous lifetime $\tau_{21}$.
    \item $E_3$: the \textbf{pump level} to which atoms are excited.  The decay
    $E_3 \to E_2$ is assumed to be very fast ($\tau_{32} \to 0$, i.e.\ atoms
    pumped into $E_3$ immediately fall to $E_2$).  As a result, the population
    of $E_3$ is negligibly small: $N_3 \approx 0$.
\end{itemize}

The total density of active atoms is fixed:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    N_a = N_1 + N_2 + N_3 \approx N_1 + N_2. 
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

Because $E_3$ empties instantly into $E_2$, pumping $E_3$ is equivalent to
pumping directly into $E_2$.  The pump rate $R$ (atoms per unit volume per unit
time transferred from $E_1$ to $E_2$ via $E_3$) plays the role of the source
term in the rate equations.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 3/three_level_pumping_architecture.jpg}
    \caption{System Architecture: Physical realization of a 3-level optical pumping scheme, showcasing the rapid non-radiative decay $\tau_{32}$ creating the population inversion bottleneck at $E_2$.}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Case 1: Constant Pump Rate, No Signal (\texorpdfstring{$\Phi = 0$}{Phi=0})}

We first ask: what population inversion can the pump establish \emph{before}
any signal is present?  With $\Phi = 0$ (no signal) and a constant pump rate
$R$ (atoms~m$^{-3}$~s$^{-1}$, independent of the populations), the rate
equations for $N_3$ and $N_2$ are:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{dN_3}{dt} = R - \frac{N_3}{\tau_{32}} = 0
    \quad \Longrightarrow \quad N_3 = \tau_{32}\,R \approx 0
    \tag{I}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dN_2}{dt} = \frac{N_3}{\tau_{32}} - \frac{N_2}{\tau_{21}} = 0
    \quad \Longrightarrow \quad N_2 = \tau_{21}\,R
    \tag{II}
\end{equation}

The equation for $N_1$ is redundant --- it follows from \textup{(I)} and \textup{(II)} together with the constraint $N_1 + N_2 = N_a$. We do not need to write it separately.

From the constraint, $N_1 = N_a - N_2 = N_a - \tau_{21}R$.  The population
inversion is:
\begin{equation*}
    N \equiv N_2 - N_1 = \tau_{21}R - (N_a - \tau_{21}R) = 2\tau_{21}R - N_a.
\end{equation*}

\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    N_0 = 2R\tau_{21} - N_a
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

This is the \textbf{small-signal population inversion} of the three-level system.

\subsubsection*{Threshold Pump Rate}

For positive inversion ($N_0 > 0$, hence $\gamma_0 > 0$), we need:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    2R\tau_{21} - N_a > 0
    \quad \Longrightarrow \quad
    R > R_\text{th} \equiv \frac{N_a}{2\tau_{21}}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

This is the \textbf{threshold pump rate} for the three-level system.  It says:
the pump must transfer atoms from $E_1$ to $E_2$ at a rate fast enough to more
than half-empty the ground state — we need to move more than $N_a/2$ atoms out
of $E_1$ into $E_2$ to achieve $N_2 > N_1$.  This is one of the fundamental
difficulties of the three-level laser (compared to the four-level laser):
since the lower lasing level is the heavily-populated ground state, the pump
must first fight its way through a very large population before achieving
inversion.

Without any driving signal, the physics dictates that the pump establishes
$N_0 = 2R\tau_{21} - N_a$. This mathematically sets the minimum required pump rate at $R > N_a/(2\tau_{21})$ just to break even on inversion.

    \caption{Population Inversion Dynamics: Linear dependence of the inversion $N_0$ on the steady-state pump rate $R$, illustrating the critical threshold $R_\text{th}$.}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Case 2: Constant Pump Rate, With Signal (\texorpdfstring{$\Phi \neq 0$}{Phi not=0})}

Now we introduce the signal: $\Phi \neq 0$.  The signal drives stimulated
transitions between $E_2$ and $E_1$.  The rate equations become:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{dN_3}{dt} = R - \frac{N_3}{\tau_{32}} = 0
    \quad \Longrightarrow \quad N_3 = \tau_{32}\,R \approx 0
    \tag{I}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dN_2}{dt} = \frac{N_3}{\tau_{32}} - \frac{N_2}{\tau_{21}}
    - \sigma\Phi(N_2 - N_1) = 0
    \tag{II}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dN_1}{dt} = \frac{N_2}{\tau_{21}}
    + \sigma\Phi(N_2 - N_1) - R = 0
    \tag{III}
\end{equation}

The third equation is redundant — it is the negative of the second, added to the time derivative of the conservation law $N_a = N_1 + N_2$. We work only with \textup{(I)} and \textup{(II)}.

From equation~(I): $N_3 \approx 0$, so equation~(II) becomes:
\begin{equation*}
    R = \frac{N_2}{\tau_{21}} + \sigma\Phi(N_2 - N_1).
\end{equation*}
We denote $N \equiv N_2 - N_1$ as before and substitute $N_1 = N_a - N_2 - N_3
\approx N_a - N_2$:
\begin{equation*}
    R = \frac{N_2}{\tau_{21}} + \sigma\Phi\,N.
\end{equation*}
We also have $N_2 - N_1 = N \Rightarrow 2N_2 = N + N_a \Rightarrow N_2 = (N + N_a)/2$.
Substituting:
\begin{equation*}
    R = \frac{N + N_a}{2\tau_{21}} + \sigma\Phi\,N.
\end{equation*}
Solving for $N$:
\begin{equation*}
    R - \frac{N_a}{2\tau_{21}} = N\!\left(\frac{1}{2\tau_{21}} + \sigma\Phi\right).
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
    N = \frac{R - \dfrac{N_a}{2\tau_{21}}}{\dfrac{1}{2\tau_{21}} + \sigma\Phi}
    = \frac{2R\tau_{21} - N_a}{1 + 2\sigma\Phi\tau_{21}}.
\end{equation*}

We recognise the numerator as $N_0 = 2R\tau_{21} - N_a$ — the no-signal population inversion established by the pump — and define the saturation photon flux density for the three-level system:
\begin{equation*}
    \Phi_s \equiv \frac{1}{2\sigma\tau_{21}},
\end{equation*}
which allows us to write the result in the standard saturation form:

\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    N = \frac{N_0}{1 + \Phi/\Phi_s}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

This is exactly the same saturation form we derived in the general case, now
with the explicit identification $\Phi_s = 1/(2\sigma\tau_{21})$ for the
three-level system.  The gain coefficient follows immediately:
\begin{equation*}
    \gamma = \sigma N = \frac{\sigma N_0}{1 + \Phi/\Phi_s}
    = \frac{\gamma_0}{1 + \Phi/\Phi_s}.
\end{equation*}

Setting $\Phi = 0$ in the saturation form $N = N_0/(1+\Phi/\Phi_s)$ recovers Case~1 exactly — as expected.

\subsection{Case 3: Optical Pump, With Signal (\texorpdfstring{$\Phi \neq 0$, $R = \sigma_p\Phi_p N_1$}{realistic pump model})}

In a real optical pumping scheme, the pump rate is \emph{not} independent of
the atomic populations.  A pump laser of photon flux density $\Phi_p$ and
absorption cross-section $\sigma_p$ for the $E_1 \to E_3$ transition pumps
atoms out of $E_1$ at a rate proportional to the population of $E_1$ itself:
\begin{equation*}
    R = \sigma_p\,\Phi_p\,N_1.
\end{equation*}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 3/optical_pumping_mechanics.jpg}
    \caption{Optical Pumping Mechanics: Absorption of the $\Phi_p$ pump flux physically depleting the ground state population $N_1$.}
\end{figure}

This is the most realistic model and it is the one used in the EDFA analysis.
The rate equations (at steady state, with $N_3 \approx 0$) are now:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{dN_3}{dt} = \sigma_p\Phi_p N_1 - \frac{N_3}{\tau_{32}} = 0
    \;\Longrightarrow\; N_3 = \sigma_p\Phi_p N_1\,\tau_{32} \approx 0
    \tag{I}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dN_2}{dt} = \sigma_p\Phi_p N_1 - \frac{N_2}{\tau_{21}}
    + \sigma\Phi(N_1 - N_2) = 0
    \tag{II}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dN_1}{dt} = -\sigma_p\Phi_p N_1 + \frac{N_2}{\tau_{21}}
    + \sigma\Phi(N_2 - N_1) = 0
    \tag{III}
\end{equation}

Note the sign change in the stimulated term in \textup{(III)}: from $E_1$'s
perspective, stimulated emission reduces $N_1$ (atoms move from $E_1$ up to $E_2$
during absorption) and increases $N_1$ (atoms fall from $E_2$ to $E_1$ during
stimulated emission).  The \emph{net} stimulated rate that appears in $dN_1/dt$
is $\sigma\Phi(N_2 - N_1)$ contributing positively (net return to $E_1$).

From \textup{(II)}, at steady state:
\begin{equation*}
    \sigma_p\Phi_p N_1 + \sigma\Phi(N_1 - N_2) = \frac{N_2}{\tau_{21}}.
\end{equation*}
Adding \textup{(III)} gives: $0 = 0$ (redundant). We use the constraint
$N_1 + N_2 = N_a$ to eliminate $N_1 = N_a - N_2$.  Let $N \equiv N_2 - N_1$,
so $N_2 = (N_a + N)/2$ and $N_1 = (N_a - N)/2$.  Substituting into
equation~(II):
\begin{equation*}
    \sigma_p\Phi_p \cdot \frac{N_a - N}{2} - \sigma\Phi\,N
    = \frac{(N_a + N)/2}{\tau_{21}}.
\end{equation*}
Multiply through by $2\tau_{21}$:
\begin{equation*}
    \sigma_p\Phi_p\tau_{21}(N_a - N) - 2\sigma\Phi\tau_{21}\,N
    = N_a + N.
\end{equation*}
Expand and collect all $N$ terms on the right:
\begin{equation*}
    \sigma_p\Phi_p\tau_{21}N_a - N_a
    = N\left(1 + \sigma_p\Phi_p\tau_{21} + 2\sigma\Phi\tau_{21}\right).
\end{equation*}
Dividing:
\begin{equation}
    N = \frac{N_a(\sigma_p\Phi_p\tau_{21} - 1)}
    {1 + \sigma_p\Phi_p\tau_{21} + 2\sigma\Phi\tau_{21}}. \tag{I} 
\end{equation}

We now define the \textbf{small-signal population inversion} — the inversion that exists before any signal is present, i.e.\ with $\Phi = 0$:
\begin{equation}
    N_0 = N_a\,\frac{\sigma_p\Phi_p\tau_{21} - 1}{1 + \sigma_p\Phi_p\tau_{21}}. \tag{II} 
\end{equation}
and the saturation photon flux density:
\begin{equation*}
    \Phi_s = \frac{1 + \sigma_p\Phi_p\tau_{21}}{2\tau_{21}\sigma}.
\end{equation*}
This then takes the standard saturation form:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    N = \frac{N_0}{1 + \Phi/\Phi_s}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

And the gain coefficient is $\gamma = \sigma N = \gamma_0/(1+\Phi/\Phi_s)$ as before.

\subsubsection*{Threshold Condition for Case 3}

For positive inversion ($N_0 > 0$), we need the numerator $N_a(\sigma_p\Phi_p\tau_{21} - 1)$ to be positive:
\begin{equation*}
    \sigma_p\Phi_p\tau_{21} - 1 > 0
    \quad \Longrightarrow \quad
    \Phi_p > \frac{1}{\sigma_p\tau_{21}} \equiv \Phi_{p,\text{th}}.
\end{equation*}

This is the \textbf{threshold pump photon flux} for optical pumping of a
three-level system.  The pump laser must be strong enough to excite atoms out
of $E_1$ faster than they relax back — only then can inversion build up.

\subsection{Case 4: Optical Pump, No Signal (\texorpdfstring{$\Phi = 0$}{Phi=0})}

Setting $\Phi = 0$ in the Case~3 result gives the population inversion
established by optical pumping alone (before the signal arrives):
\begin{equation*}
    N_0 = N_a\,\frac{\sigma_p\Phi_p\tau_{21} - 1}{1 + \sigma_p\Phi_p\tau_{21}}.
\end{equation*}
This is identical to the small-signal result derived above, and the threshold
condition remains $\Phi_p > 1/(\sigma_p\tau_{21})$.  If $\Phi_p \to \infty$
(extremely strong pumping), $N_0 \to N_a$ — complete population inversion,
with all atoms in $E_2$ and none in $E_1$, which is the ideal limit.

The beautiful synthesis here is that the three-level laser system universally yields the explicit saturation form
$N = N_0/(1+\Phi/\Phi_s)$ in all cases. The specific $N_0$ and $\Phi_s$ adapt to the pumping model used. Under optical pumping, the hard threshold $\Phi_p > 1/(\sigma_p\tau_{21})$ tells us the pump flux must literally outpace the spontaneous decay rate just to keep $E_2$ heavier than $E_1$. This entire three-level mathematical framework serves as the direct engine for the EDFA example at the end
of this chapter.

\section{Steady-State Analysis}
% =============================================================

\subsection{Case 1: No Input Signal (\texorpdfstring{$\Phi = 0$}{Phi=0}) --- Preparing the Inversion}

The three-level system gave us concrete intuition for how pumping and saturation work in one important specific architecture. We now step back and derive the equivalent results for the fully \textbf{general two-pump model} with abstract rates $R_1$ and $R_2$, which reveals the fundamental lifetime condition governing whether population inversion is achievable regardless of the specific architecture.

We study the steady-state populations before any signal is injected. At steady state the time derivatives vanish ($d/dt = 0$), and with no signal present ($\Phi = 0$) all stimulated-emission and absorption terms vanish. The rate equations simplify to:

For $E_2$ (setting $dN_2/dt = 0$ and $\Phi = 0$):
\begin{equation}
  R_2 - \frac{N_2}{\tau_{sp}} = 0 \tag{I} 
\end{equation}

For $E_1$ (setting $dN_1/dt = 0$ and $\Phi = 0$):
\begin{equation}
  -R_1 - \frac{N_1}{\tau_1} + \frac{N_2}{\tau_{21}} = 0 \tag{II} 
\end{equation}

From \textup{(I)}, rearranging directly for $N_2$:
\begin{equation*}
  N_2 = \tau_{sp}\,R_2
\end{equation*}

From \textup{(II)}, we isolate $N_1$ by moving $N_1/\tau_1$ to the left:
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{N_1}{\tau_1} = \frac{N_2}{\tau_{21}} - R_1
\end{equation*}
Multiplying both sides by $\tau_1$:
\begin{equation*}
  N_1 = \tau_1\left(\frac{N_2}{\tau_{21}} - R_1\right)
\end{equation*}
Substituting our result $N_2 = \tau_{sp}\,R_2$:
\begin{equation*}
  N_1 = \tau_1\left(\frac{\tau_{sp}\,R_2}{\tau_{21}} - R_1\right)
\end{equation*}

We now compute the population difference $N_0 = N_2 - N_1$:
\begin{equation*}
  N_0 = N_2 - N_1 = \tau_{sp}\,R_2 - \tau_1\left(\frac{\tau_{sp}\,R_2}{\tau_{21}} - R_1\right)
\end{equation*}
Distributing $\tau_1$ through the parentheses:
\begin{equation*}
  N_0 = \tau_{sp}\,R_2 - \frac{\tau_1\,\tau_{sp}\,R_2}{\tau_{21}} + \tau_1\,R_1
\end{equation*}
Factoring $R_2\,\tau_{sp}$ from the first two terms:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
  N_0 = R_2\,\tau_{sp}\!\left(1 - \frac{\tau_1}{\tau_{21}}\right) + R_1\,\tau_1
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

This is the \textbf{population inversion parameter} in the case of no signal input. It tells us the population difference that the pump establishes before any signal is present.

\subsection{Condition for Positive Population Inversion}

For amplification we need $N_0 > 0$, i.e.\ $N_2 > N_1$. Looking at the expression for $N_0$, since $R_2, \tau_{sp}, R_1, \tau_1$ are all positive quantities, the critical factor is the sign of $\left(1 - \tau_1/\tau_{21}\right)$. Population inversion requires:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
  \left(1 - \frac{\tau_1}{\tau_{21}}\right) > 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \frac{\tau_1}{\tau_{21}} < 1 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \tau_1 < \tau_{21}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 3/steady_state_condition.jpg}
    \caption{Steady-State Population Condition: Visualizing the dynamic inequality $\tau_1 < \tau_{21}$. The lower state must act as an extremely wide drain to prevent a population bottleneck from suppressing the inversion.}
\end{figure}

This condition has a beautiful physical interpretation: it says that the \emph{lifetime of the lower lasing level} $E_1$ must be \emph{shorter} than the spontaneous emission lifetime for the $E_2 \to E_1$ transition. In other words, $E_1$ must empty out faster than $E_2$ fills it from above. If $E_1$ drains quickly, the lower-level population stays small, and the upper-level population accumulates — exactly what we need for inversion. This directly confirms the earlier argument that the lower lasing level should have a short lifetime (which is why the four-level laser is easier to invert than the three-level laser).

Structurally, the necessary and sufficient condition for holding a population inversion at steady state, in the absence of any signal, is entirely dictated by the simple lifetime inequality $\tau_1 < \tau_{21}$.

% =============================================================
\section{With Input Signal: Saturation and the General Case}
% =============================================================

\subsection{Population Inversion with a Signal Present}

Now we'll introduce the signal: $\Phi \neq 0$. We again look at steady state ($d/dt = 0$), but now we keep all the stimulated emission and absorption terms. The rate equations become:

For $E_2$:
\begin{equation}
  R_2 - \frac{N_2}{\tau_{sp}} - \sigma\,\Phi\,N_2 + \sigma\,\Phi\,N_1 = 0 \tag{I} 
\end{equation}

For $E_1$:
\begin{equation}
  -R_1 - \frac{N_1}{\tau_1} + \frac{N_2}{\tau_{21}} + \sigma\,\Phi\,N_2 - \sigma\,\Phi\,N_1 = 0 \tag{II} 
\end{equation}

To explicitly solve these two simultaneous equations for $N = N_2 - N_1$, we first isolate $N_2$ from \textup{(I)}, grouping the stimulated terms as $-\sigma\,\Phi(N_2 - N_1) = -\sigma\,\Phi\,N$ and noting $\tau_{sp} = \tau_2$:
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{N_2}{\tau_2} = R_2 - \sigma\,\Phi\,N \quad \Longrightarrow \quad N_2 = \tau_2(R_2 - \sigma\,\Phi\,N)
\end{equation*}

Next, we isolate $N_1$ from \textup{(II)} by moving $N_1/\tau_1$ to the left side and factoring the $\sigma\,\Phi$ terms:
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{N_1}{\tau_1} = -R_1 + \frac{N_2}{\tau_{21}} + \sigma\,\Phi(N_2 - N_1) = -R_1 + \frac{N_2}{\tau_{21}} + \sigma\,\Phi\,N
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
  N_1 = \tau_1\left( -R_1 + \sigma\,\Phi\,N \right) + \frac{\tau_1}{\tau_{21}}N_2
\end{equation*}

We now substitute $N_1$ directly into the definition of $N$:
\begin{equation*}
  N = N_2 - N_1 = N_2 - \left[ \tau_1\left( -R_1 + \sigma\,\Phi_\nu\,N \right) + \frac{\tau_1}{\tau_{21}}N_2 \right]
\end{equation*}

Factoring $N_2$ and isolating $N$ terms:
\begin{equation*}
  N = N_2\left(1 - \frac{\tau_1}{\tau_{21}}\right) + \tau_1\,R_1 - \tau_1\,\sigma\,\Phi\,N
\end{equation*}

Now we substitute our earlier expression $N_2 = \tau_2(R_2 - \sigma\,\Phi\,N)$:
\begin{equation*}
  N = \tau_2(R_2 - \sigma\,\Phi\,N)\left(1 - \frac{\tau_1}{\tau_{21}}\right) + \tau_1\,R_1 - \tau_1\,\sigma\,\Phi\,N
\end{equation*}

Expand the first term and collect all pure $N$ configurations onto the left side:
\begin{equation*}
  N + \tau_2\,\sigma\,\Phi\,N\left(1 - \frac{\tau_1}{\tau_{21}}\right) + \tau_1\,\sigma\,\Phi\,N = \tau_2\,R_2\left(1 - \frac{\tau_1}{\tau_{21}}\right) + \tau_1\,R_1
\end{equation*}

Factor $N$ entirely on the left side:
\begin{equation*}
  N\left[ 1 + \sigma\,\Phi\left( \tau_2\left(1 - \frac{\tau_1}{\tau_{21}}\right) + \tau_1 \right) \right] = \tau_2\,R_2\left(1 - \frac{\tau_1}{\tau_{21}}\right) + \tau_1\,R_1
\end{equation*}

We immediately recognise that the right side of this equality is exactly equal to the no-signal population inversion $N_0$ derived in the previous section. We also define the bracketed temporal factor attached to $\sigma\,\Phi_\nu$ as a new effective saturation time constant $\tau_s$:
\begin{equation*}
  \tau_s = \tau_2\left(1 - \frac{\tau_1}{\tau_{21}}\right) + \tau_1 = \tau_2 - \frac{\tau_2\,\tau_1}{\tau_{21}} + \tau_1 = \tau_2 + \tau_1\left(1 - \frac{\tau_2}{\tau_{21}}\right)
\end{equation*}

Dividing through by the bracket yields our final explicit solution for $N$:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
  N = N_2 - N_1 = \frac{N_0}{1 + \tau_s\,\sigma\,\Phi_\nu}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

\subsection{Saturation Photon Flux Density, Intensity, and Power}

To write this result more compactly, we define the \textbf{saturation photon flux density}:
\begin{equation*}
  \Phi_s = \frac{1}{\tau_s\,\sigma}
\end{equation*}
Substituting into the expression for $N$:
\begin{equation*}
  N = \frac{N_0}{1 + \Phi/\Phi_s}
\end{equation*}
Since $\Phi \propto I \propto P$, we can write equivalent forms in terms of intensity $I$ and power $P$:
\begin{equation*}
  N = \frac{N_0}{1 + I/I_s} = \frac{N_0}{1 + P/P_s}
\end{equation*}
where $I_s$ is the \textbf{saturation intensity} and $P_s$ is the \textbf{saturated power}. Note carefully that $N \neq N_0$ when a signal is present: the signal itself perturbs the populations (it stimulates atoms down from $E_2$ and promotes atoms up from $E_1$), so the population difference is always smaller with a signal than without.

The physical friction is clear: the presence of a signal necessarily crushes its own population inversion from $N_0$ down to $N = N_0/(1+P/P_s)$. The signal's own intensifying photons aggressively drive stimulated transitions that forcibly equalise the two working populations.

The gain coefficient in the general case is:
\begin{equation*}
  \gamma = \sigma\,N = \frac{\sigma\,N_0}{1 + P/P_s} = \frac{\gamma_0}{1 + P/P_s}
\end{equation*}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 3/gain_saturation_curve.jpg}
    \caption{Gain Saturation Curve: The available gain progressively collapses as the intensified signal physically depletes the population inversion that supports it.}
\end{figure}

This is the \textbf{saturated gain coefficient}. It is a function of $\Phi$ (or equivalently, of $P$) — it is no longer constant. Since $\gamma$ depends on the signal power, the system is nonlinear in the general case. This confirms why we cannot use the simple constant-$\gamma_0$ solution outside the small-signal limit.

\subsection{Two Operating Regimes Summarised}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{General (saturated) case} ($P_\text{in}$ comparable to $P_s$, or $P_\text{out}$ comparable to $P_s$):
    \begin{align*}
      \Phi &\neq 0, \quad N = \frac{N_0}{1+P/P_s}, \quad N \neq N_0 \\
      \gamma &= \sigma N = \frac{\gamma_0}{1+P/P_s} \quad\text{(saturated gain coeff., function of signal)}
    \end{align*}
  \item \textbf{Small-signal case} ($P_\text{in} \ll P_s$ \textbf{and} $P_\text{out} \ll P_s$):
    \begin{align*}
      \Phi &\approx 0, \quad N = N_0, \quad \gamma = \gamma_0 = \sigma N_0 = \text{const.}
    \end{align*}
\end{enumerate}

In practice, to decide which regime applies, it is best to compare the \emph{output} power against $P_s$, because even if the input is small, the signal may have grown large enough by the exit of the amplifier to cause saturation. If $P_\text{out} \ll P_s$, use the small-signal formulae. If $P_\text{out}$ is comparable to $P_s$, use the general case.

Consequently, the gain coefficient $\gamma$ is continually crushed as the signal scales up—a hard physical bottleneck known universally as \textbf{gain saturation}. This operates exactly like the gain compression seen in any RF electronic amplifier. There is a strict maximum power the amplifier can linearly push; push harder, and the gain simply collapses.

% =============================================================
\section{Parameters of an Optical Amplifier}
% =============================================================

Now that we know how to create population inversion and how the signal grows or saturates, we can characterise our \textbf{optical amplifier} properly. Any amplifier — optical or electronic — is described by three fundamental parameters: \textbf{gain} $G$, \textbf{bandwidth} $\Delta\nu$, and \textbf{noise performance}. Let's find each one.

% ------- GAIN -------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Gain \texorpdfstring{$G$}{G}}

The gain of any amplifier is the ratio of output power to input power:
\begin{equation*}
  G = \frac{P_\text{out}}{P_\text{in}}
\end{equation*}

We must now find explicit expressions for $G$ in both operating regimes.

\subsubsection{Case 1: Small-signal gain $G_0$}

As we already know from the small-signal section, when $P_\text{in} \ll P_s$, the gain coefficient is constant: $\gamma = \gamma_0$. The propagation equation is:
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{dP}{dz} = \gamma_0\,P
\end{equation*}
Integrating from $z=0$ to $z=L$ (where $L$ is the physical length of the gain medium):
\begin{equation*}
  \int_{P_\text{in}}^{P_\text{out}} \frac{dP}{P} = \int_0^L \gamma_0\,dz
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
  \left[\ln P\right]_{P_\text{in}}^{P_\text{out}} = \gamma_0\,L
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
  \ln P_\text{out} - \ln P_\text{in} = \gamma_0\,L
\end{equation*}
Using the logarithm rule $\ln a - \ln b = \ln(a/b)$:
\begin{equation*}
  \ln\!\left(\frac{P_\text{out}}{P_\text{in}}\right) = \gamma_0\,L
\end{equation*}
Exponentiating both sides:
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{P_\text{out}}{P_\text{in}} = e^{\gamma_0 L}
\end{equation*}
Therefore the small-signal gain is:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
  G_0 = e^{\gamma_0 L}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}
In decibels:
\begin{equation*}
  G_0\big|_\text{dB} = 10\log_{10}\!\left(e^{\gamma_0 L}\right) = 10\,\gamma_0 L\,\log_{10} e \approx 4.343\,\gamma_0 L \; \text{(dB)}
\end{equation*}

This is the \textbf{small-signal gain}: it depends only on the gain coefficient $\gamma_0$ and the amplifier length $L$. Since $\gamma_0 = \sigma N_0$, a longer amplifier or a more strongly inverted medium gives a higher small-signal gain.

Therefore, while still safely in the small-signal regime, the total gain holds its pure exponential form $G_0 = e^{\gamma_0 L}$. This value is perfectly determined by merely the per-unit-length gain coefficient and the physical length constraint of the active medium.

\subsubsection{Case 2: General (saturated) gain $G$}

When $P_\text{in}$ is comparable to $P_s$, the gain coefficient is no longer constant:
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{dP}{dz} = \gamma\,P = \frac{\gamma_0}{1+P/P_s}\,P
\end{equation*}
Rearranging to separate variables — multiply both sides by $(1+P/P_s)$ and divide by $P$:
\begin{equation*}
  \left(\frac{1}{P} + \frac{1}{P_s}\right) dP = \gamma_0\,dz
\end{equation*}
Integrating the left side from $P_\text{in}$ to $P_\text{out}$ and the right side from $0$ to $L$:
\begin{equation*}
  \int_{P_\text{in}}^{P_\text{out}} \frac{dP}{P} + \int_{P_\text{in}}^{P_\text{out}} \frac{dP}{P_s} = \int_0^L \gamma_0\,dz
\end{equation*}
Evaluating each integral:
\begin{equation*}
  \left[\ln P\right]_{P_\text{in}}^{P_\text{out}} + \frac{1}{P_s}\left[P\right]_{P_\text{in}}^{P_\text{out}} = \gamma_0\,L
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
  \ln\!\left(\frac{P_\text{out}}{P_\text{in}}\right) + \frac{P_\text{out} - P_\text{in}}{P_s} = \gamma_0\,L
\end{equation*}
We now recognise that $\ln(P_\text{out}/P_\text{in}) = \ln G$ and $\gamma_0 L = \ln G_0$ (from the small-signal result). Substituting these:
\begin{equation*}
  \ln G + \frac{P_\text{in}}{P_s}\!\left(\frac{P_\text{out}}{P_\text{in}} - 1\right) = \ln G_0
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
  \ln G + \frac{P_\text{in}}{P_s}(G - 1) = \ln G_0
\end{equation*}
Isolating $\ln G - \ln G_0$:
\begin{equation*}
  \ln G - \ln G_0 = -\frac{P_\text{in}}{P_s}(G-1)
\end{equation*}
Using the logarithm rule $\ln G - \ln G_0 = \ln(G/G_0)$:
\begin{equation*}
  \ln\!\left(\frac{G}{G_0}\right) = -\frac{P_\text{in}}{P_s}(G-1)
\end{equation*}
Exponentiating:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
  G = G_0\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{P_\text{in}}{P_s}(G-1)\right)
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

This is the \textbf{saturated gain} expressed in terms of the small-signal gain $G_0 = e^{\gamma_0 L}$. Notice that when $P_\text{in} \ll P_s$, the exponential factor $\to 1$ and $G \to G_0$ — recovering our small-signal result as a special case. When $P_\text{in}$ is comparable to $P_s$, the negative exponent reduces $G$ below $G_0$: the gain compresses.

\begin{keyinsight}[Distinguishing $\gamma$ from $G$]
There is a very important distinction to keep in mind: $\gamma$ (or $\gamma_0$) is the \textbf{gain coefficient per unit length} (units m$^{-1}$), while $G$ is the \textbf{total power gain} of the amplifier (dimensionless). Some textbooks call both of them ``gain coefficient,'' which is confusing. Always check the context and the units. In our notation: $G_0 = e^{\gamma_0 L}$.
\end{keyinsight}

Tracing the full envelope, the gain of an optical amplifier effortlessly rides $G_0 = e^{\gamma_0 L}$ in the small-signal regime, but inevitably crashes back toward unity as the input power violently forces its way past the structural saturation power $P_s$.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 3/gain_compression.jpg}
    \caption{Gain Compression: The transition from the small-signal logarithmic gain $G_0$ region down to unity transmission as the input power heavily exceeds $P_s$.}
\end{figure}

% ------- BANDWIDTH --------------------------------------------
\subsection{Bandwidth \texorpdfstring{$\Delta\nu$}{Delta-nu}}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 3/gain_coefficient_profile.jpg}
    \caption{Gain Coefficient Profile: A standard Lorentzian lineshape $\gamma_0(\nu)$ with a peak value $\gamma_{\text{max}}$ and an atomic linewidth FWHM $\Delta\nu$.}
\end{figure}

As we always do when characterising an amplifier, we find the bandwidth by plotting
the gain as a function of frequency and reading off the half-power width.  The key
subtlety here is that there are \emph{two} distinct quantities whose frequency
dependence we must track: the \textbf{gain coefficient} $\gamma_0(\nu)$ and the
\textbf{power gain} $G_0(\nu) = e^{\gamma_0(\nu)L}$.  They do not have the same
spectral shape, and confusing them is a common source of error.

\subsubsection*{Shape of \texorpdfstring{$\gamma_0(\nu)$}{gamma\_0(nu)}}

Since $\gamma_0 = \sigma(\nu)\,N_0$, and the cross-section
$\sigma(\nu)$ inherits its frequency dependence from the lineshape function
$g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$.  For homogeneous broadening the lineshape is a
\textbf{Lorentzian}, so:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    \gamma_0(\nu)
    = \frac{\gamma_{\max}}{1 + \left(\dfrac{2(\nu-\nu_0)}{\Delta\nu}\right)^2}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}
where $\gamma_{\max} \equiv \gamma_0(\nu_0)$ is the peak gain coefficient at the line
centre $\nu_0$, and $\Delta\nu$ is the FWHM of the Lorentzian — this is the
\textbf{atomic linewidth}.  For inhomogeneous (Doppler) broadening the lineshape is a
\textbf{Gaussian}:
\begin{equation*}
    \gamma_0(\nu)
    = \gamma_{\max}\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{(\nu-\nu_0)^2}{2\sigma_D^2}\right),
\end{equation*}
where $\sigma_D$ is the Doppler width parameter and the FWHM is
$\Delta\nu_D = 2\sqrt{2\ln 2}\,\sigma_D \approx 2.35\,\sigma_D$.

\subsubsection*{Shape of \texorpdfstring{$G_0(\nu)$}{G\_0(nu)}: Neither Lorentzian Nor Gaussian}

Now here is the important point: the small-signal power gain is
\begin{equation*}
    G_0(\nu) = e^{\gamma_0(\nu)\,L}.
\end{equation*}
Because the exponential function is nonlinear, $G_0(\nu)$ is \emph{not} the
same shape as $\gamma_0(\nu)$.  If $\gamma_0(\nu)$ is Lorentzian,
$e^{\gamma_0(\nu)L}$ is not Lorentzian.  If $\gamma_0(\nu)$ is Gaussian,
$e^{\gamma_0(\nu)L}$ is not Gaussian either.

This has a very important practical consequence:
the \textbf{bandwidth of $G_0$} (call it $\Delta\nu_{G_0}$) is always
\emph{narrower} than the atomic linewidth $\Delta\nu$ — a phenomenon called
\textbf{bandwidth narrowing} — whenever $G_0$ is large enough.  Let us now
derive the formula for $\Delta\nu_{G_0}$ precisely.

\subsubsection*{Deriving \texorpdfstring{$\Delta\nu_{G_0}$}{Delta-nu\_G0} for a Lorentzian Gain Profile}

We start with the peak gain:
\begin{equation}
    G_{0,\max} = e^{\gamma_{\max} L}. \tag{I} 
\end{equation}
By definition, the FWHM bandwidth $\Delta\nu_{G_0}$ of $G_0(\nu)$ is the
frequency separation at which $G_0$ falls to half its peak value.  Setting
$G_0(\nu_0 \pm \Delta\nu_{G_0}/2) = G_{0,\max}/2$:
\begin{equation}
    e^{\gamma_0(\nu_0 + \Delta\nu_{G_0}/2)\,L} = \frac{G_{0,\max}}{2}. \tag{II} 
\end{equation}
Taking the natural logarithm of both sides of \textup{(II)}:
\begin{equation*}
    \gamma_0\!\left(\nu_0 + \frac{\Delta\nu_{G_0}}{2}\right)\cdot L
    = \ln\!\left(\frac{G_{0,\max}}{2}\right).
\end{equation*}
Substituting the Lorentzian gain coefficient $\gamma_0(\nu) = \gamma_{\max}/\bigl[1+(2(\nu-\nu_0)/\Delta\nu)^2\bigr]$ with $\nu - \nu_0 = \Delta\nu_{G_0}/2$ into the left-hand side:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\gamma_{\max} L}{1 + \left(\dfrac{\Delta\nu_{G_0}}{\Delta\nu}\right)^2}
    = \ln\!\left(\frac{G_{0,\max}}{2}\right).
\end{equation*}
Substituting $\gamma_{\max}L = \ln(G_{0,\max})$ from \textup{(I)} into the left-hand side:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\ln(G_{0,\max})}{1 + \left(\dfrac{\Delta\nu_{G_0}}{\Delta\nu}\right)^2}
    = \ln\!\left(\frac{G_{0,\max}}{2}\right).
\end{equation*}
Dividing both sides by $\ln(G_{0,\max}/2)$:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\ln(G_{0,\max})}{\ln(G_{0,\max}/2)}
    = 1 + \left(\frac{\Delta\nu_{G_0}}{\Delta\nu}\right)^2.
\end{equation*}
Isolating the ratio $\Delta\nu_{G_0}/\Delta\nu$:
\begin{equation*}
    \left(\frac{\Delta\nu_{G_0}}{\Delta\nu}\right)^2
    = \frac{\ln(G_{0,\max})}{\ln(G_{0,\max}/2)} - 1.
\end{equation*}
Taking the square root (taking the positive root since both bandwidths are positive):
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    \Delta\nu_{G_0}
    = \Delta\nu\,\sqrt{\frac{\ln(G_{0,\max})}{\ln(G_{0,\max}/2)} - 1}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\Delta\nu_{G_0}$: FWHM bandwidth of the amplifier power gain $G_0(\nu)$
    \item $\Delta\nu$: FWHM of the underlying atomic lineshape (bandwidth of $\gamma_0(\nu)$)
    \item $G_{0,\max} = e^{\gamma_{\max}L}$: peak small-signal gain, as defined in \textup{(I)} above
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{The Bandwidth-Narrowing Condition}

Now let us ask: is $\Delta\nu_{G_0}$ always less than $\Delta\nu$?  We need to check
when $\Delta\nu_{G_0} < \Delta\nu$, i.e. when the square root factor is less than 1:
\begin{equation*}
    \sqrt{\frac{\ln G_{0,\max}}{\ln(G_{0,\max}/2)} - 1} < 1
    \;\Longrightarrow\;
    \frac{\ln G_{0,\max}}{\ln(G_{0,\max}/2)} - 1 < 1
    \;\Longrightarrow\;
    \frac{\ln G_{0,\max}}{\ln(G_{0,\max}/2)} < 2.
\end{equation*}
Cross-multiplying by $\ln(G_{0,\max}/2) > 0$:
\begin{equation*}
    \ln G_{0,\max} < 2\ln(G_{0,\max}/2) = \ln(G_{0,\max}/2)^2 = \ln(G_{0,\max}^2/4).
\end{equation*}
Exponentiating:
\begin{equation*}
    G_{0,\max} < \frac{G_{0,\max}^2}{4}
    \;\Longrightarrow\;
    4 < G_{0,\max}.
\end{equation*}
So the bandwidth-narrowing condition is simply:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    G_{0,\max} > 4 \;\Longrightarrow\; \Delta\nu_{G_0} < \Delta\nu \quad
    \text{(bandwidth narrowing)}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

This is the practical case for any useful amplifier.  If $G_{0,\max} \leq 4$, then
$\Delta\nu_{G_0} \geq \Delta\nu$ (the gain bandwidth equals or slightly exceeds the
atomic linewidth — not a useful amplifier regime).

The physical picture makes this intuitive: at the edges of the gain profile,
$\gamma_0(\nu)$ is small, so $G_0(\nu) = e^{\gamma_0(\nu)L}$ is barely above unity.
At the centre, $G_0 = e^{\gamma_{\max}L}$ is large.  The exponential
strongly amplifies the contrast between centre and edges, making the gain
profile sharper than the gain coefficient profile.  The higher $G_{0,\max}$ is,
the more pronounced this sharpening effect becomes.

Note also that at $\nu_0 \pm \Delta\nu/2$ (the atomic FWHM points), the gain
coefficient is $\gamma_{\max}/2$, so the gain there is:
\begin{equation*}
    G_0\!\left(\nu_0 \pm \frac{\Delta\nu}{2}\right)
    = e^{(\gamma_{\max}/2)L}
    = e^{\frac{1}{2}\gamma_{\max}L}
    = \sqrt{G_{0,\max}}.
\end{equation*}
Notice that at the atomic FWHM points, the amplifier gain is $\sqrt{G_{0,\max}}$, not
$G_{0,\max}/2$.  The amplifier's half-power point occurs at a \emph{closer} frequency
to $\nu_0$ than the atomic half-power point — hence the narrower bandwidth.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 3/bandwidth_narrowing_exponential.jpg}

The compounding math reveals a hidden cost: the amplifier's exponential power gain $G_0(\nu) = e^{\gamma_0(\nu)L}$ is structurally neither Lorentzian nor Gaussian, even when the underlying $\gamma_0(\nu)$ is remarkably clean. Its FWHM will aggressively sharpen, becoming strictly narrower than the intrinsic atomic linewidth anytime $G_{0,\max} > 4$, given explicitly by the ratio $\Delta\nu_{G_0}/\Delta\nu = \sqrt{\ln(G_{0,\max})/\ln(G_{0,\max}/2)-1}$ derived above. The harder we push the peak gain, the more severely we slice the available bandwidth.

% ------- NOISE -----------------------------------------------
\subsection{Noise Performance}

The noise performance is the third fundamental parameter of an optical amplifier.  In
an optical amplifier, the dominant noise source is \textbf{amplified spontaneous
emission (ASE)}: spontaneous emission photons emitted into the signal mode get
amplified along with the signal, adding a fluctuating background that degrades the
signal quality.  We now derive the ASE power and the optical signal-to-noise ratio
from first principles.

\subsubsection*{Why Spontaneous Emission Is the Dominant Noise Source}

Spontaneous emission occurs randomly in all directions, with random polarisation, and at random frequencies within the atomic linewidth.
Only a fraction of these spontaneous photons happen to be emitted into the
\emph{same spatial mode} as the signal, and only half of those share the same
\emph{polarisation}.  Those two fractions are what get amplified by the gain medium
and added to the signal — everything else is noise in the wrong direction or wrong
polarisation, and is either lost or filtered out.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 3/ase_noise.jpg}
    \caption{ASE Noise: Visualizing spontaneous emission creating uncorrelated broadband noise photons ($\text{ASE}^{+}$ and $\text{ASE}^{-}$) travelling collinearly with the amplified signal.}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection*{Step 1: Spontaneous Emission Rate Into a Volume Element}

We start by considering a thin slab of gain medium of cross-sectional area $A$
and thickness $\delta z$.  The rate of spontaneous emission from $N_2$ atoms per
unit volume per unit time per unit frequency interval is:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{N_2}{\tau_{sp}} \cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu) \cdot \delta\nu,
\end{equation*}
where $\tau_{sp}$ is the spontaneous emission lifetime, $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ is the
normalised lineshape function, and $\delta\nu$ is the frequency bandwidth we are
tracking.  The total spontaneous emission rate (photons per second) from the volume
$A \cdot \delta z$ into bandwidth $\delta\nu$ is therefore:
\begin{equation*}
    \text{Rate} = \frac{N_2}{\tau_{sp}} \cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu) \cdot \delta\nu
    \cdot A \cdot \delta z.
\end{equation*}

\subsubsection*{Step 2: Fraction Coupled Into the Signal Mode}

These spontaneous photons are emitted isotropically into a total solid angle
of $4\pi$ steradians.  To find the fraction that are emitted into the
\emph{laser beam direction} (i.e., into the signal spatial mode), we use the
concept of the \textbf{laser solid angle}.

The solid angle element in spherical coordinates is:
\begin{equation*}
    d\Omega = \sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi,
\end{equation*}
and the total solid angle of a full sphere is:
\begin{equation*}
    \Omega_\text{total} = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi} \sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi = 4\pi.
\end{equation*}

For a beam of wavelength $\lambda$ propagating through a medium of cross-sectional
area $A$, diffraction theory tells us that the beam subtends a solid angle:
\begin{equation*}
    d\Omega_\text{laser} = \frac{\lambda^2}{A}.
\end{equation*}
This is the \textbf{laser solid angle} — the solid angle subtended by the single
spatial mode of the laser beam.

The fraction of spontaneous emission coupled into the laser mode is therefore:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d\Omega_\text{laser}}{\Omega_\text{total}} = \frac{\lambda^2/A}{4\pi}
    = \frac{\lambda^2}{4\pi A}.
\end{equation*}

\subsubsection*{Step 3: Accounting for Polarisation}

The signal beam has a definite polarisation.  Spontaneous emission is equally
likely to be in any polarisation — on average, only half the spontaneously emitted
power is in the same polarisation as the signal.  This introduces an additional
factor of $1/2$.

To see this more formally: the electric field of a spontaneously emitted photon
can be decomposed into two orthogonal polarisations, $E_x = E_0\cos\theta$ and
$E_y = E_0\sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is uniformly distributed over $[0, 2\pi)$.
The average intensity in the $x$-polarisation is:
\begin{equation*}
    \langle I_x \rangle = E_0^2 \langle \cos^2\theta \rangle
    = E_0^2 \cdot \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}\cos^2\theta\,d\theta
    = \frac{E_0^2}{2} = \frac{I}{2}.
\end{equation*}
So only half the intensity is in the signal's polarisation — this factor of $1/2$
is sometimes called the \textbf{Stokes factor}.

\subsubsection*{Step 4: Spontaneous Emission Power Coupled to Signal}

Combining steps 1–3, the spontaneous emission power coupled into the signal mode
(same direction and same polarisation) from the volume element $A \cdot \delta z$
within bandwidth $\delta\nu$ is:
\begin{equation*}
    dP_\text{sp-coupled}
    = \frac{N_2}{\tau_{sp}} \cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu) \cdot \delta\nu
    \cdot A \cdot \delta z \cdot h\nu
    \cdot \frac{\lambda^2}{4\pi A} \cdot \frac{1}{2}.
\end{equation*}
Simplifying (the area $A$ cancels):
\begin{equation*}
    dP_\text{sp-coupled}
    = \frac{N_2}{\tau_{sp}} \cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu) \cdot \delta\nu \cdot h\nu
    \cdot \frac{\lambda^2}{8\pi} \cdot \delta z.
\end{equation*}
Dividing by $\delta z$ to get the spontaneous emission power added per unit length:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{dP_\text{ASE}}{dz}
    = \frac{N_2 h\nu\,\lambda^2}{8\pi\tau_{sp}} \cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu) \cdot \delta\nu.
\end{equation*}

\subsubsection*{Step 5: Introducing the Spontaneous Emission Factor \texorpdfstring{$n_{sp}$}{n\_sp}}

The cross-section relation $\sigma(\nu) = \frac{\lambda^2}{8\pi\tau_{sp}}\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ and the gain coefficient definition $\gamma = \sigma(N_2 - N_1)$, giving $\sigma = \gamma/(N_2 - N_1)$, let us write:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{dP_\text{ASE}}{dz}
    = N_2 h\nu \cdot \sigma(\nu) \cdot \delta\nu
    = h\nu\,\delta\nu \cdot \sigma(\nu) \cdot N_2.
\end{equation*}
Physically, we expect the noise power to scale with how fully inverted the medium is: a completely inverted medium (all atoms in $E_2$, none in $E_1$) should produce the minimum possible noise per unit gain, while a weakly inverted medium wastes more of its pump energy on re-absorption. To isolate this quality factor, we multiply and divide the expression by $(N_2 - N_1)$, which lets us group the $\sigma(N_2 - N_1) = \gamma$ term and expose the inversion-quality ratio separately:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{dP_\text{ASE}}{dz}
    = h\nu\,\delta\nu \cdot \underbrace{\sigma(\nu)(N_2 - N_1)}_{\gamma}
    \cdot \frac{N_2}{N_2 - N_1}.
\end{equation*}
We define the \textbf{spontaneous emission factor} (also called the \textbf{population
inversion factor}):
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    n_{sp} = \frac{N_2}{N_2 - N_1}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

where $n_{sp}$ is always $\geq 1$ (since $N_2 \geq N_2 - N_1$ whenever $N_1 \geq 0$),
and approaches 1 only in the limit of complete inversion ($N_1 \to 0$).  It is a
direct measure of how noisy the amplifier is: $n_{sp} = 1$ is the ideal (quantum
noise limited) case, while $n_{sp} \gg 1$ indicates significant noise.

After passing through an optical bandpass filter of bandwidth $B_0$ (which selects
only the frequency band of interest around the signal), the ASE power per unit
length becomes:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dP_\text{ASE}}{dz} = \gamma\,n_{sp}\,h\nu\,B_0
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

This is the ASE noise power added to the signal beam \emph{per unit length of
amplifier}, after spatial and polarisation filtering.

\subsubsection*{Step 6: Total Power Propagation Equation}

Now we write the total power change equation.  The signal power $P$ increases due
to stimulated emission (gain) and is augmented by ASE noise:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dP}{dz} = \underbrace{\gamma\,P}_{\text{signal gain}}
    + \underbrace{\gamma\,n_{sp}\,h\nu\,B_0}_{\text{ASE noise}}. \tag{I} 
\end{equation}

To solve this, we assume the small-signal regime ($\gamma = \gamma_0 = $ constant). This is then a first-order linear ODE with constant coefficients. Rearranging:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{dP}{dz} - \gamma_0\,P = \gamma_0\,n_{sp}\,h\nu\,B_0.
\end{equation*}
The right-hand side is a constant (call it $C = \gamma_0 n_{sp} h\nu B_0$).  The
general solution to $P' - \gamma_0 P = C$ is the sum of the homogeneous solution
$P_h = P(0)\,e^{\gamma_0 z}$ and a particular solution $P_p = -C/\gamma_0 = -n_{sp}
h\nu B_0$.  Applying the boundary condition $P(0) = P_\text{in}$:
\begin{equation*}
    P(z) = \left(P_\text{in} + n_{sp}h\nu B_0\right)e^{\gamma_0 z} - n_{sp}h\nu B_0.
\end{equation*}
Evaluating at $z = L$ and using $G_0 = e^{\gamma_0 L}$:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    P(L) = G_0\,P_\text{in} + n_{sp}\,h\nu\,B_0\,(G_0 - 1)
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

This is a beautiful and important result.  The output power has two terms:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $G_0\,P_\text{in}$: the amplified signal — this is the desired output.
    \item $n_{sp}\,h\nu\,B_0\,(G_0 - 1)$: the ASE noise power added by the
    amplifier.  Notice that this term vanishes ($G_0 = 1$) when there is no gain,
    and grows with $G_0$ — more gain means more noise.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Step 7: Optical Signal-to-Noise Ratio}

The \textbf{optical signal-to-noise ratio (OSNR)} of the amplifier output is
defined as the ratio of amplified signal power to ASE noise power:
\begin{equation*}
    \text{OSNR} = \frac{G_0\,P_\text{in}}{n_{sp}\,h\nu\,B_0\,(G_0 - 1)}.
\end{equation*}
In the practically relevant limit of high gain ($G_0 \gg 1$, so $G_0 - 1 \approx
G_0$):
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    \text{OSNR} \approx \frac{P_\text{in}}{n_{sp}\,h\nu\,B_0}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

The OSNR is \emph{independent of the gain} $G_0$ in the high-gain limit — a remarkable and practically decisive result. No matter how large the gain is, the OSNR saturates at $P_\text{in}/(n_{sp}h\nu B_0)$. The only levers available to improve the OSNR are: increasing the input signal power $P_\text{in}$, achieving a more complete population inversion (reducing $n_{sp}$ toward 1 as $N_1 \to 0$), or narrowing the filter bandwidth $B_0$ (at the cost of signal bandwidth).


Analyzing the output floor, the undeniable dominant noise source in any optical amplifier is ASE. These are just rogue spontaneous emission photons that statistically happen to couple perfectly into the signal mode and steal amplification. Only the tiny fraction aligning with the signal's exact direction and true polarization manages to matter. That fraction piles up as the ASE power term $n_{sp}h\nu B_0(G_0-1)$ on the backend, hard-locking the high-gain optical SNR strictly at $P_\text{in}/(n_{sp}h\nu B_0)$, entirely immune to further gain. The spontaneous emission factor $n_{sp} = N_2/(N_2-N_1) \geq 1$ acts as the ultimate figure of merit for the amplifier's internal noise hygiene — an $N_1$ starved of atoms ($n_{sp} \to 1$) makes for the cleanest possible amplifier.

\section{Inhomogeneous Broadening}
% =============================================================

\subsection{Why the Homogeneous Treatment Fails}

In \textbf{inhomogeneous broadening}, the atoms in the medium are not identical — each atom has a slightly different resonance frequency due to local variations in the crystalline environment, Doppler shifts, or lattice strains. The overall lineshape of an inhomogeneously broadened medium is a \textbf{Gaussian} envelope, the statistical average over all the individual Lorentzian responses of the different sub-groups.

\subsection{The Averaging Problem}

One might think: why not just average the Lorentzian line shape functions and substitute the average into the gain formula? In the absence of a signal ($\Phi_\nu = 0$), that is indeed valid and gives $N = N_0$ and $\gamma_0 = \sigma N_0$. But the moment a signal is present, the gain formula for the general case is:
\begin{equation*}
  \gamma = \frac{\gamma_0}{1 + P/P_s}
\end{equation*}
Now the saturation power $P_s$ appears in the denominator. Since different atoms have different resonance frequencies, each sub-group of atoms has a \emph{different} effective $P_s$. The average of $\gamma_0/(1+P/P_s)$ is \emph{not} the same as $\langle\gamma_0\rangle / (1 + P/\langle P_s\rangle)$. Averaging a ratio is not the same as the ratio of averages:
\begin{equation*}
  \left\langle \frac{\gamma_0}{1+P/P_s} \right\rangle \neq \frac{\langle\gamma_0\rangle}{1+P/\langle P_s\rangle}
\end{equation*}

\subsection{The Correct Result for Inhomogeneous Broadening}

The correct formula, derived rigorously from averaging the Lorentzian sub-groups over the Gaussian distribution, replaces the linear denominator with a square-root denominator:
\begin{equation*}
  \gamma_\text{inhomo} = \frac{\gamma_0}{\sqrt{1 + P/P_s}}
\end{equation*}
and correspondingly for the population difference:
\begin{equation*}
  N_\text{inhomo} = \frac{N_0}{\sqrt{1 + P/P_s}}
\end{equation*}

\subsection{Comparing the Two Saturation Behaviours}

Let's see what this means physically. In the homogeneous case:
\begin{equation*}
  \gamma_\text{homo} = \frac{\gamma_0}{1 + P/P_s}
\end{equation*}
Saturation (defined as $\gamma = \gamma_0/2$) occurs when $P = P_s$.

In the inhomogeneous case:
\begin{equation*}
  \gamma_\text{inhomo} = \frac{\gamma_0}{\sqrt{1 + P/P_s}}
\end{equation*}
Saturation (again $\gamma = \gamma_0/2$) occurs when $\sqrt{1+P/P_s} = 2$, i.e.\ $1 + P/P_s = 4$, i.e.\ $P = 3P_s$.

So the inhomogeneously broadened amplifier requires approximately \textbf{three times} as much power to reach saturation. This is physically sensible: in a homogeneous medium every atom is identical, so all atoms saturate simultaneously at $P = P_s$. In an inhomogeneous medium the different sub-groups saturate at different power levels (because each sees a slightly different cross-section $\sigma$). As you increase the power, you saturate one sub-group at a time, so you have to keep pushing to saturate them all — and you end up needing much more power overall before the total gain is halved.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 3/homogeneous_vs_inhomogeneous.jpg}
    \caption{Homogeneous vs. Inhomogeneous Saturation: Graphical proof that an inhomogeneously broadened gain medium exhibits far stiffer resistance to saturation, heavily delaying the gain collapse curve.}
\end{figure}

Macroscopically, inhomogeneous broadening arms the overall gain coefficient with a rigid resistance to saturation. You have to hammer the medium with roughly three times the raw saturation power just to collapse the total gain by the same degree you would in a homogeneous equivalent.

\begin{keyinsight}[Phase Shifts in the Gain Medium]
Our analysis has tracked only how the amplifier changes the \emph{amplitude} (power) of the signal. In reality, any medium that exhibits gain at a particular frequency \emph{must} also introduce a frequency-dependent \textbf{phase shift} on the signal. This is a fundamental consequence of causality, enforced by the Kramers-Kronig relations: the real and imaginary parts of the complex susceptibility are inextricably linked, so gain and phase shift always appear as a pair. This phase shift is especially important in dense WDM systems and inside laser cavities. Its precise calculation requires the Kramers-Kronig framework, which appears in a more advanced photonics context.
\end{keyinsight}

\section{Host + Transition Decomposition}

To connect the population-inversion physics of $N_1$ and $N_2$ to the actual electromagnetic wave propagation inside a real solid-state device, we must translate from the atomic population language into the language of wave optics. The key is to recognise that a practical gain medium like an EDFA fibre is not a uniform sea of atoms — it is a two-component system: a passive, bulk \textbf{host} material (silica glass) and a dilute cloud of active \textbf{transition ions} (Erbium) embedded within it. Their electromagnetic polarisation responses are physically very different, so we must separate them.

The total macroscopic polarization vector $\bm{P}$ is therefore simply the exact sum of the passive host material's polarization and the active transition material's polarization:
\begin{equation*}
\bm{P} = \bm{P}_{\text{host}} + \bm{P}_{\text{trans}}
\end{equation*}
where $\bm{P}_{\text{host}} = \varepsilon_0 \chi_{\text{host}} \bm{E}$ and $\bm{P}_{\text{trans}} = \varepsilon_0 \chi_{\text{trans}} \bm{E}$.

Recall our displacement field definition $\bm{D}=\varepsilon_0\bm{E}+\bm{P}$. If we substitute our split sum into this formula, we can factor the terms to derive an effective permittivity for the entire doped structure:
\begin{equation*}
\bm{D} = \varepsilon_0\bm{E} + (\varepsilon_0 \chi_{\text{host}} \bm{E} + \varepsilon_0 \chi_{\text{trans}} \bm{E})
\end{equation*}
We first distribute the additions to drop the parentheses:
\begin{equation*}
\bm{D} = \varepsilon_0\bm{E} + \varepsilon_0 \chi_{\text{host}} \bm{E} + \varepsilon_0 \chi_{\text{trans}} \bm{E}
\end{equation*}
Next, we factor out $\varepsilon_0 \bm{E}$ strictly from the first two terms:
\begin{equation*}
\bm{D} = \varepsilon_0 \bm{E}(1 + \chi_{\text{host}}) + \varepsilon_0 \chi_{\text{trans}} \bm{E}
\end{equation*}
We recognize from fundamental dielectrics that the relative permittivity is defined as $\varepsilon_r = 1 + \chi$. Substitute $\varepsilon_{r,\text{host}} = 1 + \chi_{\text{host}}$:
\begin{equation*}
\bm{D} = \varepsilon_0 \varepsilon_{r,\text{host}} \bm{E} + \varepsilon_0 \chi_{\text{trans}} \bm{E}
\end{equation*}
Finally, to define a singular effective permittivity for the entire doped structure, we factor out $\varepsilon_0 \varepsilon_{r,\text{host}}$ entirely from both terms:
\begin{equation*}
\bm{D} = \underbrace{\varepsilon_0\varepsilon_{r,\text{host}} \left(1+\frac{\chi_{\text{trans}}}{\varepsilon_{r,\text{host}}}\right)}_{\text{Effective Permittivity } \varepsilon_{r,\text{eff}}} \bm{E}
\end{equation*}

This is a beautiful result. It shows exactly how the active trace ions act as a fundamental perturbation (a small fractional addition) directly on top of the bulk properties of the host fiber! 

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 3/host_plus_transition_decomposition.jpg}
    \caption{Host + Transition Decomposition: Understanding a macroscopic Erbium optical fiber as a pristine silica host glass with a perturbative cloud of transition ions injecting local gain into the overall permittivity.}    
\end{figure}

Because the susceptibility of the transition dopant is almost always very weak compared to the massive host dielectric ($\chi_{\text{trans}} \ll \varepsilon_{r,\text{host}}$), we can formulate a highly accurate approximation for the new complex propagation constant $k = k_0 \tilde{n}_{\text{eff}}$. 

First, we define $\tilde{n}_{\text{eff}} = \sqrt{\varepsilon_{r,\text{eff}}}$. Substituting our new effective permittivity:
\begin{equation*}
\tilde{n}_{\text{eff}} = \sqrt{\varepsilon_{r,\text{host}} \left(1+\frac{\chi_{\text{trans}}}{\varepsilon_{r,\text{host}}}\right)} = \sqrt{\varepsilon_{r,\text{host}}} \sqrt{1+\frac{\chi_{\text{trans}}}{\varepsilon_{r,\text{host}}}}
\end{equation*}
Substitute the host refractive index $n_{\text{host}} = \sqrt{\varepsilon_{r,\text{host}}}$, which simultaneously means $\varepsilon_{r,\text{host}} = n_{\text{host}}^2$:
\begin{equation*}
\tilde{n}_{\text{eff}} = n_{\text{host}} \sqrt{1+\frac{\chi_{\text{trans}}}{n_{\text{host}}^2}}
\end{equation*}
We again apply the binomial approximation $(1+x)^{1/2} \approx 1 + x/2$. The prerequisite condition $x \ll 1$ is satisfied here because the susceptibility of the transition dopant is almost always very weak compared to the massive host dielectric ($\chi_{\text{trans}} \ll n_{\text{host}}^2$). Substitute $x = \frac{\chi_{\text{trans}}}{n_{\text{host}}^2}$:
\begin{equation*}
\tilde{n}_{\text{eff}} \approx n_{\text{host}} \left( 1 + \frac{\chi_{\text{trans}}}{2n_{\text{host}}^2} \right)
\end{equation*}
Distribute the $n_{\text{host}}$ multiplier:
\begin{equation*}
\tilde{n}_{\text{eff}} \approx n_{\text{host}} + \frac{n_{\text{host}}\chi_{\text{trans}}}{2n_{\text{host}}^2} = n_{\text{host}} + \frac{\chi_{\text{trans}}}{2n_{\text{host}}}
\end{equation*}

Finally, we multiply this entire effective index $\tilde{n}_{\text{eff}}$ strictly by the free-space wavenumber ($k_0 = 2\pi/\lambda_0$) to yield the total propagation constant $k$:
\begin{equation*}
k = k_0 \tilde{n}_{\text{eff}} \approx k_0 \left( n_{\text{host}} + \frac{\chi_{\text{trans}}}{2n_{\text{host}}} \right)
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
k \approx k_0 n_{\text{host}} + \frac{k_0 \chi_{\text{trans}}}{2n_{\text{host}}}
\end{equation*}

By separating the transition parameter $\chi_{\text{trans}}$ into its real and imaginary parts $\chi' - j\chi''$ (using the sign convention that naturally aligns with our approximations), we trace exactly how the doped ions physically alter the propagation wave:
\begin{align*}
\text{Phase Shift Increment (Dispersion velocity shift)} &\propto \Re\{\chi_{\text{trans}}\} \\
\text{Power Attenuation/Gain Increment ($\alpha/2$)} &\propto \Im\{\chi_{\text{trans}}\}
\end{align*}

From an engineering perspective, whenever we inject dopants into a host glass, we can cleanly split our math. The giant host structure simply locks down the massive constant base refractive index. Meanwhile, the tiny complex perturbative fraction $\chi_{\text{trans}}$ holds full control over the exact localized dispersion shifts and the all-important final power gain profile. We will now deploy this directly onto the most lucrative physics example in the world.


\section{EDFA Example (Erbium-Doped Fiber Amplifier)}
\subsection{Practical Application: The EDFA}
The Erbium-Doped Fiber Amplifier (EDFA) is the perfect real-world engineering application of everything we just derived regarding light-matter interaction, decomposition, and population inversion. The EDFA is a specific Traveling-Wave Amplifier (TWA) device that amplifies light directly inside optical fibers \emph{without} ever needing to convert the light stream into electricity first! 

As the name implies, "Erbium Doped" simply means the native silica glass fiber core (the Host) has been infused with Erbium ions ($\mathrm{Er}^{3+}$) during manufacturing.

\subsection{Working Principle (A Step-By-Step Sequence)}
To master how the EDFA works, we must walk through its sequence chronologically:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Signal Light Enters:} A weak optical signal carrying internet information enters the erbium-doped fiber section. The wavelength is typically near $\lambda = \SI{1550}{nm}$ because this is the global minimum loss window for silica host glass.
  \item \textbf{Pump Laser Creates Inversion:} A separate, higher-energy laser (usually permanently shining at $\lambda = \SI{980}{nm}$) pumps energy rapidly down the fiber alongside the signal. This $\SI{980}{nm}$ light violently excites the sluggish $\mathrm{Er}^{3+}$ ions, ripping them from their ground state $E_1$ up into a metastable (slow-to-relax, meaning long $\tau_t$) upper state $E_2$. This pump action sustains the highly non-natural \textbf{Population Inversion} ($N_2 > N_1$) we mathematically proved was required for gain!
  \item \textbf{Stimulated Emission Triggers:} When the weak $\SI{1550}{nm}$ signal wave smoothly passes through this excited cloud of erbium ions, the electric field of the signal interacts with the inversion. It forces the ions to quickly release their stored top-level energy. The ions instantly drop down to $E_1$ and release photons that are exact engineered "clones" of the incident signal. 
  \item \textbf{Output Signal Exits:} Because the cloned photons perfectly match the original wave's frequency and phase, they constructively add to the signal envelope. The light exits the fiber massively stronger ($10 - 30$ dB gain), yet containing the exact same pristine digital data packet ($P_{\text{out}} \gg P_{\text{in}}$).
\end{enumerate}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 3/edfa_architecture.jpg}
    \caption{The Erbium-Doped Fiber Amplifier (EDFA): A masterclass application synthesizing all principles—population inversion via 980 nm pumping, metastable persistence, and massive 1550 nm single-pass signal amplification driven entirely by stimulated emission within a solid state fiber core.}
\end{figure}

So, at its core, the EDFA wields a secondary 980 nm pump laser explicitly to force and lock a massive population inversion across the embedded Erbium ions. This brilliantly sets the kinetic stage for a weak 1550 nm passing signal to linearly drain that stored atomic energy precisely via stimulated emission, allowing it to exit the fiber continuously and heavily amplified.

\section{Summary and Looking Ahead}

% =============================================================



We have now built a complete picture of optical amplification:



\begin{enumerate}

  \item We derived the small-signal propagation equation $d\Phi_\nu/dz = \gamma_0\,\Phi_\nu$ by balancing photon creation (stimulated emission) and destruction (absorption) in a differential element of the medium.

  \item We showed that gain ($\gamma_0 > 0$) requires $N_2 > N_1$ --- \textbf{population inversion} --- which is achieved by a pump before the signal enters.

  \item We wrote the complete \textbf{rate equations} for $N_2$ and $N_1$ including pump terms $R_1$ and $R_2$, and solved them at steady state.

  \item We found that population inversion is achievable if and only if $\tau_1 < \tau_{21}$: the lower-level lifetime must be shorter than the upper-level spontaneous emission lifetime.

  \item We worked through the \textbf{three-level laser system} explicitly, deriving $N_0$, $\Phi_s$, and the threshold pump rate for constant-rate and optical pumping models --- the direct foundation for the EDFA.

  \item Once a signal is present, the gain saturates: $\gamma = \gamma_0/(1+P/P_s)$ in the general (homogeneous) case.

  \item The amplifier's \textbf{gain} is $G_0 = e^{\gamma_0 L}$ in the small-signal limit and decreases with input power in the general case.

  \item The amplifier's \textbf{bandwidth} is narrower than the atomic linewidth $\Delta\nu$ whenever $G_{0,\max} > 4$ (bandwidth narrowing), with the precise FWHM given by $\Delta\nu_{G_0} = \Delta\nu\sqrt{\ln(G_{0,\max})/\ln(G_{0,\max}/2)-1}$.

  \item The amplifier's \textbf{noise} is dominated by ASE, yielding the output power $P(L) = G_0 P_\text{in} + n_{sp}h\nu B_0(G_0-1)$ and a high-gain OSNR of $P_\text{in}/(n_{sp}h\nu B_0)$, independent of gain.

  \item For inhomogeneously broadened media, the gain saturates as $\gamma_0/\sqrt{1+P/P_s}$, requiring $\sim 3P_s$ to halve the gain rather than $P_s$.

  \item We decomposed the polarisation of a doped host medium into \textbf{host} and \textbf{transition} contributions, showing that the tiny complex $\chi_\text{trans}$ acts as a perturbation on the host refractive index, governing both the phase shift and the gain.

  \item We applied all of the above in the \textbf{EDFA} --- the Erbium-Doped Fiber Amplifier --- the most important practical realisation of single-pass optical amplification in modern telecommunications.

\end{enumerate}



Now that we fully understand the concept of optical amplification and the physics of the gain medium, we are ready to place this gain medium inside a resonant cavity and study how feedback turns a travelling-wave amplifier into a \textbf{laser oscillator}. The next chapter will derive the conditions for self-sustained oscillation, the allowed mode frequencies, the threshold pump rate, and the output power of the laser.
