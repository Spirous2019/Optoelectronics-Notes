\section{Scope of This Chapter}
To master optical devices, we must first understand how light interacts with materials at the fundamental level. The very first question to ask is: \emph{when does a photon actually interact with matter?} The answer depends on the photon's energy relative to the material's energy gap:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{If $E_{\text{photon}} < E_{\text{gap}}$:} The photon does not have enough energy to excite any transition. The material is \textbf{transparent} to this wavelength—no interaction occurs, and the light simply passes through.
  \item \textbf{If $E_{\text{photon}} > E_{\text{gap}}$:} The photon carries sufficient energy to promote electrons into the conduction band, changing the carrier distribution. A real interaction takes place—absorption, emission, or scattering.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/transparency_vs_absorption.jpg}
    \caption{Transparency vs. Absorption: Interaction depends on the photon energy relative to the band gap.}
    \label{fig:ch1_transparency_absorption}
\end{figure}

This chapter develops the first and most foundational of these models — the classical electron oscillator. As we'll see, relying on a single model isn't enough; we need three progressive levels of description:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Classical Electron Oscillator (Lorentz model):} This provides our baseline physical intuition. It treats energy levels as continuous.
  \item \textbf{Einstein transition model:} This introduces discrete energy levels and transition probabilities to explain what the classical model cannot.
   \item \textbf{Quantum-mechanical interpretation:} This gives us the full mathematical rigor needed for advanced photonics.
\end{enumerate}

Note that the interaction picture differs between \textbf{gases} (where we deal with bound electrons in isolated atoms) and \textbf{semiconductors} (where we deal with free carriers in energy bands), but the underlying Lorentz model captures the essential physics of both.

\begin{motivation}[Journey Through Light-Matter Interaction]
Understanding light-matter interaction requires a progressive approach. We will start with the \textbf{Classical Electron Oscillator (CEO)} to build an intuitive picture of dispersion and absorption. However, as we will prove shortly, the classical model cannot explain true optical gain. This necessitates the introduction of the \textbf{Einstein Model} for population inversion, and eventually, a full \textbf{Quantum Mechanical} treatment to capture the probabilistic nature of electrons.
\end{motivation}

\section{Classical Electron Oscillator (CEO): Lossless Start}\label{sec:classical_electron_oscillator}
\subsection{Bound Electron as a Restoring-Force System}
Now we'll model our atom. Let's first build our physical intuition. Treat the atom as a heavy, fixed positive core (nucleus) and a much lighter bound electron orbiting it. 

If the electron is slightly displaced from its equilibrium position by an external force (like an incoming electromagnetic wave), the Coulomb force pulls it back. By linearizing this Coulomb force around the equilibrium point, it behaves exactly like a mechanical spring system obeying Hooke's Law! This gives us our simple restoring force:
\begin{equation*}
F_{\text{rest}} \approx -k_s x
\end{equation*}
where $k_s$ is the effective "spring constant" of the atom, $x$ is the displacement distance from equilibrium, and the negative sign indicates the force strictly opposes the displacement.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/mechanical_atom_model.jpg}
    \caption{The Mechanical Atom Schematic: A bound electron modeled as a mass-spring system with a restoring force.}
    \label{fig:ch1_mechanical_atom}
\end{figure}

Now, applying Newton's second law ($F = m a$, where acceleration $a = \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}$) to this motion, we substitute our force to get our natural, unforced impulse response equation:
\begin{equation*}
m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = -k_s x
\end{equation*}
Rearranging terms by bringing everything to one side, we write:
\begin{equation*}
m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + k_s x = 0
\end{equation*}
To isolate the highest derivative, we must divide the entire equation strictly by the mass $m$:
\begin{equation*}
\frac{m}{m}\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + \frac{k_s}{m} x = \frac{0}{m}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + \left(\frac{k_s}{m}\right) x = 0
\end{equation*}
We now substitute a new constant $k_s/m$ with $\omega_0^2$. Substitute $\omega_0^2 = \frac{k_s}{m}$:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + \omega_0^2 x = 0
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}
where $\omega_0 = \sqrt{\frac{k_s}{m}}$ is explicitly defined as the \textbf{natural frequency} (or resonance frequency) of the bound electron.

\textbf{Interpretation:} If perturbed, the electron will simply oscillate back and forth forever at this specific frequency $\omega_0$. Because this is a standard harmonic oscillator equation of the form $x'' + \omega_0^2 x = 0$, the mathematical solution is purely sinusoidal. Let's write the solution explicitly:
\begin{equation*}
x(t)=A\cos(\omega_0 t+\phi)
\end{equation*}
where $A$ is the amplitude of the displacement, and $\phi$ is the phase.

In short, the basic structure of an atom naturally acts like a spring mass system, producing a pure, endless oscillation when disturbed at its resonance frequency $\omega_0$.

\begin{keyinsight}[On Thermal Energy]
One might ask: doesn't thermal energy already displace the electron and cause oscillations? Recall that at temperature $T$, each degree of freedom carries an average thermal energy of $\frac{1}{2}k_B T$. This thermal energy gives the electron a random thermal velocity, but it does \textbf{not} produce coherent oscillations. The random thermal motion averages to zero displacement over time. Only an externally applied coherent field (such as an electromagnetic wave) can drive a sustained, phase-locked oscillation of the electron cloud.
\end{keyinsight}

\subsection{Why This Is Incomplete}
While simple, this lossless model is critically flawed and not physically realistic. It predicts an infinitely sharp resonance (meaning the atom interacts with light \emph{only} at exactly $\omega_0$) and assumes the electron will oscillate forever without losing any energy. In reality, real atoms lose energy through radiation (the antenna effect) and through collisions (lattice vibrations). We must now upgrade our model to incorporate these losses.

\section{Damping, Lifetime, and Broadening}\label{sec:damping_and_lifetime}
\subsection{The Damped Oscillator Equation}
To account for these energy losses, we must introduce an equivalent frictional force into our equation. Recall from basic electromagnetics that any electron undergoing acceleration will radiate electromagnetic waves, losing energy. Additionally, electrons scatter off other atoms in the lattice. 

So, we model this by adding a damping force that is strictly proportional to the electron's velocity. Let $b$ be the friction factor:
\begin{equation*}
m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + \underbrace{b\frac{dx}{dt}}_{\text{damping}} + k_s x = 0
\end{equation*}

Once again, to solve this, we must isolate the highest order derivative by dividing the entire equation by the electron mass $m$:
\begin{equation*}
\frac{m}{m}\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}+\frac{b}{m}\frac{dx}{dt}+\frac{k_s}{m}x= \frac{0}{m}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}+\left(\frac{b}{m}\right)\frac{dx}{dt}+\left(\frac{k_s}{m}\right)x=0
\end{equation*}
We now define two fundamental substitutions. Substitute $\eta = \frac{b}{m}$ (defined as the damping coefficient) and substitute $\omega_0^2 = \frac{k_s}{m}$ (our natural frequency squared). This yields our new governing equation:
\begin{equation*}
\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}+\eta\frac{dx}{dt}+\omega_0^2x=0
\end{equation*}

To solve this linear homogeneous differential equation with constant coefficients, we assume a solution of the form $x(t) = Ae^{st}$, leading directly to the characteristic equation:
\begin{equation*}
s^2 + \eta s + \omega_0^2 = 0
\end{equation*}
We apply the quadratic formula $s = \frac{-B \pm \sqrt{B^2 - 4AC}}{2A}$. Substitute $A=1$, $B=\eta$, and $C=\omega_0^2$:
\begin{equation*}
s = \frac{-\eta \pm \sqrt{\eta^2 - 4(1)(\omega_0^2)}}{2(1)}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
s = -\frac{\eta}{2} \pm \frac{\sqrt{\eta^2 - 4\omega_0^2}}{2}
\end{equation*}
To bring the $\frac{1}{2}$ inside the square root, we square it as $1/4$:
\begin{equation*}
s = -\frac{\eta}{2} \pm \sqrt{\frac{\eta^2}{4} - \frac{4\omega_0^2}{4}} = -\frac{\eta}{2} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{\eta}{2}\right)^2 - \omega_0^2}
\end{equation*}

The behavior of the system depends entirely on the value inside this square root. We must define the cases:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Case 1: Overdamped ($\eta/2 > \omega_0$).} The term $\left(\frac{\eta}{2}\right)^2 - \omega_0^2$ is strictly positive. The square root evaluates to a real number. The roots $s$ are purely real and negative. The electron just slowly decays exponentially back to equilibrium without ever crossing the center.
    \item \textbf{Case 2: Critically Damped ($\eta/2 = \omega_0$).} The term inside the square root is exactly zero. The roots are equal ($s=-\eta/2$). The electron returns to equilibrium as fast as possible without oscillating.
    \item \textbf{Case 3: Underdamped ($\eta/2 < \omega_0$).} The term $\left(\frac{\eta}{2}\right)^2 - \omega_0^2$ is negative. The damping is relatively weak compared to the restoring force. When we take the square root of a negative number, it forces $s$ to be complex. A complex exponent transforms mathematically into a real exponentially decaying sinusoid.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/three_damping_regimes.jpg}
    \caption{The Three Damping Regimes: Comparison of the displacement decay for overdamped, critically damped, and underdamped oscillators.}
    \label{fig:ch1_three_damping_regimes}
\end{figure}

Since we are dealing with optical phenomena where damping is relatively small, we always assume the \textbf{Underdamped} case. Thus, our solution becomes an exponentially decaying oscillation:
\begin{equation*}
x(t)=A e^{-\eta t/2}\cos(\omega_d t+\phi)
\end{equation*}
where $\omega_d$ is the damped natural frequency, defined as:
\begin{equation*}
\omega_d=\sqrt{\omega_0^2-\left(\frac{\eta}{2}\right)^2}
\end{equation*}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/underdamped_oscillation.jpg}
    \caption{Underdamped Oscillation: The electron's displacement oscillates inside an exponentially decaying envelope.}
    \label{fig:ch1_underdamped_oscillation}
\end{figure}

\begin{keyinsight}[Justifying the Underdamped Assumption]
Is the underdamped assumption ($\eta/2 < \omega_0$) valid for optical materials? Yes. The displacement solution $x(t) = A e^{-\eta t/2}\cos(\omega_d t+\phi)$ explicitly shows that the coefficient $\eta$ acts purely as a decay rate for the amplitude envelope, while $\omega_0$ (via the damped frequency $\omega_d=\sqrt{\omega_0^2-(\eta/2)^2}$) sets the rapid oscillation. For visible light, the natural oscillation frequency of an electron is immense: $\omega_0 \approx 10^{15}$ rad/s. In contrast, the atomic ``friction'' causing the oscillation to decay acts much more slowly. The amplitude typically takes a few nanoseconds to visibly shrink, making the decay rate roughly $\eta \approx 10^9$ s$^{-1}$. Because $10^9 \ll 10^{15}$ (meaning $\eta \ll \omega_0$), the electron will ring for millions of cycles before the envelope dies out. Therefore, atomic electron oscillators are strictly underdamped!
\end{keyinsight}

\subsection{Energy Decay and the Concept of "Lifetime"}
Now let's talk about the energy of this system. The total energy $U$ of our Classical Electron Oscillator (CEO) is the sum of its potential and kinetic energy. 

First, let's derive the potential energy stored in our ideal spring model. The restoring force of the spring is $F_s = -k_s x$. The potential energy (P.E) is the work done against this force to stretch the spring from equilibrium ($x=0$) to a displacement $x$:
\begin{equation*}
\text{P.E} = -\int_0^x F_s \, dx' = \int_0^x k_s x' \, dx' = \frac{1}{2} k_s x^2
\end{equation*}
The total energy $U$ maxes out completely as potential energy exactly when the kinetic energy is zero (meaning its velocity is zero at the peak of its swing, where $x = \text{Amplitude } A$):
\begin{equation*}
U = \text{P.E} + \text{K.E} \approx \max (\text{P.E}) \Big|_{K.E = 0} = \frac{1}{2} k_s A^2
\end{equation*}
Recall from our displacement solution that the amplitude decays as $x_{\text{peak}} \propto e^{-\eta t/2}$. If we plug this amplitude constraint into our energy equation:
\begin{equation*}
U(t) \propto \left(e^{-\eta t/2}\right)^2 = e^{-\eta t}
\end{equation*}
Thus, the total energy $U$ decays exponentially at a rate of $\eta$:
\begin{equation*}
U(t)=U(0)e^{-\eta t}
\end{equation*}

We now define a critical metric called the \textbf{Total Lifetime ($\tau_t$)}—sometimes denoted as $\eta^{-1}$. This term represents the specific amount of time it takes for the system's energy to decay to exactly $1/e$ ($\approx 37\%$) of its initial starting value $U(0)$:
\begin{equation*}
\text{If } U(\tau_t) = U(0)e^{-1}, \text{ then explicitly } \tau_t = \frac{1}{\eta}.
\end{equation*}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/energy_decay_profile.jpg}
    \caption{Energy Decay Profile: Total energy decays exponentially, with the lifetime $\tau_t$ marking the $1/e$ point.}
    \label{fig:ch1_energy_decay}
\end{figure}

In general, the atom has multiple ways to "relax" and lose this energy. We distinguish two primary relaxation channels. Because these events occur independently, their rates add together. The fastest relaxation method always dominates the total lifetime:
\begin{equation*}
\frac{1}{\tau_t}=\frac{1}{\tau_r}+\frac{1}{\tau_{nr}}
\end{equation*}
We fully explore both channels below:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Radiative Lifetime ($\tau_r$):} The atom relaxes by shining! This happens because an accelerated charge radiates an electromagnetic field (the ``antenna effect''). A classic example is spontaneous emission. This property is governed by the relation:
    \begin{equation*}
    \tau_r = \frac{6 \pi \epsilon m c^3}{e^2 \omega_0^2}, \quad \text{where } \epsilon = n^2 \varepsilon_0, \ c = \frac{c_0}{n}
    \end{equation*}
    \item \textbf{Non-Radiative Lifetime ($\tau_{nr}$):} The atom relaxes through "collisions" without ever emitting light. Examples include hitting the surrounding atomic lattice (creating lattice vibrations/phonons) or Auger recombination where the energy is kicked directly to another nearby electron.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/relaxation_paths.jpg}
    \caption{Relaxation Paths: An excited atom can decay purely via radiative mechanisms (photon emission) or non-radiative multi-phonon mechanisms (lattice heating).}
    \label{fig:ch1_relaxation_paths}
\end{figure}

In short, the friction parameter $\eta$ governs the total lifetime $\tau_t$ of the atom's excited state, combining both radiative light-emitting effects and non-radiative collisional effects.

\subsection{Broadening Insight: Why Damping Creates Bandwidth}\label{subsec:broadening_insight}
To master light-matter interaction, we must understand how damping changes the frequency response. Without damping, the interaction between light and matter happens strictly at an infinitely sharp impulse at $\omega_0$. 

However, because of the damping mechanisms (both radiative and collisional) causing energy loss, the spectral response broadens. This phenomenon is called \textbf{Broadening}. 

\textbf{What exactly is Broadening?:} Stronger damping ($\eta \uparrow$) means the atom loses energy faster, meaning a heavily decreased lifetime $\tau_t$. In the frequency domain, this shorter lifetime translates into a \emph{wider} resonance peak. 
If the peak is wider, it means the atom can interact with a wider "Bandwidth" of frequencies surrounding $\omega_0$, though its peak response will inevitably be lower.

Now that we fully understand the internal mechanics of the single atom, we must apply an external field to see how it forces the atom to behave.

\section{Forced Response to an Optical Field}
\subsection{Lorentz Force Simplification}
Now we'll see what happens when we expose our damped electron to light. Recall that light is an electromagnetic wave, meaning it carries both an electric field $\bm{E}$ and a magnetic field $\bm{B}$. 

If we shine this wave onto our electron, it exerts a force known as the Lorentz force. The total Lorentz force $\bm{F}$ exerted by an electromagnetic wave on a charge $q$ moving with velocity $\bm{v}$ is:
\begin{equation*}
\bm{F}=\bm{F}_e+\bm{F}_m=q\bm{E}+q(\bm{v}\times\bm{B})
\end{equation*}
where $q$ is the charge ($-e$ for an electron), $\bm{E}$ is the electric field vector, $\bm{v}$ is the electron velocity vector, and $\bm{B}$ is the magnetic field vector.

\begin{keyinsight}[Why the Magnetic Force is Negligible]
For a plane wave in a medium, the ratio of the magnetic field magnitude to the electric field magnitude is strictly determined by the phase velocity $v_p$ of the light:
\begin{equation*}
\frac{|\bm{B}|}{|\bm{E}|} = \frac{1}{v_p}
\end{equation*}
where the phase velocity $v_p$ is defined by the free-space speed of light $c_0 \approx 3 \times 10^8$ m/s as $v_p = \frac{c_0}{n}$. We rearrange this to isolate $|\bm{B}|$:
\begin{equation*}
|\bm{B}| = \frac{|\bm{E}|}{v_p}
\end{equation*}

Now let's compare the magnitude of the magnetic force part $|\bm{F}_m| = |q(\bm{v}\times\bm{B})| \approx q v |\bm{B}|$ (assuming perpendicular velocity to maximize the possible cross product force) to the basic electric force part $|\bm{F}_e| = q |\bm{E}|$. We structure their ratio explicitly:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{|\bm{F}_m|}{|\bm{F}_e|} = \frac{q v |\bm{B}|}{q |\bm{E}|}
\end{equation*}
The charges $q$ cancel completely:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{|\bm{F}_m|}{|\bm{F}_e|} = \frac{v |\bm{B}|}{|\bm{E}|}
\end{equation*}
Substitute $|\bm{B}| = \frac{|\bm{E}|}{v_p}$ into the numerator:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{|\bm{F}_m|}{|\bm{F}_e|} = \frac{v \left(\frac{|\bm{E}|}{v_p}\right)}{|\bm{E}|}
\end{equation*}
The electric field magnitudes $|\bm{E}|$ perfectly cancel out:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{|\bm{F}_m|}{|\bm{F}_e|} = \frac{v}{v_p}
\end{equation*}
Substitute $v_p = \frac{c_0}{n}$:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{|\bm{F}_m|}{|\bm{F}_e|} = \frac{v}{(c_0/n)} = \frac{n v}{c_0}
\end{equation*}
For standard optical index materials ($n \approx 1$ to $4$), since bound electron velocities are highly non-relativistic (which rigorously defines the condition $v \ll c_0$), the fraction $v/c_0$ evaluates extremely close to zero. Therefore, if $v \ll c_0$, then the ratio $|\bm{F}_m|/|\bm{F}_e| \to 0$, meaning the magnetic force is completely negligible compared to the electric force. 
\end{keyinsight}

Because we established the strict condition $v \ll c_0$, we can safely drop the entire $\bm{F}_m$ vector from the total Lorentz force and assume a purely \textbf{one-dimensional} external electric driving force:
\begin{equation*}
\bm{F}\approx \bm{F}_e = q\bm{E} = -e\bm{E}
\end{equation*}

In short, for all standard linear optics where electrons aren't moving anywhere near the speed of light, we only need to worry about the electric push of the light wave, ignoring its magnetic tug entirely.

\subsection{The Driven Equation: Adding an External Wave}\label{subsec:driven_equation}
So far, we looked at how the electron naturally behaves under damping without external influence. Now, let's force the system. We will substitute our external force $F \approx -e E(t)$ into the right-hand side of our damped differential equation (replacing the zero):
\begin{equation*}
m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}+b\frac{dx}{dt}+k_s x = -e E(t)
\end{equation*}
Dividing entirely by $m$ and using our substitutions $\eta = b/m$ and $\omega_0^2 = k_s/m$, we construct the fundamental equation of linear optics:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}+\eta\frac{dx}{dt}+\omega_0^2x=-\frac{e}{m}E(t)
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

Because our optical field strongly oscillates, we must solve this in the \textbf{Phasor Domain}. 

If we assume the incoming light wave oscillates at a steady driving frequency $\omega$ in the form $E(t)=\Re\{E_0 e^{j\omega t}\}$, then because the system is perfectly linear, the steady-state displacement $x(t)$ must also oscillate at that exact same frequency $\omega$, taking the form $x(t)=\Re\{x_0 e^{j\omega t}\}$. 

\begin{keyinsight}[The Two Frequencies: $\omega_0$ vs. $\omega$]
It is crucial to never confuse the two distinct frequencies now present in our equations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Natural Frequency ($\omega_0$):} This is the \emph{internal} property of the atom itself, determined by the ``spring stiffness'' $k_s$ binding the electron. It is the frequency the electron inherently ``wants'' to vibrate at.
    \item \textbf{Driving Frequency ($\omega$):} This is the \emph{external} property of the incoming electromagnetic light wave. It is the frequency at which the light physically \emph{forces} the electron to move.
\end{itemize}
The difference between them, called the \textbf{Detuning} ($\Delta\omega = \omega - \omega_0$), dictates the entire interaction. When the external push matches the natural rhythm ($\omega \approx \omega_0$), we hit resonance, causing massive energy transfer.
\end{keyinsight}

Substitute $x(t) = x_0 e^{j\omega t}$ into our differential equation. This allows us to convert time derivatives algebraically: $\frac{d}{dt} \to j\omega$ and $\frac{d^2}{dt^2} \to (j\omega)^2 = -\omega^2$.
\begin{equation*}
-\omega^2 x_0 e^{j\omega t} + \eta (j\omega) x_0 e^{j\omega t} + \omega_0^2 x_0 e^{j\omega t} = -\frac{e}{m} E_0 e^{j\omega t}
\end{equation*}
The exponential terms $e^{j\omega t}$ explicitly cancel from every single term on both sides. Next, we group the terms on the left side to factor out $x_0$:
\begin{equation*}
x_0(-\omega^2) + x_0(j\eta\omega) + x_0(\omega_0^2) = -\frac{e}{m} E_0
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
x_0 \left(-\omega^2+j\eta\omega + \omega_0^2 \right) = -\frac{e}{m} E_0
\end{equation*}
This perfectly simplifies to our final solution for the phasor displacement $x_0$:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
x_0=-\frac{e}{m}\frac{E_0}{\omega_0^2-\omega^2+j\eta\omega}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

What does this equation tell us? It proves that the electron's movement $x_0$ depends enormously on the denominator. 
We must explicitly consider the resonance cases:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Case 1: Low-frequency drive ($\omega \ll \omega_0$).} The $\omega_0^2$ term dominates. The displacement $x_0$ is completely real and simply tracks the applied field in-phase. The atom acts purely like a perfect rigid spring.
    \item \textbf{Case 2: High-frequency drive ($\omega \gg \omega_0$).} The $-\omega^2$ term dominates. The displacement is real but negative (out of phase by 180 degrees). The electron cannot move fast enough to keep up with the light, limited purely by its mass inertia.
    \item \textbf{Case 3: On Resonance ($\omega = \omega_0$).} The $\omega_0^2$ and $-\omega^2$ terms cancel each other perfectly! The denominator becomes purely imaginary ($j\eta\omega$). The displacement $x_0$ is purely imaginary (out of phase by exactly 90 degrees), and its amplitude is limited entirely by the friction $\eta$. This is where maximum energy is absorbed.
\end{enumerate}

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/resonance_amplitude_phase.jpg}
    \caption{Resonance Effects: Amplitude and phase shift of the electron's displacement as a function of the external driving frequency.}
    \label{fig:ch1_resonance_effects}
\end{figure}

\begin{keyinsight}[Physical Meaning of Phase and Energy Absorption]
Think of pushing a child on a swing. To pump maximum energy in, you must push forward exactly when the swing is moving forward — your Force must be in sync with the swing's \emph{Velocity}. Push at the wrong time and you fight the swing's own momentum, canceling your work. This same logic governs how a light wave pumps energy into an atom.

The relevant rule is Power $= \bm{F} \cdot \bm{v}$. The light wave exerts a force $F = -eE(t)$ on the electron. Whether it can permanently do net work on the electron depends on whether the electron's \emph{velocity} is in phase with this force. The key is that we derived the phasor displacement amplitude:
\begin{equation*}
    x_0 = -\frac{e}{m}\frac{E_0}{\underbrace{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2 + j\eta\omega}_{\text{determines the phase of }x_0}}
\end{equation*}
Since velocity is the derivative of displacement ($v = \frac{dx}{dt}$), differentiating in the time domain turns a $\cos(\omega t)$ wave into a $-\sin(\omega t)$ wave. In phasor notation, this maps to $v_0 = j\omega\, x_0$ — meaning differentiation always rotates the phase by exactly $+90^\circ$. Let's assume the light's force oscillates as a simple cosine wave, $F(t) \propto \cos(\omega t)$, and track where the math leads:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Case 1 — Low Frequency ($\omega \ll \omega_0$):} The denominator $(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2 + j\eta\omega)$ is dominated by the real term $\omega_0^2$, making $x_0$ purely real. This means the electron's position perfectly tracks the force in the time domain: $x(t) \propto \cos(\omega t)$. Taking the derivative to find velocity gives $v(t) \propto -\sin(\omega t)$ (which is $90^\circ$ out of phase). The light does positive work half the cycle and takes it right back the other half. Net result over a full optical cycle:
    \begin{equation*}
        \langle P \rangle \propto \langle F(t) \cdot v(t) \rangle = \langle \cos(\omega t) \cdot (-\sin(\omega t)) \rangle = 0
    \end{equation*}
    No net absorption. The atom just jiggles elastically and lets the light pass.

    \item \textbf{Case 2 — High Frequency ($\omega \gg \omega_0$):} The denominator is dominated by the real term $-\omega^2$, so $x_0$ is purely real but negative. The position is exactly inverted: $x(t) \propto -\cos(\omega t)$. The derivative is velocity $v(t) \propto \sin(\omega t)$. The force and velocity are still $90^\circ$ out of sync, so the exact same cancellation occurs:
    \begin{equation*}
        \langle P \rangle \propto \langle F(t) \cdot v(t) \rangle = \langle \cos(\omega t) \cdot \sin(\omega t) \rangle = 0
    \end{equation*}
    No net absorption. The light oscillates too fast for the electron to keep up.

    \item \textbf{Case 3 — On Resonance ($\omega = \omega_0$):} The two real terms kill each other: $\omega_0^2 - \omega^2 = 0$. The denominator collapses to the purely imaginary $j\eta\omega$, making $x_0$ purely imaginary. In the time domain, dividing by an imaginary number forces the position wave to lag by $90^\circ$, meaning it becomes a sine wave: $x(t) \propto \sin(\omega t)$. Now, when we take the derivative to find velocity, it turns back into a cosine wave! $v(t) \propto \cos(\omega t)$. Velocity is now perfectly in phase with the force $F(t) \propto \cos(\omega t)$. 
    \begin{equation*}
        v_0 = j\omega \cdot x_0 \quad \xrightarrow{x_0 \text{ is imaginary}} \quad v_0 \text{ is purely \emph{real} (in phase with force)}
    \end{equation*}
    Because force and velocity are perfectly in sync, Power $= F \cdot v$ is strictly positive at every instant. The light irreversibly dumps all its energy into the atom:
    \begin{equation*}
        \langle P \rangle \propto \langle F(t) \cdot v(t) \rangle = \langle \cos(\omega t) \cdot \cos(\omega t) \rangle = \langle \cos^2(\omega t) \rangle = \tfrac{1}{2} > 0
    \end{equation*}
    This is the perfect resonance absorption condition — the atom acts as an uninterrupted energy sink for the light wave.
\end{itemize}
\end{keyinsight}

Before we can pull these microscopic insights into the macroscopic world, we must do one mathematical housekeeping step: explicitly separating the electron's displacement phasor ($x_0$) into purely real ($x'$) and entirely imaginary ($x''$) components. This split is the strict mathematical root of why a material can simultaneously shift the phase of light while absorbing its energy.

We start directly from the base equation for the displacement amplitude we just derived in the classical model:
\begin{equation*}
    x_0 = -\frac{e}{m} \left[ \frac{E_0}{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2) + j\eta\omega} \right]
\end{equation*}

To isolate the real and imaginary parts, we must clear the complex unit $j$ from the denominator. We do this by multiplying the numerator and denominator by the complex conjugate of the denominator, $(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2) - j\eta\omega$:
\begin{align*}
    x_0 &= -\frac{e}{m} \left[ \frac{E_0}{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2) + j\eta\omega} \cdot \frac{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2) - j\eta\omega}{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2) - j\eta\omega} \right] \\[1ex]
    &= -\frac{e}{m} \left[ \frac{E_0[(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2) - j\eta\omega]}{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2)^2 + (\eta\omega)^2} \right]
\end{align*}

We can now cleanly separate the fraction into a purely real part ($x'$) and a purely imaginary part ($x''$), defined such that $x_0 = x' + jx''$.

The real part $x'$ gives the in-phase displacement component, and the imaginary part $x''$ gives the out-of-phase (lagging) displacement component:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{gather}
    x' = -\frac{e}{m} \left[ \frac{E_0(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2)}{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2)^2 + (\eta\omega)^2} \right] \quad \text{(Real Part $\to$ In-Phase Displacement)} \\[1ex]
    x'' = \frac{e}{m} \left[ \frac{E_0(\eta\omega)}{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2)^2 + (\eta\omega)^2} \right] \quad \text{(Imaginary Part $\to$ Out-of-Phase Displacement)}
\end{gather}
\end{keyresult}

In short, by applying the complex conjugate technique to the displacement phasor, we cleanly separated the electron's response into two strictly real quantities: $x'$, the in-phase component that will scale up to drive the macroscopic refractive index, and $x''$, the out-of-phase component that will dictate optical absorption. With the single-atom picture fully resolved, we are now ready to scale this up to an entire block of material.

\section{Susceptibility and the Complex Refractive Index}

\subsection{From Single-Atom Dipole to Macroscopic Polarization}

Now we'll scale up from a single microscopic atom to an entire macroscopic block of material. Recall from \textbf{\nameref{subsec:driven_equation}} that we derived the phasor displacement $x_0$ of the electron relative to its nucleus. This displaced charge pair — the positive nucleus and the displaced negative electron — forms an electric dipole. Physically, the \textbf{atomic dipole moment} quantifies how far the charge has been pulled apart and how strong the resulting local electric field distortion is. We define it as charge times displacement:
\begin{equation*}
    p_0 = q\,x_0 = -e\,x_0
\end{equation*}
where $q = -e$ is the electron charge (negative by convention). The larger the displacement $x_0$, the stronger the dipole.

If our material contains $N_A$ optically active atoms per unit volume, then the total \textbf{macroscopic polarization} $P_0$ — the aggregate electrical stretching of the material per unit volume — is simply the sum of all individual dipoles:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    P_0 = N_A\,p_0 = -N_A\,e\,x_0
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $P_0$: The macroscopic polarization (units: C/m$^2$), representing the total dipole moment per unit volume.
    \item $N_A$: The number density of active atoms (atoms per m$^3$).
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/microscopic_to_macroscopic.jpg}
    \caption{From Microscopic to Macroscopic: Integrating $N_A$ microscopic atomic dipoles per unit volume yields the macroscopic polarization vector $\mathbf{P}$.}
    \label{fig:ch1_macroscopic_polarization}
\end{figure}

In short, each atom's displaced electron cloud creates a tiny dipole $p_0$, and stacking $N_A$ of these per unit volume gives the macroscopic polarization $P_0 = -N_A e\,x_0$. This is the quantity that links the microscopic atomic world to Maxwell's macroscopic equations.

\subsection{Deriving the Susceptibility \texorpdfstring{$\chi(\omega)$}{χ(ω)}}

Recall from fundamental electromagnetics that macroscopic polarization is formally linked to the external electric field by a dimensionless material property called the \textbf{electric susceptibility} $\chi$:
\begin{equation*}
    P_0 = \varepsilon_0\,\chi(\omega)\,E_0
\end{equation*}
where $\varepsilon_0$ is the permittivity of free space and $\chi(\omega)$ is a complex, frequency-dependent number that tells us exactly how strongly the material polarizes in response to the applied field at frequency $\omega$.

To find a concrete expression for $\chi(\omega)$, we equate our two definitions of $P_0$:
\begin{equation*}
    \varepsilon_0\,\chi(\omega)\,E_0 = -N_A\,e\,x_0
\end{equation*}
Substitute the phasor displacement $x_0 = -\dfrac{e}{m}\dfrac{E_0}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2 + j\eta\omega}$ that we derived in \textbf{\nameref{subsec:driven_equation}}:
\begin{equation*}
    \varepsilon_0\,\chi(\omega)\,E_0 = -N_A\,e \left(-\frac{e}{m}\frac{E_0}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2 + j\eta\omega}\right) \implies \varepsilon_0\,\chi(\omega)\,E_0 = N_A\,e \cdot \frac{e}{m}\frac{E_0}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2 + j\eta\omega}
\end{equation*}
Next, we group all numerator terms together:
\begin{equation*}
    \varepsilon_0\,\chi(\omega)\,E_0 = \frac{N_A\,e^2\,E_0}{m(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2 + j\eta\omega)}
\end{equation*}
To isolate $\chi(\omega)$, divide both sides by $\varepsilon_0\,E_0$:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\varepsilon_0\,\chi(\omega)\,E_0}{\varepsilon_0\,E_0} = \frac{N_A\,e^2\,E_0}{m\,\varepsilon_0\,E_0(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2 + j\eta\omega)}
\end{equation*}
The $E_0$ fields explicitly cancel on both sides, yielding our fundamental result:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    \chi(\omega) = \frac{N_A\,e^2}{\varepsilon_0\,m}\,\frac{1}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2 + j\eta\omega}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}
Note that $\chi(\omega)$ is complex-valued — its real part will drive dispersion and its imaginary part will drive absorption or gain. This expression is \emph{exact} within the Lorentz model. Its magnitude peaks sharply at $\omega = \omega_0$, and its phase encodes whether the material's response leads or lags the driving field.

\subsubsection{The Exact \texorpdfstring{$\chi'$, $\chi''$}{χ', χ''} Spectral Profiles}

Just like we did for the microscopic displacement $x_0$, we can decompose the total complex susceptibility $\chi(\omega)$ into its purely real part ($\chi'$) and purely imaginary part ($\chi''$). To find their exact profiles, we recall the foundational bridge equation established earlier that links the macroscopic susceptibility directly to the microscopic displacement:
\begin{equation*}
    \chi = -\frac{N_A e}{\varepsilon_0 E_0} x_0
\end{equation*}

By explicitly writing both variables in their expanded complex forms ($\chi = \chi' + j\chi''$ and $x_0 = x' + jx''$), we can substitute them directly into the bridge equation:
\begin{equation*}
    \chi' + j\chi'' = -\frac{N_A e}{\varepsilon_0 E_0} (x' + jx'')
\end{equation*}

To see the relationship clearly, we distribute the scaling coefficient on the right-hand side:
\begin{equation*}
    \chi' + j\chi'' = \left(-\frac{N_A e}{\varepsilon_0 E_0}x'\right) + j\left(-\frac{N_A e}{\varepsilon_0 E_0}x''\right)
\end{equation*}

For two complex equations to be mathematically equal, their purely real parts must exactly match, and their strictly imaginary parts must exactly match. Equating them directly proves that the macroscopic susceptibility components are nothing more than perfectly scaled replicas of the electron's displacement components:
\begin{equation*}
    \chi' = -\frac{N_A e}{\varepsilon_0 E_0}x' \quad \text{and} \quad \chi'' = -\frac{N_A e}{\varepsilon_0 E_0}x''
\end{equation*}

Finally, we simply substitute our exact algebraic formulas for $x'$ and $x''$ derived in \textbf{\nameref{subsec:driven_equation}} into these two scaling equations. This immediately yields the exact mathematical forms for the macroscopic susceptibility profiles:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{gather}
    \chi'(\omega) = \frac{N_A\,e^2}{\varepsilon_0\,m} \left[ \frac{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2}{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2)^2 + (\eta\omega)^2} \right] \quad \text{(Real Part $\to$ Controls Dispersion)} \\[1ex]
    \chi''(\omega) = -\frac{N_A\,e^2}{\varepsilon_0\,m} \left[ \frac{\eta\omega}{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2)^2 + (\eta\omega)^2} \right] \quad \text{(Imaginary Part $\to$ Controls Absorption)}
\end{gather}
\end{keyresult}

\begin{keyinsight}[The Universal Scope of the Exact Profiles]
These exact classical equations are universally true. They completely dictate the optical response of the material at \emph{every} possible electromagnetic frequency — from $0$ Hz (a DC field) all the way up to extreme X-rays. While physically flawless, their massive denominator terms make them bulky and slightly unintuitive to manipulate when mathematically modeling modern lasers or narrow-band filters.
\end{keyinsight}

In short, the full macroscopic susceptibility profiles perfectly inherit the real and imaginary structure of the isolated electron's displacement. Because dealing with these exact, full-spectrum formulas is mathematically cumbersome, we will now zoom in strictly on the ultra-narrow frequency range where lasers actually operate to derive a far more elegant and powerful approximation.

\subsubsection{The Near-Resonance Approximation}\label{subsec:susceptibility_spectral_forms}

While the exact equations cover the entire electromagnetic spectrum, in laser physics and applied optics we almost exclusively operate extremely close to the atomic resonance peak. This allows us to make a powerful simplifying assumption: $\omega \approx \omega_0$. 

Let's look at the term $\omega_0^2 - \omega^2$ appearing in our susceptibility equations. This is a classic difference of squares, which explicitly factors as:
\begin{equation*}
    \omega_0^2 - \omega^2 = (\omega_0 - \omega)(\omega_0 + \omega)
\end{equation*}
If we apply our assumption that $\omega \approx \omega_0$, then the sum $(\omega_0 + \omega) \approx 2\omega$:
\begin{equation*}
    \omega_0^2 - \omega^2 \approx (\omega_0 - \omega)(2\omega)
\end{equation*}

To write this cleanly, we define the \textbf{normalised frequency detuning} $\delta$, which measures exactly how far the driving frequency $\omega$ is from the resonance $\omega_0$, scaled by half the damping rate $\eta/2$:
\begin{equation*}
    \delta = \frac{\omega - \omega_0}{\eta/2} = \frac{2(\omega - \omega_0)}{\eta}
\end{equation*}
From this definition, we isolate $(\omega_0 - \omega)$:
\begin{equation*}
    \delta\eta = 2(\omega - \omega_0) \quad \Rightarrow \quad \omega - \omega_0 = \frac{\delta\eta}{2} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \omega_0 - \omega = -\frac{\delta\eta}{2}
\end{equation*}
Substitute back into the approximated difference of squares:
\begin{equation*}
    \omega_0^2 - \omega^2 \approx \left(-\frac{\delta\eta}{2}\right)(2\omega) = -\delta\eta\omega
\end{equation*}

Now we plug this into the denominator of $\chi(\omega)$:
\begin{equation*}
    \chi(\omega) \approx \frac{N_A\,e^2}{\varepsilon_0\,m}\,\frac{1}{-\delta\eta\omega + j\eta\omega}
\end{equation*}
Factor out the common term $\eta\omega$ from the denominator:
\begin{equation*}
    \chi(\omega) \approx \frac{N_A\,e^2}{\varepsilon_0\,m\,\eta\omega}\,\frac{1}{j - \delta}
\end{equation*}

To separate this into its purely real and purely imaginary parts ($\chi = \chi' + j\chi''$), we multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate of $(j - \delta)$, which is $(-j - \delta)$:
\begin{equation*}
    \chi(\omega) \approx \frac{N_A\,e^2}{\varepsilon_0\,m\,\eta\omega}\left(\frac{1}{j - \delta}\cdot\frac{-j - \delta}{-j - \delta}\right)
\end{equation*}
So the equation becomes:
\begin{equation*}
    \chi(\omega) \approx \frac{N_A\,e^2}{\varepsilon_0\,m\,\eta\omega}\left(\frac{-j - \delta}{1 + \delta^2}\right) = \frac{N_A\,e^2}{\varepsilon_0\,m\,\eta\omega}\left(\frac{-\delta}{1 + \delta^2} + j\,\frac{-1}{1 + \delta^2}\right)
\end{equation*}

By cleanly separating the real and imaginary components, we obtain the two functions that govern how light interacts with matter near a resonance:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{gather}
    \chi'(\omega) \approx \frac{N_A\,e^2}{\varepsilon_0\,m\,\eta\omega}\left(\frac{-\delta}{1 + \delta^2}\right) \quad \text{(Real Part $\to$ Controls Dispersion)}\\[1ex]
    \chi''(\omega) \approx \frac{N_A\,e^2}{\varepsilon_0\,m\,\eta\omega}\left(\frac{-1}{1 + \delta^2}\right) \quad \text{(Imaginary Part $\to$ Controls Loss/Gain)}
\end{gather}
\end{keyresult}

\begin{keyinsight}[Understanding the Detuning Factor $\delta$]
Both $\chi'$ and $\chi''$ share the same building blocks — a prefactor and a spectral shape function that depends entirely on $\delta$. But what is $\delta$ actually telling us?

Recall that we defined the normalised detuning as:
\begin{equation*}
    \delta = \frac{2(\omega - \omega_0)}{\eta}
\end{equation*}
Think of $\delta$ as a dimensionless ``score'' of how far off-resonance the driving light is. The denominator $\eta$ normalises that raw frequency gap against the damping rate, so $\delta$ tells you the mismatch \emph{relative to how fast the atom naturally loses energy}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{$\delta = 0$:} The driving frequency $\omega$ exactly equals the resonance $\omega_0$. We are perfectly on resonance. Because $\delta$ is exactly zero in the numerator of $\chi'$, dispersion vanishes entirely ($|\chi'| = 0$). However, the denominator $(1 + \delta^2) = 1$ is at its absolute minimum, meaning the absorption term $|\chi''|$ reaches its absolute maximum peak. At exact resonance, the atom's entire response goes into absorbing energy, not bending light.
    \item \textbf{$|\delta| = 1$:} We are off resonance by exactly $\eta/2$ in frequency (i.e., $|\omega - \omega_0| = \eta/2$). The denominator $(1 + \delta^2) = 2$, meaning the absorption $|\chi''|$ has dropped to exactly half of its peak value. However, something completely different happens for dispersion! Because $\chi' \propto \frac{\delta}{1+\delta^2}$, setting $|\delta|=1$ yields $\frac{1}{2}$, which is the mathematical \emph{maximum} of this function. So exactly at the points where absorption drops to half, dispersion fiercely peaks.
    \item \textbf{$|\delta| \gg 1$:} We are far from resonance — the mismatch $|\omega - \omega_0|$ is much larger than the damping rate $\eta$. The denominator $(1 + \delta^2) \approx \delta^2 \gg 1$, driving both $\chi'$ and $\chi''$ to essentially zero. Recall from our analysis in \textbf{\nameref{subsec:driven_equation}} that far off-resonance, the electron either acts as a pure spring (Case 1, $\omega \ll \omega_0$) or a pure inertial mass (Case 2, $\omega \gg \omega_0$) — and in both cases no net energy is absorbed. The large $\delta^2$ in the denominator is the mathematical signature of that same physics.
\end{itemize}
In short, $\delta$ is the single number that encodes where you are sitting on the resonance curve. The closer $|\delta|$ is to zero, the more dramatically the atom responds to the light.
\end{keyinsight}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/susceptibility_profiles.jpg}
    \caption{The Susceptibility Profiles: The dispersive real part ($\chi'$) and the Lorentzian imaginary part ($\chi''$) strictly govern refraction and absorption.}
    \label{fig:ch1_susceptibility_lineshapes}
\end{figure}

Now let's extract the physical implications embedded within the susceptibility profiles shown above. This visualization provides us with a rigorous mathematical map of the atom's overall optical response, simultaneously encoding the exact behaviour of both its internal friction (manifesting as absorption) and its physical inertia (manifesting as dispersion) as it interacts with the light.

Starting with the absorption peak: notice the double-headed arrow labelled FWHM. The \textbf{Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM)} is the total horizontal width of the absorption curve measured between the two points where $|\chi''|$ has dropped to exactly half of its peak value. Recall from our earlier primer in \textbf{\nameref{subsec:broadening_insight}} that we promised friction ($\eta$) physically smears the atom's frequency response into a wider band --- the FWHM is the precise, quantitative measure of that smearing. To prove this, we need to find where the absorption drops to half its peak. Since $|\chi''|$ is normalised to peak at 1, we set:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{1}{1 + \delta^2} = \frac{1}{2}
\end{equation*}
By cross-multiplying, we solve for $\delta$:
\begin{equation*}
    1 + \delta^2 = 2 \quad \implies \quad \delta^2 = 1 \quad \implies \quad \delta = \pm 1
\end{equation*}

This tells us that the absorption drops to 50\% at two specific normalised coordinates: $\delta_1 = -1$ and $\delta_2 = +1$. The total width of the curve in this normalised $\delta$ domain is simply the distance between these two points:
\begin{equation*}
    \Delta\delta = \delta_2 - \delta_1 = (+1) - (-1) = 2
\end{equation*}

Now we must convert this dimensionless span back to a physical angular frequency span ($\Delta\omega_{\text{FWHM}}$). Recall our definition of the normalised detuning:
\begin{equation*}
    \delta = \frac{2(\omega - \omega_0)}{\eta}
\end{equation*}

To find the width in terms of physical frequency, we substitute this definition for both half-maximum points back into our width equation $\Delta\delta = \delta_2 - \delta_1$:
\begin{equation*}
    2 = \left[ \frac{2(\omega_2 - \omega_0)}{\eta} \right] - \left[ \frac{2(\omega_1 - \omega_0)}{\eta} \right]
\end{equation*}

Since the terms share a common denominator $\eta$, we can combine the fractions. Notice how the central resonance frequency $\omega_0$ cancels out completely:
\begin{equation*}
    2 = \frac{2\omega_2 - 2\omega_0 - 2\omega_1 + 2\omega_0}{\eta}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
    2 = \frac{2(\omega_2 - \omega_1)}{\eta}
\end{equation*}

The term $(\omega_2 - \omega_1)$ is precisely the physical frequency span between the two half-maximum points, which is the exact definition of $\Delta\omega_{\text{FWHM}}$. Substituting this in yields the final physical linewidth:
\begin{equation*}
    2 = \frac{2(\Delta\omega_{\text{FWHM}})}{\eta} \quad \implies \quad \Delta\omega_{\text{FWHM}} = \eta
\end{equation*}

where $\Delta\omega_{\text{FWHM}}$ is the full width at half maximum in angular frequency (rad/s), and $\eta$ is the damping rate of the atom (rad/s). 

One important practical note before moving on. Throughout our derivations we have worked exclusively in angular frequency $\omega$ (rad/s), which is the natural language of the mathematics. However, optical bandwidth in any real laboratory is measured in ordinary frequency $\nu$ (Hz). Because $\omega = 2\pi\nu$, the frequency width $\Delta\nu_{\text{FWHM}}$ is found simply by dividing the angular frequency width by $2\pi$:
\begin{equation*}
    \Delta\nu_{\text{FWHM}} = \frac{\Delta\omega_{\text{FWHM}}}{2\pi} = \frac{\eta}{2\pi}
\end{equation*}

where $\Delta\nu_{\text{FWHM}}$ is the FWHM linewidth in Hz. This is the number you would directly read off a laser spectrum analyser.

We collect both of these extremely important forms into a single definitive summary:

\begin{keyresult}
\begin{gather}
    \Delta\omega_{\text{FWHM}} = \eta \nonumber \\
    \Delta\nu_{\text{FWHM}} = \frac{\eta}{2\pi}
\end{gather}
\end{keyresult}

In short, a physical system with higher internal friction naturally restricts the electron's ability to sustain a long, ringing oscillation, causing its excited state energy to decay much more rapidly. In the frequency domain, this faster time decay translates directly into a broader, more smeared-out resonance bandwidth. By proving that the Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM) is mathematically identical to $\eta$, we have accomplished something profound: we have taken an abstract friction coefficient from a microscopic, theoretical differential equation and revealed it to be exactly the macroscopic, easily measurable spectral width of the light that you would see on a laboratory spectrum analyser!

\begin{keyinsight}[Why Dispersion Flips but Absorption is Symmetrical]
Re-examining the susceptibility profiles plotted earlier, notice that the absorption curve ($\chi''$, the dashed line) is perfectly symmetric around $\delta = 0$. Mathematically, its denominator $(1+\delta^2)$ squares the detuning, erasing the sign. Physically, the atom doesn't care if you drive it too fast ($\delta > 0$) or too slow ($\delta < 0$) — either way, you are off-rhythm, and energy transfer drops symmetrically.

Dispersion ($\chi'$, the solid line), however, is wildly asymmetric — it is positive for negative detuning, crosses zero at resonance, and dips negative for positive detuning. Why? Because of the uncanceled $\delta$ in its numerator! 
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Negative Detuning ($\delta < 0$, $\omega < \omega_0$):} We are driving the electron slowly. As we saw in the first regime of \textbf{\nameref{subsec:driven_equation}}, it acts like a pure spring and moves \emph{in-phase} with the light. This in-phase electron movement adds constructively to the light wave, effectively making the material feel ``denser'' and \textbf{slowing the light down} (positive dispersion).
    \item \textbf{Positive Detuning ($\delta > 0$, $\omega > \omega_0$):} We are driving the electron too quickly. As we saw in the second regime of \textbf{\nameref{subsec:driven_equation}}, its inertia prevents it from keeping up, and its position flips to $180^\circ$ \emph{out-of-phase}. This completely opposite movement destructively interferes with the light wave, making the material feel ``less dense'' and causing the phase velocity to \textbf{speed up} (negative dispersion).
\end{itemize}
This beautifully illustrates how microscopic electron inertia is directly responsible for macroscopic phenomena like light slowing down in glass!
\end{keyinsight}

\begin{keyinsight}[Kramers-Kronig Relations]
Notice how $\chi'$ and $\chi''$ are intrinsically linked — they share the exact same denominator $(1 + \delta^2)$. This is not a coincidence. Because causation must precede effect (the causality principle), the dispersive and absorptive responses of any linear, causal medium are never independent: they are rigorously locked together via integral transforms known as the \textbf{Kramers-Kronig relations}. Knowing the absorption profile $\chi''(\omega)$ at all frequencies is sufficient to uniquely reconstruct the dispersion profile $\chi'(\omega)$, and vice versa. Physically, this means a material cannot absorb light without also bending it — dispersion and absorption are two inseparable facets of the same underlying causal response.
\end{keyinsight}

\begin{keyinsight}[Why the Approximate Form Is the Physically Relevant Profile]
One might ask: we applied $\omega \approx \omega_0$ to reach these expressions, so how can we confidently treat them as the universal spectral profiles? The key is that $\chi$ is only appreciable within a narrow bandwidth of order $\eta$ around $\omega_0$. Outside this bandwidth, $|\chi|$ is negligibly small and contributes virtually nothing to the refractive index, absorption, or any measurable optical property. Therefore, the near-resonance Lorentzian form captures the \emph{entire} physically significant behaviour of $\chi$, and the regions where the approximation breaks down are precisely the regions where $\chi$ is too small to matter.
\end{keyinsight}

In short, scaling $x_0$ to $N_A$ atoms builds the material susceptibility $\chi$. Splitting $\chi$ into real and imaginary parts proves that any medium interacting with light will simultaneously change the light's velocity ($\chi'$, dispersion) and change its power ($\chi''$, absorption or gain). Now we must connect $\chi$ to the refractive index and the wave propagation — and for that, we need the \textbf{relative permittivity}.

\subsection{Relative Permittivity and the Complex Refractive Index}\label{subsec:permittivity_and_n}

In every real optoelectronic device, there are \textit{two} optically distinct populations of atoms that we must account for simultaneously:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The host medium.} This is the passive, transparent bulk material — silica glass in an optical fibre, a YAG crystal in a solid-state laser, or even just a background gas in a vapour cell. The host has its own refractive index $n_b$ (the subscript $b$ stands for ``background''), which is real, large, and essentially constant across the narrow frequency ranges we care about. It does not absorb at our operating frequency.

    \item \textbf{The transition atoms.} These are the optically active species embedded inside the host — Er$^{3+}$ ions in a silica fibre, Nd$^{3+}$ ions in a YAG crystal, and so on. They are a tiny minority compared to the host atoms, but they are the only ones doing anything interesting: they absorb, amplify, and shift the phase of the wave via their quantum resonance. Their contribution to the susceptibility is small, complex, and strongly frequency-dependent.
\end{enumerate}

Every subsequent derivation relies explicitly on this physical split. The host medium establishes a large, purely real background, while the transition atoms introduce a small, complex, and highly resonant correction.

With this foundation in place, we can now define the exact quantity that bridges the microscopic atomic susceptibility to the macroscopic propagation of the wave.

\subsubsection{From Susceptibility to Permittivity — The General Case}\label{subsubsec:permittivity_general}

Recall from Maxwell's equations that the electric displacement field $\mathbf{D}$ is related to the applied electric field $\mathbf{E}$ and the polarisation $\mathbf{P}$ of the medium by:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{D} = \varepsilon_0 \mathbf{E} + \mathbf{P}
\end{equation*}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{D}$: the electric displacement field — the ``total'' field including the medium's response.
    \item $\varepsilon_0$: the permittivity of free space.
    \item $\mathbf{P}$: the macroscopic polarisation — the dipole moment per unit volume induced in the medium.
\end{itemize}

Now, $\mathbf{P}$ is linearly related to $\mathbf{E}$ via the electric susceptibility $\chi$:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{P} = \varepsilon_0 \chi \mathbf{E}
\end{equation*}
where $\chi$ is dimensionless and, in general, complex. Substituting this into the expression for $\mathbf{D}$:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{D} = \varepsilon_0 \mathbf{E} + \varepsilon_0 \chi \mathbf{E} = \varepsilon_0(1 + \chi)\mathbf{E}
\end{equation*}

We now define the \textbf{relative permittivity} $\varepsilon_r$ (also called the \textbf{dielectric constant}) as precisely the factor relating $\mathbf{D}$ to $\varepsilon_0 \mathbf{E}$:
\begin{equation*}
    \varepsilon_r = 1 + \chi
\end{equation*}
so that $\mathbf{D} = \varepsilon_0 \varepsilon_r \mathbf{E}$. Since $\chi$ is complex, $\varepsilon_r$ is also complex. This single number encodes everything the material does to an electromagnetic wave propagating through it — both its phase velocity (the real part) and its absorption or gain (the imaginary part).

But this formula, $\varepsilon_r = 1 + \chi$, implicitly assumes that the atoms are sitting in \textit{vacuum}. As we noted above, that is never the case in a real device. Let's now do the general case properly.

\subsubsection{The Total Permittivity: Host + Transition Atoms}\label{subsubsec:host_medium}

Since the host and the transition atoms are two independent populations, their polarisation responses simply add. The total susceptibility of the composite medium is therefore:
\begin{equation*}
    \chi_{\text{total}} = \chi_{\text{host}} + \chi_{\text{transition}}
\end{equation*}
and so the total relative permittivity is:
\begin{equation*}
    \varepsilon_r = 1 + \chi_{\text{total}} = 1 + \chi_{\text{host}} + \chi_{\text{transition}}
\end{equation*}

Now we apply two key physical facts about the host that let us simplify this immediately.

\textbf{Fact 1 — The host is lossless at our operating frequency.} Since the host is transparent, it has no absorption, which means its susceptibility is purely real:
\begin{equation*}
    \chi''_{\text{host}} = 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \chi_{\text{host}} \in \mathbb{R}
\end{equation*}
The host shifts the phase of the wave (it has a refractive index), but it does not attenuate it.

\textbf{Fact 2 — The host already has a well-defined refractive index $n_b$.} By the standard definition linking permittivity to refractive index, the host's permittivity is simply $n_b^2$:
\begin{equation*}
    \varepsilon_{r,\text{host}} = 1 + \chi_{\text{host}} = n_b^2
\end{equation*}
where $n_b$ is purely real. For reference, $n_b \approx 1.45$ for silica glass and $n_b \approx 3.5$ for GaAs. These are the numbers you would look up in a datasheet for the host material alone, ignoring the dopant atoms entirely.

We can now substitute directly. Starting from the general expression above:
\begin{equation*}
    \varepsilon_r = \underbrace{1 + \chi_{\text{host}}}_{=\; n_b^2} + \chi_{\text{transition}}
\end{equation*}

From this point forward, we will write $\chi$ to mean $\chi_{\text{transition}}$ exclusively — the small, complex, resonant susceptibility of the active atoms only. With that convention, the full expression collapses to:

\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    \varepsilon_r = n_b^2 + \chi
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $n_b^2$: The \textbf{host background} contribution — real, large, non-resonant. It sets the baseline refractive index that the wave ``sees'' even when the active atoms are completely inactive (e.g.\ unpumped).
    \item $\chi = \chi' + j\chi''$: The \textbf{transition atoms'} contribution — small, complex, and strongly resonant. $\chi'$ causes dispersion (a frequency-dependent shift in phase velocity); $\chi''$ causes absorption or gain.
\end{itemize}

Notice what happened structurally: the $1$ in the vacuum formula $\varepsilon_r = 1 + \chi$ has been replaced by $n_b^2$. This is not just a cosmetic change — $n_b^2$ can be much larger than $1$ (e.g.\ $n_b = 3.5$ gives $n_b^2 = 12.25$), so the host completely dominates the permittivity. The vacuum case is simply the special case where $n_b = 1$.

\subsubsection{Deriving the Complex Refractive Index \texorpdfstring{$\tilde{n}$}{ñ}}\label{subsubsec:complex_n}

We know from electromagnetics that the refractive index of a material is defined as $n = \sqrt{\varepsilon_r}$. Since $\varepsilon_r = n_b^2 + \chi$ is complex (because $\chi$ is complex), the square root will also be complex in general. We therefore define the \textbf{complex refractive index} $\tilde{n}$ as the quantity whose square equals $\varepsilon_r$:
\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{n}^2 = \varepsilon_r = n_b^2 + \chi, \qquad \tilde{n} \equiv n + j\kappa
\end{equation*}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $n$: the real refractive index. It determines how fast the wave's phase propagates through the medium (phase velocity is $c_0/n$). It ``rides'' on top of the host background $n_b$, shifted slightly by the dispersive part $\chi'$ of the transition atoms' susceptibility.
    \item $\kappa$: the \textbf{extinction coefficient}. It determines how quickly the wave's amplitude grows or decays as it propagates. It is entirely due to the absorptive part $\chi''$, and is scaled down by the host index $n_b$.
\end{itemize}

Our goal now is to isolate $n$ and $\kappa$ individually in terms of quantities we already know: $n_b$, $\chi'$, and $\chi''$. We start by relating them through the square root of the relative permittivity:
\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{n} = \sqrt{\varepsilon_r} = \sqrt{n_b^2 + \chi}
\end{equation*}

By factoring out $n_b^2$, we can expose a dimensionless ratio inside the square root:
\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{n} = \sqrt{n_b^2\!\left(1 + \frac{\chi}{n_b^2}\right)} = n_b\sqrt{1 + \frac{\chi}{n_b^2}}
\end{equation*}

At this point, we apply the binomial approximation $(1+x)^{1/2} \approx 1 + x/2$. This expansion is strictly valid only when $|x| \ll 1$, which in our derivation mathematically requires that $|\chi| \ll n_b^2$. Before executing this expansion, we must ask: does this harsh mathematical constraint actually hold true in real optoelectronic materials?

\begin{keyinsight}[Why the Transition Atom Contribution is Always Small]
It is not merely a mathematical convenience—it is physically guaranteed. The active transition atoms are just a \textit{dilute dopant} embedded inside a vast host lattice. There are, for example, vastly fewer Er$^{3+}$ ions per unit volume than native SiO$_2$ molecules in an optical fiber. Since macroscopic susceptibility scales directly with number density, the dopants inherently produce only a microscopic perturbation ($\chi$) atop the host's massive background permittivity ($n_b^2$). Therefore, the condition $|\chi| \ll n_b^2$ is an intrinsic geometric feature of every physical device we will ever analyze.
\end{keyinsight}

Because the condition securely holds, we substitute $x = \chi / n_b^2$ into the binomial expansion:
\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{n} \approx n_b\!\left(1 + \frac{\chi}{2n_b^2}\right) = n_b + \frac{\chi}{2n_b}
\end{equation*}

Finally, substituting the complex formulation $\chi = \chi' + j\chi''$ explicitly separates the expression into purely real and imaginary components:
\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{n} \approx n_b + \frac{\chi' + j\chi''}{2n_b} = \left(n_b + \frac{\chi'}{2n_b}\right) + j\left(\frac{\chi''}{2n_b}\right)
\end{equation*}

Matching this directly against our definition $\tilde{n} = n + j\kappa$, we can read off the equations for the real refractive index and the extinction coefficient:

\begin{keyresult}
\begin{align}
    n      &\approx n_b + \frac{\chi'}{2n_b} \nonumber \\[1ex]
    \kappa &\approx \frac{\chi''}{2n_b}
\end{align}
\end{keyresult}

We can notice that if the transition atoms are switched off entirely ($\chi = 0$), then $n \to n_b$ and $\kappa \to 0$: the wave propagates through the host with no attenuation, exactly as expected.

\begin{keyinsight}[The Vacuum Case]
If the transition atoms were not embedded in a solid host medium but were instead floating freely in a pure vacuum (such as in a low-pressure atomic gas cell), the background refractive index becomes simply that of free space: $n_b = 1$. In this perfectly evacuated environment, the massive host background vanishes entirely, and the macroscopic permittivity reduces to $\varepsilon_r = 1 + \chi$. This direct relationship structurally yields the exact vacuum approximations $n \approx 1 + \chi'/2$ and $\kappa \approx \chi''/2$.
\end{keyinsight}

In short, by factoring the relative permittivity as $\varepsilon_r = n_b^2 + \chi$ and applying the binomial expansion (justified by the geometric restriction $|\chi| \ll n_b^2$), we dissect the complex refractive index into two fundamentally distinct physical components. The real refractive index $n$ governs the wave's phase velocity and is overwhelmingly dominated by the passive background host. In contrast, the extinction coefficient $\kappa$ exclusively controls the exponential amplitude growth or decay, and is driven entirely by the active quantum transitions of our dopant atoms. Together, these two fundamental numbers dictate the complete macroscopic evolution of an electromagnetic wave traversing a medium. Armed with these parameters, we can now investigate exactly how they manifest in the propagating electric field itself.

\section{Wave Propagation: Attenuation and Dispersion}
\subsection{Power Attenuation and the Absorption Coefficient \texorpdfstring{$\alpha$}{α}}\label{subsec:power_attenuation}

Recall that a plane wave propagating in the $+z$ direction through a medium with complex refractive index $\tilde{n}$ has the form:
\begin{equation*}
    E(z) = E_0\,e^{-j\tilde{n}\,k_0\,z} = E_0\,e^{-j(n + j\kappa)\,k_0\,z} = E_0\,e^{-jn\,k_0\,z}\cdot e^{\kappa\,k_0\,z}
\end{equation*}
where $k_0 = 2\pi/\lambda_0$ is the free-space wavenumber and $\lambda_0$ is the free-space wavelength.

The first exponential $e^{-jnk_0z}$ is the oscillating phase term — it describes the wave's spatial oscillation as it travels. The second exponential $e^{\kappa k_0 z}$ is a real amplitude term. Because $\chi''$ is inherently negative, $\kappa$ is also negative, meaning this factor causes the amplitude to decay with $z$.

The optical power (intensity) is proportional to $|E|^2$, so:
\begin{equation*}
    P(z) \propto |E(z)|^2 \propto e^{2\kappa\,k_0\,z}
\end{equation*}
We define the \textbf{power attenuation coefficient} $\alpha$ so that power decays (or grows) as:
\begin{equation*}
    P(z) = P(0)\,e^{-\alpha z}
\end{equation*}
Comparing the two expressions:
\begin{equation*}
    e^{-\alpha z} = e^{2\kappa\,k_0\,z} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \alpha = -2k_0\kappa
\end{equation*}
Substitute our expression $\kappa = \chi''/(2n_b)$:
\begin{equation*}
    \alpha = -2k_0 \cdot \frac{\chi''}{2n_b}
\end{equation*}
The factor of 2 cancels exactly:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    \alpha = -\frac{k_0}{n_b}\,\chi''
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

Because $\chi''$ is inherently negative in our sign convention (absorption corresponds to $\chi'' < 0$), the formula $\alpha = -k_0\chi''/n_b$ yields a strictly positive $\alpha$, enforcing exponential power decay. The physical meaning is clear:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\alpha > 0$: The power shrinks exponentially with distance — \textbf{attenuation (loss)}.
    \item $\alpha < 0$: The power grows exponentially with distance — \textbf{amplification (gain)}.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/power_decay_lossy_medium.jpg}
    \caption{Power Decay in a Lossy Medium: A strictly positive attenuation coefficient ($\alpha > 0$) causes the wave's power to decay exponentially with distance.}
    \label{fig:ch1_power_decay}
\end{figure}

In short, the imaginary part of the susceptibility $\chi''$ directly controls how fast power decays (or grows) through the material, via $\alpha = -k_0\chi''/n_b$. Together, $n$ and $\alpha$ completely describe a wave's journey through any linear optical medium. Now let's understand how $n$ varies across the frequency spectrum.

\subsection{Normal vs.\ Anomalous Dispersion}

Now let's talk about how the real part of the refractive index $n$ behaves across the frequency spectrum. Away from any resonance, the refractive index $n$ increases as $\omega$ increases. This is the well-known behavior called \textbf{Normal Dispersion} — it is why a prism separates white light into a rainbow, with blue (higher $\omega$) bending more than red (lower $\omega$).

However, very close to the resonance frequency $\omega_0$, something unusual happens. The real part $\chi'$ (which controls $n$) has a characteristic dispersive profile that actually \emph{dips} near $\omega_0$. In a narrow band around resonance, the refractive index $n$ \textbf{decreases} as $\omega$ increases. This phenomenon is called \textbf{Anomalous Dispersion}.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/anomalous_dispersion_single.jpg}
    \caption{Normal vs Anomalous Dispersion (Single Resonance): The localized anomalous dispersion dip temporarily interrupts the fundamental trend of normal dispersion.}
    \label{fig:ch1_single_dispersion}
\end{figure}

\begin{keyinsight}[Normal Dispersion vs.\ Our Formula: The Apparent Contradiction]
We derived $n \approx n_b + \frac{\chi'}{2n_b}$, and our near-resonance formula gives $\chi' \approx \frac{N_A e^2}{\varepsilon_0 m \eta\omega}\left(\frac{-\delta}{1+\delta^2}\right)$. This formula dictates a steep downward slope near resonance, meaning $n$ should \emph{drop} as $\omega$ increases. Yet normal dispersion in glass shows $n$ smoothly rising with frequency. Why?

The issue is that our $\delta$-based formula was derived solely for $\omega \approx \omega_0$. It captures only the steep anomalous dip \emph{inside} the resonance, and breaks down entirely in the broad transparent regions between resonances.

The full susceptibility, $\chi(\omega) = \frac{N_A e^2}{\varepsilon_0 m}\frac{1}{\omega_0^2-\omega^2+j\eta\omega}$, tells the complete story. For visible light, the atomic resonances of glass lie far in the deep UV ($\omega_0 \gg \omega$), so $\omega_0^2$ dominates the denominator. As $\omega$ slowly increases toward that distant resonance, $\omega_0^2 - \omega^2$ shrinks, causing $\chi'$ and therefore $n$ to steadily climb. This is the true mechanism of normal dispersion.
\end{keyinsight}

In short, the refractive index $n$ increases with frequency everywhere (normal dispersion) \emph{except} in the immediate vicinity of a resonance, where it temporarily decreases (anomalous dispersion). The anomalous region is also exactly where absorption is strongest.

\subsection{Multiple Resonances and the Broadband Refractive Index}

Recall that in \textbf{\nameref{sec:classical_electron_oscillator}} we only considered a single resonance at $\omega_0$. In real materials, atoms and molecules have many different resonance frequencies — some in the infrared (IR), some in the visible, and some in the ultraviolet (UV). Each resonance produces its own localised absorption peak and its own anomalous dispersion dip.

As we sweep across the entire electromagnetic spectrum, the refractive index $n$ shows a staircase-like pattern: it climbs gradually (normal dispersion) between resonances and briefly dips (anomalous dispersion) whenever it crosses through a resonance. This broadband behavior explains why real glass has a smoothly varying $n(\lambda)$ curve across the visible range — it is in a normal-dispersion region between the nearest IR and UV resonances.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/broadband_refractive_index.jpg}
    \caption{Broadband Refractive Index: Normal dispersion occurs between resonance peaks, while localized anomalous dispersion sharply dips exactly at each atomic resonance.}
    \label{fig:ch1_broadband_dispersion}
\end{figure}

In short, real materials possess multiple atomic and molecular resonances spread widely across the electromagnetic spectrum. The overall broadband refractive index $n(\omega)$ that we measure in the laboratory is the continuous cumulative result of all of them perfectly overlapping, with each distinct resonance contributing its own dedicated absorption band and highly localised anomalous dispersion dip. At this point, we have built a complete, powerful toolkit strictly from the classical model: the susceptibility $\chi$, the complex refractive index $\tilde{n}$, and the macroscopic attenuation coefficient $\alpha$. Now let's confidently use these foundational tools to dissect the exact spectral profile of absorption — the line shape function.

\section{The Line Shape Function}

\subsection{Motivation: What Happens to Light Passing Through Matter?}

Imagine shining a broadband light source—like a continuous white-light continuum covering a wide spread of frequencies—directly into a block of material or a chamber of gas. What emerges on the other side? 

If we shine this emerging light through a prism, it disperses into the continuous visible spectrum — from red at one end to violet at the other. But fundamentally, each distinct color simply represents a distinct driving frequency $\nu$. The material treats every single color (frequency) completely differently.

For the vast majority of frequencies, the light passes directly through the medium. The photons at these frequencies might slightly jiggle the electron clouds as they pass (which, as derived in \textbf{\nameref{subsubsec:complex_n}}, gives rise to the real refractive index $n$ and macroscopic phase delay), but they do not possess the exact required energy to be permanently captured. They are scattered forward and emerge on the other side. This baseline behavior is what we call \textbf{transmission}.

However, at certain highly specific frequencies—the fundamental \textbf{resonance frequencies} of the material—something completely different happens. To understand this, we must look at the quantum structure of the atoms themselves. Every atom possesses a distinct, quantized set of allowed electron orbits or energy levels. If an incoming photon carries a packet of energy that \emph{exactly} matches the energy gap between a lower level ($E_1$) and an empty upper level ($E_2$), the photon is completely annihilated. 

Its entire energy is permanently absorbed by the electron, violently promoting the electron into the higher excited state. This resonant condition is governed by the celebrated Planck-Einstein relation:
\begin{equation*}
    E_{\text{photon}} = \Delta E = E_2 - E_1 = h\nu_0
\end{equation*}
where $h$ is Planck's constant, and $\nu_0$ is the resonance frequency required to trigger the jump.

Because these specific photons are destroyed and their energy is locally consumed by the material, that specific frequency of light disappears from the transmitted beam. If you look at the prism's rainbow on the output, you will see a bright, continuous rainbow of transmitted light interrupted by dark, missing narrow shadows (exactly like the famous Fraunhofer dark lines in the solar spectrum). These missing frequencies are what we define as \textbf{absorption lines}.

In short, most frequencies pass through a material essentially unchanged, but photons whose frequency exactly matches an atomic transition energy $h\nu_0$ are destroyed. The result is a transmission spectrum punctuated by dark absorption shadows — each shadow corresponding to a specific quantum jump inside the atom.

\begin{keyinsight}[Multiple Transitions Mean Multiple Lines]
Real atoms and molecules are complex. They do not have just two energy levels; they have dozens or hundreds, creating a vast ladder of possible states. Consequently, a photon can be absorbed not just from level $1\to2$, but perhaps $1\to3$, $2\to4$, or $3\to5$. Every single unique allowed transition gap ($\Delta E$) produces its own unique resonance frequency ($\nu_0$). Because of this, the output spectrum of a real material will feature a unique ``fingerprint'' of multiple distinct absorption lines, each corresponding to a specific internal quantum jump.
\end{keyinsight}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/output_transmission_spectrum.jpg}
    \caption{The Output Transmission Spectrum: The baseline transmission remains high, but drops sharply at the specific atomic resonance frequencies, carving out characteristic absorption shadows in the spectrum.}
    \label{fig:ch1_transmission_spectrum}
\end{figure}

At this point, if we take the mathematical idealisation strictly at face value, the output intensity spectrum seems conceptually straightforward: it is essentially a completely flat baseline curve suddenly punctuated by several infinitely thin, perfectly vertical, sharp dark dips situated at exactly the atomic resonances $\nu_0$. However, as we are about to rigorously discover through the lens of decay dynamics, this highly simplified picture is fundamentally physically impossible. Atomic energy transitions are never perfectly sharp, and they must inherently occupy a continuous, finite spectral bandwidth.

\subsection{The Absorption Line Is Not a Delta Function}

If we look back at our transmission spectrum, one might naively expect the absorption at a resonance frequency to be a perfect vertical spike. Mathematically, this would be a Dirac delta function $\delta(\nu - \nu_0)$ in the frequency domain, implying the atom absorbs energy at \emph{exactly} $\nu_0$ and absolutely nowhere else. However, the physical reality is fundamentally different.

In practice, the absorption always spreads over a \textbf{range of frequencies} centred around $\nu_0$. The absorption is maximum precisely at $\nu_0$, but it smoothly tapers off on either side. Why does this happen?

Recall from \textbf{\nameref{sec:damping_and_lifetime}} that any real oscillating system experiences damping (friction $\eta$), and this damping inherently broadens the resonance response. In the quantum picture, this corresponds to the fact that atomic energy levels are not perfectly sharp single values—they possess an intrinsic energy spread dictated by the finite lifetime of the excited state (a direct consequence of the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle). 

Consequently, if a photon arrives with a frequency $\nu$ that is not exactly $\nu_0$, the atomic transition can still occur as long as $\nu$ falls within this broadened bandwidth. Maximum absorption still occurs exactly at resonance, but there is a non-zero probability of absorption happening in the immediate neighbourhood around it.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/delta_vs_real_absorption.jpg}
    \caption{Delta Function vs. Real Absorption: Mathematical idealisation of an infinitely sharp line compared to the physically smeared Lorentzian bandwidth.}
    \label{fig:ch1_delta_vs_lorentzian}
\end{figure}

To model this smeared probability mathematically, we define the \textbf{line shape function} $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ — a continuous probability density representing the chance that a photon of frequency $\nu$ will be absorbed by an atomic transition centred at resonance frequency $\nu_0$. Because $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ is a true probability density function, the total area under the curve must equal exactly 1.

In short, an atomic transition never absorbs at one infinitely sharp frequency; damping (classically $\eta$, quantum-mechanically the finite excited-state lifetime) smears the absorption into a continuous probability distribution $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ centred at $\nu_0$. Now let's derive its explicit mathematical form.

\subsection{Deriving \texorpdfstring{$g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$}{g(ν)} from the Absorption Coefficient}\label{subsec:deriving_lineshape}

We will derive the line shape function directly from the absorption coefficient $\alpha$ that we obtained in the previous section. Recall the result from \textbf{\nameref{subsubsec:host_medium}}:
\begin{equation*}
    \alpha = -\frac{k_0}{n_b}\,\chi''
\end{equation*}
where $k_0 = 2\pi/\lambda_0$ is the free-space wavenumber and $n_b$ is the background host refractive index.

We now substitute the explicit expression for $\chi''$ that we derived in \textbf{\nameref{subsec:susceptibility_spectral_forms}}:
\begin{equation*}
    \chi'' \approx \frac{N_A\,e^2}{\varepsilon_0\,m\,\eta\omega}\left(\frac{-1}{1 + \delta^2}\right)
\end{equation*}
Substituting into $\alpha = -k_0\chi''/n_b$ and noting that $-1 \times -1 = +1$:
\begin{equation*}
    \alpha = \frac{k_0}{n_b}\cdot\frac{N_A\,e^2}{\varepsilon_0\,m\,\eta\omega}\cdot\frac{1}{1 + \delta^2}
\end{equation*}
where $\delta = 2(\omega - \omega_0)/\eta$ is the normalised frequency detuning.

We start by unpacking $\delta$ back into the denominator. Substitute $\delta = 2(\omega - \omega_0)/\eta$:
\begin{equation*}
    \alpha = \frac{k_0}{n_b}\cdot\frac{N_A\,e^2}{\varepsilon_0\,m\,\eta\omega}\cdot\frac{1}{1 + \dfrac{4(\omega - \omega_0)^2}{\eta^2}}
\end{equation*}

Now we convert from angular frequency $\omega$ to ordinary frequency $\nu$. We use the following substitution dictionary:
\begin{equation*}
    \omega = 2\pi\nu, \qquad \Delta\nu = \frac{\eta}{2\pi} = \frac{1}{2\pi\tau}, \qquad \eta = \frac{1}{\tau} = 2\pi\Delta\nu, \qquad k_0 = \frac{2\pi\nu}{c}
\end{equation*}
where $\tau$ is the lifetime of the upper energy level, $\Delta\nu$ is the FWHM linewidth, and we have used the vacuum dispersion relation $k_0 = \omega/c = 2\pi\nu/c$.

Substitute $\omega = 2\pi\nu$, $\eta = 1/\tau$, and $k_0 = 2\pi\nu/c$:
\begin{equation*}
    \alpha = \frac{2\pi\nu}{n_b\,c}\cdot\frac{N_A\,e^2}{\varepsilon_0\,m\cdot\frac{1}{\tau}\cdot 2\pi\nu}\cdot\frac{1}{1 + 4(2\pi)^2(\nu - \nu_0)^2\tau^2}
\end{equation*}
The factor $2\pi\nu$ from $k_0$ cancels against the $2\pi\nu$ in the denominator of the $\chi''$ term:
\begin{equation*}
    \alpha = \frac{N_A\,e^2\,\tau}{\varepsilon_0\,m\,n_b\,c}\cdot\frac{1}{1 + 4(2\pi)^2(\nu - \nu_0)^2\tau^2}
\end{equation*}

Now we must cleanly extract a common factor from the denominator to isolate the Lorentzian. The idea is to rewrite the denominator $1 + 4(2\pi)^2(\nu - \nu_0)^2\tau^2$ so that the $(\nu - \nu_0)^2$ term stands alone. We divide the entire denominator by $4(2\pi)^2\tau^2$, which means we must also divide the numerator by the same factor to keep the fraction unchanged:
\begin{equation*}
    \alpha = \frac{N_A\,e^2\,\tau}{\varepsilon_0\,m\,n_b\,c}\cdot\frac{\dfrac{1}{4\tau^2(2\pi)^2}}{\dfrac{1}{4\tau^2(2\pi)^2} + (\nu - \nu_0)^2}
\end{equation*}

We simplify the factor in the numerator. We have $\dfrac{1}{4\tau^2(2\pi)^2} = \left(\dfrac{1}{2\pi\tau}\right)^2\cdot\dfrac{1}{4}$, and since we defined $\Delta\nu = \dfrac{1}{2\pi\tau}$, this exactly equals $\left(\dfrac{\Delta\nu}{2}\right)^2$. Substituting:
\begin{equation*}
    \alpha = \frac{N_A\,e^2\,\tau}{\varepsilon_0\,m\,n_b\,c}\cdot\frac{\left(\dfrac{\Delta\nu}{2}\right)^2}{\left(\dfrac{\Delta\nu}{2}\right)^2 + (\nu - \nu_0)^2}
\end{equation*}

We now isolate the Lorentzian. Factor $\left(\frac{\Delta\nu}{2}\right)^2$ as $\frac{\Delta\nu}{2}\cdot\frac{\Delta\nu}{2}$, and note that $\tau = \frac{1}{2\pi\Delta\nu}$. Substitute:
\begin{gather*}
    \tau\cdot\left(\frac{\Delta\nu}{2}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{2\pi\Delta\nu}\cdot\frac{(\Delta\nu)^2}{4} = \frac{\Delta\nu}{8\pi}
\end{gather*}
Therefore:
\begin{equation*}
    \alpha = \frac{N_A\,e^2}{\varepsilon_0\,m\,n_b\,c}\cdot\frac{\Delta\nu}{8\pi}\cdot\frac{4}{(\Delta\nu/2)^2 + (\nu - \nu_0)^2}
\end{equation*}
Collecting terms and rearranging:
\begin{equation*}
    \alpha = \frac{N_A\,e^2}{4\varepsilon_0\,m\,n_b\,c}\cdot\underbrace{\frac{\Delta\nu/2\pi}{(\Delta\nu/2)^2 + (\nu - \nu_0)^2}}_{g_{\nu_0}(\nu)}
\end{equation*}

\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    \alpha = \frac{N_A\,e^2}{4\varepsilon_0\,m\,n_b\,c}\cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

where we define the \textbf{line shape function} (also called the \textbf{Lorentzian function} or \textbf{absorption profile}):
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    g_{\nu_0}(\nu) = \frac{\Delta\nu/2\pi}{\left(\dfrac{\Delta\nu}{2}\right)^2 + (\nu - \nu_0)^2}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

In short, by substituting $\alpha = -k_0\chi''/n_b$ and converting from angular to ordinary frequency, we successfully separated the frequency-dependent part of $\alpha$ into the universal Lorentzian line shape $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$. What remains as the prefactor is a collection of atomic constants that does not vary with frequency.

\subsection{The Radiative Lifetime \texorpdfstring{$\tau_r$}{τr} and the Clean Final Form}

The formula $\alpha = \dfrac{N_A\,e^2}{4\varepsilon_0\,m\,n_b\,c}\cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ contains a rather abstract prefactor built from fundamental constants ($e$, $m$, $\varepsilon_0$). In optics, we prefer to replace this with a single, directly measurable quantity: the \textbf{radiative lifetime} $\tau_r$.

The radiative lifetime is the average time an excited atom will spontaneously emit a photon and return to the ground state, \emph{in the absence of any other decay mechanism}. Physically, this timescale is set by how strongly the oscillating dipole couples to the surrounding electromagnetic field: a larger charge-to-mass ratio ($e^2/m$) means a stronger oscillating dipole, which radiates faster and therefore has a shorter $\tau_r$. A higher resonance frequency $\omega_0$ also means faster oscillation and faster radiation. Collecting these dependencies from the classical Larmor radiation formula yields:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    \tau_r = \frac{6\pi\varepsilon_0\,m\,c^3}{e^2\,\omega_0^2\,n_b}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\tau_r$: The spontaneous radiative lifetime of the excited state.
    \item $\omega_0 = 2\pi\nu_0$: The resonance angular frequency.
    \item $n_b$: The host refractive index (a denser medium means more electromagnetic modes, accelerating spontaneous emission and shortening $\tau_r$).
\end{itemize}

Now let us rearrange $\tau_r$ to isolate $e^2/(\varepsilon_0 m)$:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{e^2}{\varepsilon_0\,m} = \frac{6\pi\,c^3}{\tau_r\,\omega_0^2\,n_b}
\end{equation*}
Substitute into $\alpha$:
\begin{equation*}
    \alpha = \frac{N_A}{4n_b\,c}\cdot\frac{6\pi\,c^3}{\tau_r\,\omega_0^2\,n_b}\cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu) = \frac{6\pi\,N_A\,c^2}{4n_b^2\,\tau_r\,\omega_0^2}\cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{equation*}
Simplify $6/4 = 3/2$:
\begin{equation*}
    \alpha = \frac{3\pi\,N_A\,c^2}{2n_b^2\,\tau_r\,\omega_0^2}\cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{equation*}
Convert $\omega_0^2 = (2\pi\nu_0)^2 = 4\pi^2\nu_0^2$. Also, recall that $c = \lambda_0 \nu_0$ (the fundamental wave relation in vacuum), so $\lambda_0 = c/\nu_0$ and therefore $c^2/\nu_0^2 = \lambda_0^2$. Substituting both:
\begin{equation*}
    \alpha = \frac{3\pi\,N_A\,c^2}{2n_b^2\,\tau_r\cdot 4\pi^2\nu_0^2}\cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu) = \frac{3N_A\,\lambda_0^2}{8\pi\,n_b^2\,\tau_r}\cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{equation*}
Since the wavelength inside the host medium is $\lambda = \lambda_0/n_b$, we have $\lambda_0^2 = n_b^2\lambda^2$. Substituting:
\begin{equation*}
    \alpha = \frac{3N_A\,n_b^2\lambda^2}{8\pi\,n_b^2\,\tau_r}\cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{equation*}
The $n_b^2$ factors cancel completely:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    \alpha = \frac{3N_A\,\lambda^2}{8\pi\,\tau_r}\cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}
where $\lambda = \lambda_0/n_b$ is the wavelength of the light inside the host medium.

This is the elegant final form. All the messy atomic constants ($e$, $m$, $\varepsilon_0$) have been absorbed into a single measurable number $\tau_r$, and the wavelength appears squared — a hint that the optical cross section scales as $\lambda^2$.

In short, the absorption coefficient can be expressed in two complementary forms: the explicit atomic form $\alpha = \frac{N_A e^2}{4\varepsilon_0 m n_b c}\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ and the radiative-lifetime form $\alpha = \frac{3N_A\lambda^2}{8\pi\tau_r}\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$. Both will be used extensively in the chapters ahead.

\subsection{Normalisation and the Physical Meaning of \texorpdfstring{$g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$}{g(ν)}}

The line shape function $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ is \textbf{normalised to unity}:
\begin{equation*}
    \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g_{\nu_0}(\nu)\,d\nu = 1
\end{equation*}
This is what allows us to interpret it as a probability distribution function — the total probability that a photon somewhere in the spectrum is absorbed sums to one (as long as we consider only this one transition near $\nu_0$; at frequencies far from $\nu_0$, the probability is simply very small).

The term $\frac{1}{(\Delta\nu/2)^2 + (\nu - \nu_0)^2}$ gives $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ its Lorentzian shape. The prefactor $\frac{\Delta\nu}{2\pi}$ is the normalisation constant that ensures the integral equals one.

\begin{keyinsight}[Unit of the Line Shape Function]
The unit of $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ is $1/\text{frequency}$ (i.e., seconds), since it is a probability distribution over frequency. Integrating over all frequencies gives a dimensionless number (one), so $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ must carry inverse-frequency units.
\end{keyinsight}

In short, the line shape function $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ is a normalised Lorentzian probability density: it tells us how the absorption strength is distributed across frequency around $\nu_0$, and its total area is exactly one.

\subsection{The FWHM and the Line Width}

Let us now look at the line shape function graphically. It has a peak at $\nu = \nu_0$ with value $g_{\max} = \dfrac{2}{\pi\Delta\nu}$, and it decays symmetrically on both sides.

The quantity $\Delta\nu$ has a precise physical meaning:
\begin{equation*}
    \Delta\nu = \frac{1}{2\pi\tau} = \text{FWHM} = \text{Full Width at Half Maximum}
\end{equation*}
where \emph{Full Width at Half Maximum} means the width of the curve measured where $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ has fallen to half its peak value $g_{\max}/2$. Let us verify this claim explicitly. Substitute $\nu - \nu_0 = \pm\Delta\nu/2$ into $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$:
\begin{equation*}
    g_{\nu_0}\!\left(\nu_0 \pm \frac{\Delta\nu}{2}\right) = \frac{\Delta\nu/2\pi}{\left(\dfrac{\Delta\nu}{2}\right)^2 + \left(\pm\dfrac{\Delta\nu}{2}\right)^2} = \frac{\Delta\nu/2\pi}{\dfrac{(\Delta\nu)^2}{4} + \dfrac{(\Delta\nu)^2}{4}} = \frac{\Delta\nu/2\pi}{\dfrac{(\Delta\nu)^2}{2}}
\end{equation*}
Simplify:
\begin{equation*}
    g_{\nu_0}\!\left(\nu_0 \pm \frac{\Delta\nu}{2}\right) = \frac{\Delta\nu}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{2}{(\Delta\nu)^2} = \frac{1}{\pi\Delta\nu} = \frac{g_{\max}}{2} \quad \checkmark
\end{equation*}
This confirms that at exactly $\nu = \nu_0 \pm \Delta\nu/2$, the line shape function has fallen to exactly half its peak value. The total width between these two half-maximum points is $\Delta\nu/2 - (-\Delta\nu/2) = \Delta\nu$, which is indeed the Full Width at Half Maximum.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/lineshape_function_lorentzian.jpg}
    \caption{The Line Shape Function: The Lorentzian distribution normalised to unity, explicitly marking the peak amplitude and the Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM).}
    \label{fig:ch1_line_shape_fwhm}
\end{figure}

$\Delta\nu$ is also called the \textbf{line width} (or bandwidth, BW) of the transition. We can notice that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item As $\Delta\nu$ increases, the peak drops and $g(\nu)$ flattens and spreads.
    \item The area under $g(\nu)$ is always fixed at one, by normalisation.
\end{itemize}

The relationship $\Delta\nu = 1/(2\pi\tau)$ reveals a deep physical insight: the linewidth and the excited-state lifetime are inversely related. If an atom's excited state decays rapidly (short $\tau$), the emission/absorption event is brief, and the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle forces the energy — and thus the frequency — to be poorly defined. The result is a broad, smeared spectral profile. Conversely, if the excited state is long-lived (large $\tau$, as in a metastable state), the oscillation persists for many cycles, precisely defining the frequency and producing a very narrow, sharp spectral line. This is quantified by the celebrated \textbf{lifetime-bandwidth product}: $\Delta\nu \cdot \tau = 1/(2\pi)$.

In short, the line shape function $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ is a Lorentzian probability distribution that describes the frequency-resolved absorption probability around a resonance, and its width $\Delta\nu$ is set entirely by the lifetime $\tau$ of the upper energy state — short lifetime means broad line, long lifetime means narrow line. Now let's connect it to the absorption cross section — but first, we must address a fundamental limitation of the classical model.

\section{Why the Classical Model Cannot Produce Gain}

Before introducing the absorption cross section, let us make a crucial observation about everything we have derived so far. Recall from \textbf{\nameref{subsec:power_attenuation}} that our final absorption coefficient is:
\begin{equation*}
    \alpha = \frac{N_A\,e^2}{4\varepsilon_0\,m\,n_b\,c}\cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{equation*}

We can inspect every single factor in this expression:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $N_A$: Number density of atoms — strictly positive.
    \item $e^2$: Square of the electron charge — strictly positive.
    \item $\varepsilon_0$, $m$, $n_b$, $c$: All fundamental constants — strictly positive.
    \item $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$: A Lorentzian probability density — strictly positive everywhere.
\end{itemize}

There is absolutely no way for $\alpha$ to ever become negative. A strictly positive $\alpha$ dictates exponential power decay (loss) via $P(z) = P(0)e^{-\alpha z}$, which means the classical electron oscillator is fundamentally incapable of producing optical amplification. There is no parameter we can tune, no frequency we can choose, and no material density we can adjust that would flip the sign of $\alpha$.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/loss_vs_gain.jpg}
    \caption{Loss vs. Gain: The classical electron oscillator inherently enforces energy decay ($\alpha > 0$), mathematically forbidding the exponential amplification ($\alpha < 0$) required for lasers.}
    \label{fig:ch1_loss_vs_gain}
\end{figure}

In short, the classical electron oscillator gives us a beautiful framework for dispersion and absorption, but it cannot produce gain. To ultimately explain optical amplification — the mechanism that makes lasers possible — we will need a quantum model that separates atoms into distinct energy levels and introduces the concept of population inversion. This is precisely the motivation for everything that follows.

Now that we understand the line shape function $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ and the fundamental limitation of the classical model, let's connect $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ to a physically intuitive quantity: the absorption cross section.

\section{Absorption Cross Section}

\subsection{Definition and Physical Picture}

The \textbf{absorption cross section} $\sigma(\nu)$ is a quantity that tells us how strongly a single molecule (or equivalently, for our purposes, a single atom in a gas) interacts with light at frequency $\nu$. In the geometric picture used here, it is represented as an \emph{effective interaction area}: if the path of the light intersects that area, the event is counted as absorption. It is therefore not the literal physical size of the atom, but a compact way to describe the absorption strength of one atom at a given frequency.

Think of it this way. We have a large material through which light is passing. Inside that material, each atom contributes its own effective area $\sigma(\nu)$. The total absorption in the material is then the cumulative effect of all these microscopic interaction areas distributed throughout the medium. This allows us to represent a complicated distributed medium as a collection of discrete absorbers, each characterised by the same per-atom cross section at that frequency.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/absorption_cross_section.jpg}
    \caption{The Absorption Cross Section: a conceptual picture in which each atom contributes the same effective interaction area $\sigma(\nu)$; paths that intersect this area are absorbed, while paths that miss all such areas are transmitted.}
    \label{fig:ch1_cross_section_concept}
\end{figure}

The term we use is $\sigma(\nu)$, and its unit is area (e.g., $\text{m}^2$ or $\text{cm}^2$). The goal now is to derive what $\sigma(\nu)$ actually equals, using the intensity decay that we already know.

\subsection{Relating Intensity Change to the Cross Section}

As established in \textbf{\nameref{subsec:power_attenuation}}, the intensity of light decays exponentially as it travels through an absorbing medium:
\begin{equation*}
    I(z) = I_0\, e^{-\alpha z} \qquad (\text{intensity at position } z)
\end{equation*}
The intensity at position $z + \Delta z$ is:
\begin{equation*}
    I(z + \Delta z) = I_0\, e^{-\alpha(z+\Delta z)} = I_0\, e^{-\alpha z}\, e^{-\alpha \Delta z}
\end{equation*}
This is the intensity that exits the material slab of thickness $\Delta z$.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/fractional_intensity_decay.jpg}
    \caption{Fractional Intensity Decay: Tracking the drop in wave intensity precisely across a differential material slice of thickness $\Delta z$.}
    \label{fig:ch1_intensity_decay_slab}
\end{figure}

The fractional change in intensity across this slab is:
\begin{gather*}
    \frac{\Delta I}{I} = \frac{I(z+\Delta z) - I(z)}{I(z)} = \frac{I_0 e^{-\alpha z} e^{-\alpha\Delta z} - I_0 e^{-\alpha z}}{I_0 e^{-\alpha z}} \notag \\
    = e^{-\alpha\Delta z} - 1
\end{gather*}

Now we apply the Taylor approximation $e^{-\alpha\Delta z} \approx 1 - \alpha\Delta z$, which is valid as long as $\alpha\Delta z \ll 1$:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\Delta I}{I} = (1 - \alpha\Delta z) - 1 = -\alpha\Delta z
\end{equation*}

Substitute $\alpha = N_A\,\sigma(\nu)$ (which we will shortly justify):
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\Delta I}{I} = -N_A\,\sigma(\nu)\,\Delta z
\end{equation*}

Now, $N_A$ is the number of atoms per unit volume (number density). We can write:
\begin{equation*}
    N_A = \frac{\text{number of atoms}}{\text{volume}} = \frac{\text{number of atoms}}{\text{area} \times \Delta z}
\end{equation*}
Substituting this expression:
\begin{gather*}
    \frac{\Delta I}{I} = -\frac{\text{number of atoms}}{\text{area} \times \Delta z} \cdot \sigma(\nu) \cdot \Delta z \notag \\
    = -\frac{\text{number of atoms}}{\text{area}} \cdot \sigma(\nu)
\end{gather*}

Therefore:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\Delta I}{I} = -\frac{\text{number of atoms}}{\text{area}} \cdot \sigma(\nu)
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

This result is dimensionless (as it must be, since $\Delta I/I$ is a ratio), which confirms that $\sigma(\nu)$ must have units of area. This is why we call it a \emph{cross section}.

\textbf{Interpretation for a single atom:} If there is exactly one atom in the cross-sectional area, then:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\Delta I}{I} = -\frac{\sigma(\nu)}{\text{area}}
\end{equation*}
This tells us that a single atom reduces the incident intensity by a fraction equal to its cross section divided by the total beam area.

\textbf{For more than one atom:} Each atom contributes independently, so:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\Delta I}{I} = -\frac{\sigma}{\text{area}} - \frac{\sigma}{\text{area}} - \cdots = -\frac{\text{number of atoms} \times \sigma}{\text{area}}
\end{equation*}
which is entirely consistent with what we derived above.

\begin{keyinsight}[Equivalence of Intensity and Power]
Note that $\dfrac{\Delta I}{I} = \dfrac{\Delta P}{P}$, since $I = P/A$ and the area $A$ cancels. This means all the results we derived for fractional changes in intensity apply equally to fractional changes in optical power.
\end{keyinsight}

\subsection{Deriving the Explicit Formula for \texorpdfstring{$\sigma(\nu)$}{sigma(nu)}}

Now we derive the formula for $\sigma(\nu)$. We start from what we already know:
\begin{equation*}
    \tau_r = \frac{6\pi\varepsilon_0 n^2 m C_0^3}{q^2 \omega_0^2 n^3}
\end{equation*}
This is the radiation lifetime — the only quantity in our expression that accounts for how quickly the excited state spontaneously emits. It is a pure material/geometric constant.

Recall from \textbf{\nameref{subsec:deriving_lineshape}} that:
\begin{equation*}
    \alpha = \frac{N_A q^2}{4\varepsilon_0 m n C_0}\cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{equation*}

We now multiply $\alpha$ by the factor $\tau_r / \tau_r$ (which equals one, so nothing changes physically). This seemingly trivial step reorganises the expression to isolate the per-atom cross section:

\begin{gather*}
    \alpha = \frac{N_A q^2}{4\varepsilon_0 m n C_0} \cdot \frac{6\pi\varepsilon_0 n^2 m C_0^3}{q^2\omega_0^2 n^3 \tau_r} \cdot \tau_r \cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{gather*}

Carrying out the algebra — $q^2$, $m$, and $\varepsilon_0$ cancel between the two fractions. The $\tau_r$ outside and inside also cancel (that was the point of multiplying by $\tau_r/\tau_r$). Group the remaining powers of $C_0$ and $n$:
\begin{gather*}
    \alpha = N_A \cdot \frac{6\pi C_0^2}{4\omega_0^2 n^2 \tau_r} \cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{gather*}

Now we strictly simplify using the vacuum relationships $\omega_0 = 2\pi\nu_0$ (so $\omega_0^2 = 4\pi^2\nu_0^2$) and $C_0 = \lambda_0\nu_0$ (so $C_0^2 = \lambda_0^2\nu_0^2$, where $\lambda_0$ is the vacuum wavelength). Substitute these explicitly into the fraction:
\begin{gather*}
    \alpha = N_A \cdot \frac{6\pi \cdot \lambda_0^2 \nu_0^2}{4 \cdot 4\pi^2 \nu_0^2 \cdot n^2 \cdot \tau_r} \cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu) \notag\\
    = N_A \cdot \frac{3\lambda_0^2}{8\pi n^2 \tau_r} \cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{gather*}

Here $\lambda_0$ is the wavelength explicitly in vacuum. However, recalling that the wavelength scaled inside the material is $\lambda = \lambda_0/n$, we mathematically recognize that $\lambda_0^2/n^2 = \lambda^2$. The standard convention in most optoelectronics texts is exactly to substitute this directly to yield the compact classical form:

\begin{gather*}
    \alpha = N_A \cdot \frac{3\lambda^2}{8\pi\tau_r} \cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{gather*}

where $\lambda$ is strictly defined as the wavelength inside the host medium.

At this point we can see that $\alpha = N_A \sigma(\nu)$, which means:

\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    \sigma(\nu) = \frac{3\lambda^2}{8\pi\tau_r}\cdot g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

And therefore the absorption coefficient relates to the cross section simply as:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    \alpha = N_A\,\sigma(\nu)
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

\textbf{Commentary:} The absorption cross section $\sigma(\nu)$ inherits the frequency shape of the Lorentzian $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ directly, scaled by a constant that depends on the wavelength $\lambda$ and the radiation lifetime $\tau_r$. The longer $\tau_r$ is, the smaller the cross section — meaning atoms that hold onto their energy for a long time are actually \emph{less efficient} per-atom absorbers per unit frequency. This makes physical sense: a long $\tau_r$ means the atom is slow to interact with the field.

\textbf{Physical edge case — if $\tau_r \to \infty$:} If the radiation lifetime were infinite, $\sigma(\nu) \to 0$, meaning the atom does not absorb anything. This is consistent with the interpretation: $\tau_r \to \infty$ means the atom never radiates (never exchanges energy with the field), which is precisely the description of the ground state itself — an electron sitting stably in its ground state with constant energy, completely undisturbed, with no interaction whatsoever. This limiting case is not a contradiction; it is simply a description of absolute stability.

In short, the absorption cross section $\sigma(\nu)$ is the effective photon-capturing area per atom, shaped by the line shape function $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ and scaled by the radiation efficiency of the atom. Through $\alpha = N_A\sigma(\nu)$, it directly determines how fast the intensity decays as light passes through the material.

Now that we fully understand how a \emph{stationary} atom absorbs light and what the resulting line shape looks like, we must address a crucial physical reality: in gases, the atoms are \emph{not} stationary. They are moving. This changes the picture significantly.

\section{Doppler Broadening}

\subsection{Overview: Why Atom Motion Matters}

Doppler broadening is a phenomenon that is exclusive to \textbf{gases}. Up to this point, we have been talking about atoms sitting fixed in place while photons come in and interact with them. In a gas, however, the atoms themselves are in constant thermal motion — they zip around in all directions with a distribution of speeds.

This motion has a well-known consequence: the \textbf{Doppler effect}. When the source of a wave (in our case, the atom acting as an absorber) and the observer (in our case, the light wave) are in relative motion, the frequency perceived is shifted from the true emitted frequency. Let's define this carefully.

\subsection{The Doppler Frequency Shift}

Let:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\nu_0$: the true resonance frequency of the atom (what it would absorb if stationary).
    \item $V$: the velocity of the atom along the direction of light propagation.
    \item $\nu_{\text{res}}$: the frequency of light that the atom actually perceives (the ``observed'' frequency from the atom's reference frame).
\end{itemize}

The relativistic Doppler formula gives the observed frequency as seen by the moving atom:
\begin{equation*}
    \nu_{\text{res}} = \nu_0\left(1 - \frac{V}{c}\right)^{-1}\cdot\left(1-\frac{V^2}{c^2}\right)^{1/2}
\end{equation*}

For non-relativistic atoms ($V \ll c$), this simplifies. The observed frequency from the atom's perspective is:
\begin{equation*}
    \nu_{\text{res}} \approx \nu_0\left(1 + \frac{V}{c}\right) \qquad \text{(using Taylor approximation)}
\end{equation*}

\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    \nu_{\text{res}} = \nu_0\left(1 + \frac{V}{c}\right)
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\nu_{\text{res}}$: the frequency of light that causes maximum absorption as seen by this particular moving atom.
    \item $\nu_0$: the resonance frequency of the stationary atom.
    \item $V$: the velocity of the atom (positive if moving opposite to the light propagation direction, negative if moving in the same direction).
    \item $c$: the speed of light.
\end{itemize}

If $V$ is in the \textbf{same direction} as the propagating light: the atom is running away from the incoming wave, so from the atom's perspective the photons are coming in slower, i.e., at a lower frequency — $\nu_{\text{res}} < \nu_0$.

If $V$ is in the \textbf{opposite direction} to the propagating light: the atom is running toward the incoming wave, so from the atom's perspective the photons appear blue-shifted — $\nu_{\text{res}} > \nu_0$.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/doppler_effect_gases.jpg}
    \caption{The Doppler Effect in Gases: Moving atoms perceive the incoming waves at shifted frequencies depending on whether they run away from (red-shift) or toward (blue-shift) the photon source.}
    \label{fig:ch1_doppler_effect}
\end{figure}

In short, a stationary atom absorbs maximally at $\nu_0$. A moving atom absorbs maximally at the Doppler-shifted resonance frequency $\nu_{\text{res}} = \nu_0(1+V/c)$, which depends on its velocity.

\subsection{The Line Shape Function for a Moving Atom}

As we derived in \textbf{\nameref{subsec:deriving_lineshape}}, the line shape function $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ for a stationary atom (at $V=0$) is:
\begin{equation*}
    g_{\nu_0}(\nu) = \frac{\Delta\nu/2\pi}{\left(\dfrac{\Delta\nu}{2}\right)^2 + (\nu-\nu_0)^2} \qquad \text{(from source at } V=0\text{)}
\end{equation*}

For a moving atom with velocity $V$, the resonance is shifted to $\nu_{\text{res}} = \nu_0(1+V/c)$, so the line shape function shifts accordingly:
\begin{equation*}
    g_{\nu_{\text{res}}}(\nu) = \frac{\Delta\nu/2\pi}{\left(\dfrac{\Delta\nu}{2}\right)^2 + \left(\nu - \nu_0\left[1+\dfrac{V}{c}\right]\right)^2} \qquad \text{(from source at } V\neq 0\text{)}
\end{equation*}

We can write this more compactly by recognising the argument shift. If we define $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ as the base function, then the Doppler-shifted version is simply:
\begin{equation*}
    g_{\nu_{\text{res}}}(\nu) = g_{\nu_0}\!\left(\nu - \nu_0\frac{V}{c}\right)
\end{equation*}

This is the line shape function for a \emph{single} atom moving at velocity $V$. The problem is: in a real gas, every atom has a different velocity. We now need to account for all of them together.

\subsection{The Velocity Distribution of Gas Atoms}

As we said, the velocity distribution of atoms inside a gas follows a \textbf{Gaussian (Maxwell-Boltzmann) distribution}. The probability density function (PDF) of the atomic velocities is:

\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    \text{PDF} = f_V(V) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma_V}\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{V^2}{2\sigma_V^2}\right)
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

with the normalisation condition:
\begin{equation*}
    \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_V(V)\,dV = 1
\end{equation*}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $V$: the velocity of a single atom.
    \item $\sigma_V$: the standard deviation of the velocity distribution (characterises the spread of speeds).
    \item $f_V(V)$: the probability that an atom has velocity $V$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/velocity_distribution.jpg}
    \caption{Velocity Distribution: The Maxwell-Boltzmann Gaussian probability density governing the speeds of the atoms in a thermal gas.}
    \label{fig:ch1_velocity_pdf}
\end{figure}

Two limiting cases to keep in mind:
\begin{itemize}
    \item If $\sigma_V \to 0$: all atoms have nearly the same velocity (narrow spread) — the PDF collapses to an impulse: $f_V(V) \to \delta(V)$.
    \item If $\sigma_V \to \infty$: atoms have velocities spread over a huge range — the PDF becomes very flat and approaches zero everywhere.
\end{itemize}
The maximum of $f_V(V)$ is at $V=0$, meaning the most probable velocity is zero (i.e., most atoms are moving slowly in any given direction).

\subsection{The Average Line Shape Function}

Now we have two things in motion simultaneously: the photons (described by the line shape function $g_{\nu_{\text{res}}}(\nu)$ for each atom) and the atoms (described by the velocity PDF $f_V(V)$). To find the \emph{effective} absorption behaviour seen by the light wave when it passes through the gas, we need to take the average over all possible atomic velocities.

The average line shape function is:
\begin{equation*}
    \bar{g}(\nu) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g_{\nu_{\text{res}}}(\nu)\, f_V(V)\, dV
\end{equation*}

Substituting explicitly:
\begin{equation}
    \bar{g}(\nu) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{\Delta\nu/2\pi}{\left(\dfrac{\Delta\nu}{2}\right)^2 + \left(\nu - \nu_0\!\left[1+\dfrac{V}{c}\right]\right)^2} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma_V}\exp\!\left(-\frac{V^2}{2\sigma_V^2}\right) dV
    \tag{I}
\end{equation}

This integral is a convolution of a Lorentzian (from the line shape function) and a Gaussian (from the velocity PDF). The result of such a convolution is called a \textbf{Voigt profile} in the general case. However, in two extreme limits, it simplifies beautifully to either a pure Lorentzian or a pure Gaussian. These two limits correspond to the two types of broadening we now discuss.

\subsection{Two Types of Doppler Broadening}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Homogeneous Broadening (Low Doppler Effect):}

    This occurs when the gas is \textbf{homogeneous} — meaning all atoms are essentially identical and have the same resonance frequency $\nu_0$. In this case, the velocity spread $\sigma_V$ is very small (the atoms are nearly at rest relative to each other), so the Gaussian PDF is extremely narrow.

    Mathematically, as $\sigma_V \to 0$, the probability distribution collapses:
    \begin{equation*}
        f_V(V) \to \delta(V)
    \end{equation*}
    Substituting this into integral \textup{(I)}:
    \begin{equation*}
        \bar{g}(\nu) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g_{\nu_0}\!\left(\nu - \nu_0\frac{V}{c}\right) \delta(V)\,dV = g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
    \end{equation*}

    The sifting property of the delta function completely kills everything except exactly the $V=0$ term. The average line shape function therefore simplifies flawlessly to our original Lorentzian:

    \begin{equation*}
        \bar{g}(\nu) = g_{\nu_0}(\nu) \qquad \text{(Lorentzian — homogeneous limit)}
    \end{equation*}

    This type of broadening is frequently called \textbf{lifetime/collision broadening}, because its width ($\Delta\nu = 1/2\pi\tau$) sits purely dictated by the lifetime $\tau$ of the state, not the atom's movement.

    \item \textbf{Inhomogeneous Broadening (High Doppler Effect):}

    This occurs when the gas is \textbf{inhomogeneous} — meaning the atoms are moving rapidly in entirely random directions with a wide spread of velocities ($\sigma_V \to \infty$).

    In this high-velocity spread limit, the line width $\Delta\nu$ of each individual atom is \emph{tiny} compared to the vast Doppler shifts sweeping across the full Gaussian. From the Gaussian's sweeping perspective, each tiny Lorentzian is effectively an infinitely sharp vertical impulse function:
    \begin{equation*}
        \lim_{\Delta\nu \to 0} g_{\nu_{\text{res}}}(\nu) = \delta\!\left(\nu - \nu_0\left[1+\frac{V}{c}\right]\right) = \delta\!\left(\nu - \nu_0 - \nu_0\frac{V}{c}\right)
    \end{equation*}

    To evaluate an integral containing this delta function over $dV$, we must formally apply the scaling property of the delta function $\delta(Ax-B) = \frac{1}{|A|}\delta(x - B/A)$ to properly extract out $V$. Here, $A = -\nu_0/c$. So we rewrite the delta effectively as:
    \begin{equation*}
        \delta\!\left(\left(-\frac{\nu_0}{c}\right)V + (\nu-\nu_0) \right) = \frac{1}{\left|-\dfrac{\nu_0}{c}\right|}\delta\!\left(V - \frac{c(\nu-\nu_0)}{\nu_0}\right) = \frac{c}{\nu_0}\delta\!\left(V - \frac{c(\nu-\nu_0)}{\nu_0}\right)
    \end{equation*}

    Now substitute this perfectly scaled delta function into our average integral \textup{(I)}:
    \begin{equation*}
        \bar{g}(\nu) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_V(V)\cdot \left[ \frac{c}{\nu_0}\delta\!\left(V - \frac{c(\nu-\nu_0)}{\nu_0}\right) \right] dV
    \end{equation*}

    Applying the sifting property, the integral fires exactly when $V = \dfrac{c(\nu-\nu_0)}{\nu_0}$. We substitute and pull out the constant:
    \begin{equation*}
        \bar{g}(\nu) = \frac{c}{\nu_0} \cdot f_V\!\left(\frac{c(\nu-\nu_0)}{\nu_0}\right)
    \end{equation*}

    Now we fully explicitly substitute the Gaussian velocity PDF $f_V(V)$:
    \begin{equation*}
        \bar{g}(\nu) = \frac{c}{\nu_0} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma_V}\exp\!\left(-\frac{\left[\frac{c(\nu-\nu_0)}{\nu_0}\right]^2}{2\sigma_V^2}\right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\left(\frac{\nu_0}{c}\sigma_V\right)} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(\nu-\nu_0)^2}{2\left(\frac{\nu_0}{c}\sigma_V\right)^2}\right)
    \end{equation*}

    Let's make a beautiful grouping substitution. Define $\sigma_0 = \frac{\nu_0}{c}\sigma_V$. Our enormous equation reduces perfectly:
    \begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
        \bar{g}(\nu) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma_0}\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{(\nu-\nu_0)^2}{2\sigma_0^2}\right) \qquad \text{(Gaussian — inhomogeneous limit)}
    \end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

    Note carefully: $\sigma_0$ correctly takes units of frequency. The ratio $\nu_0/c$ flawlessly converted the velocity spread ($\sigma_V$) into an equivalent frequency spread. Because of the sheer multitude of differently Doppler-shifted atoms, their tiny Lorentzians mathematically stacked up to perfectly trace a pure broad Gaussian envelope!

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 1/inhomogeneous_broadening.jpg}
    \caption{Inhomogeneous Broadening: A large gas ensemble produces countless thinly shifted Doppler Lorentzians, fundamentally tracing out the macroscopic Gaussian profile.}
    \label{fig:ch1_inhomogeneous_limit}
\end{figure}
\end{enumerate}

This precise mechanism defines \textbf{Doppler broadening}.
In short, Doppler broadening turns the microscopic Lorentzian line shape into a macroscopic Gaussian line shape when gas atoms have a wide thermal velocity distribution. The average absorption function $\bar{g}(\nu)$ becomes uniquely Gaussian, scaled directly by the standard deviation of velocity ($\sigma_0 = (\nu_0/c)\sigma_V$).

\subsection{Summary: Updating the Absorption Cross Section}

Now that we know the line shape is modified by Doppler broadening in a gas, the absorption cross section must be updated accordingly. We simply replace $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ with the average line shape function $\bar{g}(\nu)$:
\begin{equation*}
    \bar{\sigma}(\nu) = \frac{3\lambda^2}{8\pi\tau_r}\cdot\bar{g}(\nu)
\end{equation*}

where $\bar{g}(\nu)$ is the appropriate average line shape function — Lorentzian for homogeneous matter, Gaussian for inhomogeneous matter, and in general the full Voigt convolution integral from above.

The absorption coefficient then becomes:
\begin{equation*}
    \alpha = N_A\,\bar{\sigma}(\nu)
\end{equation*}

The same structure holds; only the shape of the line has changed. The absorption cross section with Gaussian distribution describes the actual frequency-resolved absorption in a real gas much more accurately than the simple Lorentzian alone.

\section{End of the Classical Model and Looking Ahead}

We have now completed everything we wanted to study using the classical model. Let us briefly summarise what we found and why it matters:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The \textbf{line shape function} $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ is a Lorentzian distribution in frequency, derived directly from the absorption coefficient $\alpha$ of the classical model. It tells us the frequency-resolved probability of absorption, and it is normalised to unity.
    \item The \textbf{absorption cross section} $\sigma(\nu)$ quantifies the effective photon-catching area of a single atom. Through $\alpha = N_A\sigma(\nu)$, it connects microscopic atomic physics to the macroscopic intensity decay.
    \item \textbf{Doppler broadening} modifies the line shape function for moving atoms in a gas. In the homogeneous limit, the result is a Lorentzian (lifetime/collision broadening). In the inhomogeneous limit, the result is a Gaussian (Doppler-dominated broadening). The average line shape function $\bar{g}(\nu)$ is in general the convolution of both.
\end{enumerate}

However, we must not forget what the classical model fundamentally \emph{cannot} do: it is incapable of describing optical amplification. The absorption coefficient $\alpha$ is always positive in the classical picture — the medium always attenuates light, never amplifies it. There is no mechanism in this model for population inversion or stimulated emission gain.

This is precisely why we will now move on to the next level of description. The models coming ahead — starting with Einstein's model — will show us how to flip $\alpha$ to a negative value (gain instead of loss), leading ultimately to a complete understanding of how a laser actually works. All the phenomena we studied classically — the line shape, the cross section, the broadening — will reappear in those more correct models, but now with the added richness that allows both absorption \emph{and} amplification.
