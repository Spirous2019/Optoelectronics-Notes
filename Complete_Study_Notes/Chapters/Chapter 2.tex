\section{The Einstein Model}

Now let's talk about why we need a new model and what it actually changes. We finished the classical model and established that it cannot describe amplification. We now need a different framework to describe the interaction between light and matter — one that can. We start with the \textbf{Einstein model}.

The two most fundamental changes from the classical picture are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Matter is now described by discrete energy levels.} The material is no longer a continuous medium with a smoothly varying susceptibility. Instead, each atom (or, in a semiconductor, each electron-hole pair) exists at a specific energy level $E_i$, and transitions between levels are the only way energy is exchanged.
    \item \textbf{Light is now described by photons, not waves.} We are working fully in the quantum picture now. A photon carries energy $E = h\nu$ and interacts with the atom as a discrete packet of energy, not as a continuous oscillating field.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 2/classical_vs_quantum_medium.jpg}
    \caption{Classical vs. Quantum Material: The classical continuous medium compared to the discrete quantum energy states of the Einstein model.}
    \label{fig:ch2_classical_vs_quantum}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Energy Level Populations}

In the Einstein model, each energy level $E_i$ is characterised by a number $N_i$, defined as:
\begin{quote}
    $N_i$: the number of atoms per unit volume occupying energy level $E_i$.
\end{quote}
This is called the \textbf{atom density} (or \textbf{population density}) of level $i$. Note that this is different from the classical picture: recall that in the classical model we had a single quantity $N_a$ representing the total number of atoms per unit volume for the entire material. Here, $N_a$ is split — each energy level has its own population $N_i$, and the total number of atoms is $N_a = \sum_i N_i$.

We will work with a \textbf{two-level system}, which is the simplest non-trivial case. We have:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $E_1$, $N_1$: the lower energy level and its population.
    \item $E_2$, $N_2$: the upper energy level and its population, with $E_2 > E_1$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 2/two_level_system.jpg}
    \caption{The Two-Level System: Depicting the lower and upper energy levels ($E_1$ and $E_2$), their respective populations ($N_1$ and $N_2$), and the required transition photon energy.}
    \label{fig:ch2_two_level_system}
\end{figure}

The energy difference between the two levels determines the photon frequency at which the atom can interact with light:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{photon}} = E_2 - E_1 = h\nu
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $h$: Planck's constant.
    \item $\nu$: the frequency of the photon involved in the transition.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{A Brief Note on Semiconductors vs.\ Gases}

We need to be careful about the physical picture depending on the material:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item In a \textbf{semiconductor}: the entities that move between energy levels are \emph{electrons}. An electron in the valence band can absorb a photon and jump to the conduction band. The valence band is mostly full of electrons (many electrons, few holes), while the conduction band is mostly empty (few electrons, many holes). Because there are vacant states in the conduction band, electrons from the valence band can be promoted into them.
    \item In a \textbf{gas}: the entities that move are entire \emph{atoms}, transitioning from one energy level to another. The atom as a whole absorbs or emits a photon and changes its internal energy state.
\end{enumerate}

Since we are applying the Einstein model to gases first (as we said), whenever we say ``an atom moves from $E_1$ to $E_2$,'' we mean the atom's internal energy state changes — not that the atom physically moves in space.

\subsection{The Direct vs.\ Indirect Band Gap — A Brief Clarification}

Before continuing, let us note one important distinction for semiconductors that will matter later. In a \textbf{direct band gap semiconductor}, an electron and a hole undergoing recombination have the same momentum (the same wave vector $k$), meaning they sit directly above one another in the $E$-$k$ diagram. When they recombine, all the energy difference goes into a photon — this is efficient optical emission.

In an \textbf{indirect band gap semiconductor}, the electron and hole do not have the same momentum. Before they can recombine, the momentum mismatch must first be resolved — this happens by emitting a \textbf{phonon} (a quantised lattice vibration that carries the excess momentum). Only after the momentum is equalised can the energy difference be released as a photon. This extra phonon step makes indirect-gap semiconductors far less efficient light emitters, which is why materials like Silicon (indirect gap) are not used for lasers while Gallium Arsenide (direct gap) is.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering

    \caption{Direct vs. Indirect Band Gap: Comparing the purely vertical transition of a direct gap emitter to the phonon-assisted diagonal transition of an indirect gap emitter.}
    \label{fig:ch2_direct_vs_indirect}
\end{figure}

Now, let's return to the main thread and look at the three fundamental processes by which atoms in the Einstein model interact with light.

\section{The Three Fundamental Processes}

In the Einstein model, the interaction between light and a two-level material can be described by \textbf{exactly three processes}. All three are probabilistic — none of them is certain to happen, but each has a well-defined rate. We study them one by one.

% ----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Process 1: Spontaneous Emission}
% ----------------------------------------------------------

Spontaneous emission is the process by which an atom that finds itself in the upper energy level $E_2$ relaxes back down to $E_1$ on its own, without any external trigger, by emitting a photon.

More precisely: suppose an atom is sitting in $E_2$. This is not its natural, preferred state — the atom's ground state is $E_1$. Regardless of how it got to $E_2$ (we don't care about the past), the atom will spontaneously try to shed the extra energy and return to $E_1$. It does so by emitting a photon whose energy is exactly equal to the energy difference:
\begin{equation*}
    E_{\text{photon}} = E_2 - E_1 = h\nu
\end{equation*}

This process is called \emph{spontaneous} because it happens entirely on the atom's own initiative — no external field, no incident photon is required. The atom simply falls from $E_2$ to $E_1$ and a photon pops out.

\textbf{Key property of the emitted photon:} The photon produced by spontaneous emission has a completely \textbf{random direction, random polarisation, and random phase}. It is essentially noise from the perspective of building a coherent beam. However — and this is a point to keep in the back of your mind — spontaneous emission is the \emph{seed} that starts laser action, even though the laser itself runs on stimulated emission. We will understand exactly why when we get to the laser structure. For now, just note this fact.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 2/spontaneous_emission.jpg}
    \caption{Spontaneous Emission: An atom in the excited state ($E_2$) decays spontaneously to the ground state ($E_1$), emitting a photon with random phase and direction.}
    \label{fig:ch2_spontaneous_emission}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Mathematical representation:} To describe this process mathematically, we track the rate of change of the upper-level population $N_2$ with time. Since spontaneous emission takes atoms \emph{out of} $E_2$, it causes $N_2$ to decrease. The rate of decrease is proportional to $N_2$ itself (more atoms in $E_2$ means more spontaneous emission events per second), and it is governed by the \textbf{spontaneous emission lifetime} $\tau_{sp}$.

The inverse of $\tau_{sp}$ is the probability per unit time that a single atom will spontaneously leave $E_2$. Multiplying by the total number of atoms in $E_2$ gives the total rate:

\begin{equation*}
    \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{sp} = -A\,N_2
\end{equation*}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $N_2$: the population density of the upper energy level $E_2$ (atoms per unit volume currently in $E_2$).
    \item $A$: the \textbf{Einstein A coefficient}, the spontaneous emission rate per atom (units: $\text{s}^{-1}$).
    \item The negative sign indicates that $N_2$ is decreasing because atoms are leaving $E_2$.
\end{itemize}

The Einstein A coefficient is formally defined as the inverse of the lifetime:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    A = \frac{1}{\tau_{sp}}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\tau_{sp}$: the \textbf{spontaneous emission lifetime} — the average time an atom spends in $E_2$ before spontaneously decaying.
\end{itemize}

This is the probability that a single atom transitions from $E_2$ to $E_1$ per unit time via spontaneous emission. The total rate at which the $E_2$ population decreases due to spontaneous emission is $A\,N_2$.

In short, spontaneous emission drains the upper-level population at a rate proportional to $N_2$, with a proportionality constant $A = 1/\tau_{sp}$ that tells us how quickly the atom typically decays on its own.

% ----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Process 2: Absorption}
% ----------------------------------------------------------

Absorption is the process by which an atom sitting in its ground state $E_1$ absorbs an incoming photon and gets promoted to the upper level $E_2$, but only if the photon's energy exactly matches the energy gap:
\begin{equation*}
    E_{\text{photon}} = E_2 - E_1 = h\nu
\end{equation*}

In other words, the photon must arrive at the \textbf{resonance frequency} of the transition. If its frequency is right, the atom absorbs the photon's energy and jumps up to $E_2$. What happens to the atom after that is irrelevant to this particular process — it might subsequently undergo spontaneous emission, or lose energy through collisions, or do something else entirely. We describe absorption in isolation.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 2/photon_absorption.jpg}
    \caption{Absorption: An incoming photon interacts with an atom in the ground state ($E_1$), transferring its energy to promote the atom to the excited state ($E_2$).}
    \label{fig:ch2_absorption}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Mathematical representation:} Absorption takes atoms \emph{into} $E_2$ from $E_1$, so it causes $N_2$ to increase (positive contribution). The rate of absorption depends on two things:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item How many atoms are available in $E_1$ to be absorbed (i.e., $N_1$).
    \item How many photons are available at the right frequency to trigger the absorption, i.e., the energy spectral density $\mathcal{E}(\nu)$ of the incident light.
\end{enumerate}

Unlike spontaneous emission, absorption is not self-driven — it requires an incoming photon. That is why the rate depends on $\mathcal{E}(\nu)$: the denser the photon field at the resonance frequency, the faster atoms get promoted. We introduce a new proportionality constant $B_{12}$, the \textbf{Einstein $B_{12}$ coefficient}, where the subscript ``12'' indicates the direction of the transition (from level 1 to level 2):

\begin{equation*}
    \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{abs} = B_{12}\,\mathcal{E}(\nu)\,N_1
\end{equation*}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $N_1$: the population density of the lower level $E_1$ (atoms per unit volume currently in $E_1$, available to be excited).
    \item $B_{12}$: the \textbf{Einstein $B_{12}$ coefficient} — the absorption rate per atom per unit energy spectral density (units: $\text{m}^3/(\text{J}\cdot\text{s}^2)$, or equivalently volume per energy per time).
    \item $\mathcal{E}(\nu)$: the \textbf{energy spectral density} of the incident radiation, defined as the energy per unit volume per unit frequency (units: $\text{J}/(\text{m}^3 \cdot \text{Hz})$). This is also related to black-body radiation, as we will see shortly.
\end{itemize}

The subscript on $B_{12}$ encodes the direction: the ``1'' means we start at level 1, the ``2'' means we end at level 2. We will see shortly that there is also a $B_{21}$ for the reverse process.

In short, absorption promotes atoms from $E_1$ to $E_2$ at a rate $B_{12}\,\mathcal{E}(\nu)\,N_1$, driven by the incident photon field.

% ----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Process 3: Stimulated Emission}
% ----------------------------------------------------------

Stimulated emission is the most interesting process, and the one that makes lasers possible. Here, an incoming photon interacts with an atom that is \emph{already sitting in the upper level $E_2$} (already in an excited state). The photon \emph{stimulates} the atom to fall down from $E_2$ to $E_1$, releasing its stored energy in the form of a \emph{second photon}.

The remarkable thing is the nature of the emitted photon: it is an \textbf{exact clone} of the incident photon. It has the same frequency, the same phase, the same polarisation, and the same direction of propagation. This is fundamentally different from spontaneous emission, where the emitted photon has a random direction and phase. Stimulated emission produces coherent, phase-locked copies of the incoming photon — this is the physical mechanism behind laser amplification.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 2/stimulated_emission.jpg}
    \caption{Stimulated Emission: An incoming photon triggers an excited atom ($E_2$) to decay. Crucially, the process yields two perfectly identical, coherent photons.}
    \label{fig:ch2_stimulated_emission}
\end{figure}

\begin{equation*}
    E_2 - E_1 = \Delta E = h\nu
\end{equation*}

\textbf{Alternative viewpoint — time reversibility:} There is an elegant way to think about stimulated emission as the time-reverse of absorption. Recall that Maxwell's equations satisfy time-reversal symmetry: if you replace $t \to -t$ in the equations, they remain valid. In absorption, one incident photon hits an atom in $E_1$, the photon is absorbed, and the atom jumps up to $E_2$. If we time-reverse this picture — reversing all arrows, swapping input and output — we get: an excited atom in $E_2$ emits one photon and drops to $E_1$, yielding one outgoing photon. But that describes spontaneous emission. For stimulated emission, the correct time-reversal partner involves two photons: in absorption, one photon arrives and is destroyed; in stimulated emission, one photon arrives and \emph{two} photons leave (the triggering photon plus the newly emitted clone). The time-reversal symmetry thus connects an absorption event (one photon in, zero photons out) to a stimulated emission event (one photon in, two photons out).

\textbf{Mathematical representation:} Stimulated emission takes atoms \emph{out of} $E_2$ (the atom falls down to $E_1$), so it decreases $N_2$ — hence the negative sign. The rate depends on the number of atoms available in $E_2$ (i.e., $N_2$) and on the photon field energy density $\mathcal{E}(\nu)$, since an incident photon is required to trigger the process. The proportionality constant is $B_{21}$, the \textbf{Einstein $B_{21}$ coefficient}:

\begin{equation*}
    \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{stim} = -B_{21}\,\mathcal{E}(\nu)\,N_2
\end{equation*}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $N_2$: the population density in the upper level (atoms available to be stimulated down).
    \item $B_{21}$: the \textbf{Einstein $B_{21}$ coefficient} — the stimulated emission rate per atom per unit energy spectral density. The subscript ``21'' means the atom moves from level 2 down to level 1.
    \item $\mathcal{E}(\nu)$: the energy spectral density of the incident field (same as in absorption).
    \item The negative sign: $N_2$ is decreasing as atoms leave $E_2$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{keyinsight}[Three Processes and Their Signs]
It is worth pausing here to collect the signs and confirm they are all physically consistent. Spontaneous emission removes atoms from $E_2$ on its own ($-AN_2$). Absorption adds atoms to $E_2$ by taking them from $E_1$ ($+B_{12}\mathcal{E}N_1$). Stimulated emission removes atoms from $E_2$ with the help of an incident photon ($-B_{21}\mathcal{E}N_2$). All three are rates of change of $N_2$ and all three signs make physical sense.
\end{keyinsight}

\subsection{An Important Note on Probability}

None of these three processes is guaranteed to happen. Each one occurs with a certain probability. There are many other possible processes that can occur in a semiconductor or a gas — for example, an excited electron in the conduction band can give its extra energy to another electron already sitting in the conduction band (Auger process), or it can lose energy through phonon interactions. We acknowledge that these other processes exist. However, we study only the three processes above because they are the ones relevant to our goal of building a laser. All other processes come in conjugate pairs (each process has a reverse process that balances it), so they cancel out of the rate equation at thermal equilibrium.

\section{Thermal Equilibrium and Deriving the Einstein Coefficients}

\subsection{Setting Up the Thermal Equilibrium Condition}

Now let's talk about thermal equilibrium. A material is said to be at \textbf{thermal equilibrium} when the population of each energy level stays constant in time — atoms may continuously transition up and down, but the net number in each level does not change. In semiconductor language, this is equivalent to saying that the generation rate equals the recombination rate everywhere.

Mathematically, thermal equilibrium means:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{dN_2}{dt} = 0
\end{equation*}

The total rate of change of $N_2$ is the sum of contributions from all three processes:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{dN_2}{dt} = \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{sp} + \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{abs} + \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{stim} = 0
\end{equation*}

Substituting the expressions we derived for each process:
\begin{equation*}
    -A\,N_2 + B_{12}\,\mathcal{E}(\nu)\,N_1 - B_{21}\,\mathcal{E}(\nu)\,N_2 = 0
\end{equation*}

Collecting the $\mathcal{E}(\nu)$ terms on one side:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{E}(\nu)\left[B_{12}\,N_1 - B_{21}\,N_2\right] = A\,N_2
\end{equation*}

Solving for $\mathcal{E}(\nu)$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{E}(\nu) = \frac{A\,N_2}{B_{12}\,N_1 - B_{21}\,N_2}
    \tag{I}
\end{equation}

In short, thermal equilibrium requires the spontaneous and stimulated downward flows to perfectly balance the upward absorption flow, yielding an exact expression (I) for the necessary equilibrium photon field $\mathcal{E}(\nu)$.

This gives us $\mathcal{E}(\nu)$ as a function of the populations and the Einstein coefficients. But we don't know the values of $B_{12}$ and $B_{21}$ yet, and we don't know the ratio $N_1/N_2$ in equilibrium without additional physics. We now bring in two separate pieces of known physics to evaluate both.

\subsection{The Boltzmann Population Ratio}

Recall from modern physics that the \textbf{Boltzmann distribution} describes how atoms (or electrons, in the case of a semiconductor) are distributed among energy levels at thermal equilibrium. The probability of finding an atom at energy $E$ is proportional to $e^{-E/(k_B T)}$, where $k_B$ is Boltzmann's constant and $T$ is the absolute temperature.

For our two-level system, this gives:
\begin{equation*}
    N_1 \approx e^{-E_1/(k_B T)}, \qquad N_2 \approx e^{-E_2/(k_B T)}
\end{equation*}

Note: we always assume in the Boltzmann approximation that $N \approx e^{-E/(k_B T)}$; there are pre-exponential factors (density of states, degeneracy) that we ignore in this simplified treatment.

Taking the ratio:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{N_1}{N_2} = \frac{e^{-E_1/(k_B T)}}{e^{-E_2/(k_B T)}} = e^{-(E_1 - E_2)/(k_B T)} = e^{(E_2 - E_1)/(k_B T)}
\end{equation*}

Since $E_2 - E_1 = h\nu$:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{N_1}{N_2} = e^{h\nu/(k_B T)}
    \tag{II}
\end{equation}

This confirms what we expect: since $E_2 > E_1$, we have $h\nu > 0$, so $N_1/N_2 > 1$, meaning $N_1 > N_2$ always at thermal equilibrium. At thermal equilibrium, there are always more atoms in the lower level than in the upper level — this is a fundamental constraint that the laser must overcome.

Note that at room temperature, $k_B T \approx 26\,\text{meV}$, which is a useful value to remember for quick estimates.

In short, the Boltzmann statistical reality proves that without external interference, populations exponentially favour the lower energy state; establishing an absolute ceiling $N_1 \gg N_2$ at room temperature.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 2/boltzmann_distribution.jpg}
    \caption{Boltzmann Population Distribution: The exponential decay curve illustrates why the lower energy state inherently sustains a vastly larger population than the upper state in thermal equilibrium.}
    \label{fig:ch2_boltzmann_distribution}
\end{figure}

\textbf{An important restriction:} The Boltzmann distribution applies to \textbf{non-degenerate semiconductors} (where the Fermi level lies at least $3k_BT$ away from the nearest band edge, sitting comfortably inside the bandgap) and to gases. It does \emph{not} apply to \textbf{degenerate semiconductors} — those with very heavy doping, where the Fermi level has been pushed inside one of the bands. In that regime, the material starts to behave more like a metal, and the strict Fermi-Dirac distribution must be used instead of Boltzmann.

\subsection{Substituting the Boltzmann Ratio into \texorpdfstring{$\mathcal{E}(\nu)$}{E(nu)}}

We now substitute ratio \textup{(II)} into expression \textup{(I)}. Start from expression \textup{(I)}:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{E}(\nu) = \frac{A\,N_2}{B_{12}\,N_1 - B_{21}\,N_2}
\end{equation*}

Divide numerator and denominator by $N_2$:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{E}(\nu) = \frac{A}{B_{12}\,\dfrac{N_1}{N_2} - B_{21}}
\end{equation*}

We previously found that $\dfrac{N_1}{N_2} = e^{h\nu/(k_BT)}$. We substitute this absolute ratio into the denominator explicitly:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{E}(\nu) = \frac{A}{B_{12}\left(e^{h\nu/(k_BT)}\right) - B_{21}}
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{E}(\nu) = \frac{A}{B_{12}\,e^{h\nu/(k_BT)} - B_{21}}
    \tag{III}
\end{equation}

This is the energy spectral density implied by the Einstein model at thermal equilibrium. At this point we can see that we still have two unknowns: $B_{12}$ and $B_{21}$ (and we know $A = 1/\tau_{sp}$, but $\tau_{sp}$ itself depends on the atomic system). We need another equation. We get it by comparing with a known result.

\subsection{Comparing with Black-Body Radiation}

The \textbf{energy spectral density of black-body radiation} is a well-known result from physics:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{E}_{BB}(\nu) = \frac{8\pi n^3 h\nu^3}{C_0^3} \cdot \frac{1}{e^{h\nu/(k_BT)} - 1}
\end{equation*}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $n$: the refractive index of the medium.
    \item $h$: Planck's constant.
    \item $\nu$: the frequency of the radiation.
    \item $C_0$: the speed of light in vacuum.
    \item $k_B$: Boltzmann's constant.
    \item $T$: the absolute temperature.
    \item Note: since $C_0 = \lambda_0\nu$ (where $\lambda_0$ is the free-space wavelength), we can also write $\lambda_0 = C_0/\nu$ and $\lambda = \lambda_0/n$ is the wavelength inside the medium with refractive index $n$.
\end{itemize}

The black-body formula describes the energy distribution of radiation in a cavity at thermal equilibrium — which is exactly the same physical situation we have been computing with the Einstein model. Therefore, the $\mathcal{E}(\nu)$ from expression \textup{(III)} must equal $\mathcal{E}_{BB}(\nu)$:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{A}{B_{12}\,e^{h\nu/(k_BT)} - B_{21}} = \frac{8\pi n^3 h\nu^3}{C_0^3} \cdot \frac{1}{e^{h\nu/(k_BT)} - 1}
\end{equation*}

For these two expressions to be equal for all temperatures $T$ (since the equation must hold at every temperature), we match the structure term by term. The denominator on the left, $B_{12}\,e^{h\nu/(k_BT)} - B_{21}$, must be proportional to $e^{h\nu/(k_BT)} - 1$. This requires:
\begin{equation*}
    B_{12} = B_{21} \equiv B
\end{equation*}

This is a remarkable result: \textbf{the absorption coefficient equals the stimulated emission coefficient}. There is a deep symmetry between the two processes — they are each other's time-reversal, as we noted earlier, and this symmetry forces their Einstein coefficients to be equal.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 2/einstein_coefficients_symmetry.jpg}
    \caption{Einstein Coefficients Symmetry: Highlighting the fundamental bidirectional equivalence ($B_{12} = B_{21}$) between the rates of upward absorption and downward stimulated emission.}
    \label{fig:ch2_einstein_symmetry}
\end{figure}

With $B_{12} = B_{21} = B$, equation \textup{(III)} simplifies to:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{E}(\nu) = \frac{A}{B\left(e^{h\nu/(k_BT)} - 1\right)}
\end{equation*}

Comparing with the black-body formula:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{A}{B\left(e^{h\nu/(k_BT)} - 1\right)} = \frac{8\pi n^3 h\nu^3}{C_0^3\left(e^{h\nu/(k_BT)} - 1\right)}
\end{equation*}

The denominators are identical, so we equate the numerators:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{A}{B} = \frac{8\pi n^3 h\nu^3}{C_0^3}
\end{equation*}

Using $\lambda_0 = C_0/\nu$ and $C_0 = \lambda_0\,\nu$, so $C_0^3 = \lambda_0^3\,\nu^3$, and recalling that $A = 1/\tau_{sp}$:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{1}{B\,\tau_{sp}} = \frac{8\pi n^3 h\nu^3}{C_0^3} = \frac{8\pi n^3 h\nu^3}{\lambda_0^3\nu^3} = \frac{8\pi n^3 h}{\lambda_0^3}
\end{equation*}

Now we substitute the in-medium wavelength relation $\lambda_0 = n\lambda$, so $\lambda_0^3 = n^3\lambda^3$. The $n^3$ cancels completely:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{1}{B\,\tau_{sp}} = \frac{8\pi n^3 h}{n^3\lambda^3} = \frac{8\pi h}{\lambda^3}
\end{equation*}

Inverting and dividing by $\tau_{sp}$ fully isolates $B$:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    B = B_{12} = B_{21} = \frac{\lambda^3}{8\pi h\,\tau_{sp}}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}
where $\lambda = \lambda_0/n$ is the \textbf{in-medium wavelength}. Notice that $n$ has vanished entirely from the expression — because $\lambda_0^3$ contains an $n^3$ that exactly cancels the $n^3$ from the density of states in the black-body formula. This is the same reason the cross-section will come out clean.

And the spontaneous emission rate is determined by the same chain:
\begin{equation*}
    A = \frac{1}{\tau_{sp}} = \frac{8\pi h}{\lambda^3}\,B
\end{equation*}

\textbf{Commentary:} These are the Einstein coefficients. The A coefficient governs how quickly atoms spontaneously release energy from $E_2$; the B coefficient (which is the same for absorption and stimulated emission) governs how strongly the atom responds to an external photon field. Notably, $B_{12} = B_{21}$ means that at a fundamental level, an atom is equally likely to absorb a photon (going up) as it is to emit a stimulated photon (going down), for the same photon density. The asymmetry between absorption-dominated and gain-dominated behaviour comes entirely from the populations $N_1$ and $N_2$, not from any asymmetry in the coefficients themselves.

In short, the thermal equilibrium condition combined with the known black-body radiation spectrum forces us to the conclusion that $B_{12} = B_{21} = B = \lambda^3 / (8\pi h\,\tau_{sp})$, where $\lambda$ is the in-medium wavelength, and gives us the Einstein A coefficient as $A = 1/\tau_{sp}$.

\subsection{Why the Einstein Model Stops Here — The Missing Broadening}

At this point, a natural question arises: we have the three processes, we have the Einstein coefficients, and we have the energy spectral density. Why don't we just push forward and find the intensity? Why stop here?

The problem is \textbf{broadening}. Recall from Chapter 1 that the interaction between light and matter does not happen at a single sharp frequency — it happens over a range of frequencies described by the line shape function $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$. In everything we have written so far in this chapter, we have been treating the photon as interacting at a single, exact frequency $\nu$. This is not physically correct.

The Einstein model as originally formulated has no line shape function — there is no $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ anywhere in the expressions for $dN_2/dt$. This means it implicitly treats the transition as a perfectly sharp delta function in frequency, which we already know from the classical model is wrong.

Since we cannot yet jump to the full quantum mechanical model (as we discussed in Chapter 1, that requires a background we haven't built yet), we will instead \textbf{modify the Einstein model} to include the line shape function. This modified version will be our working model going forward.

\section{Modification of the Einstein Model}

\subsection{The Physical Motivation: Uncertainty and Energy Level Spread}

Now let's understand why broadening appears in the quantum picture. In the classical model, we explained broadening through the damping term $\gamma$. In the quantum model, the fundamental explanation is the \textbf{Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle}.

Recall from modern physics that the uncertainty principle states:
\begin{equation*}
    \Delta E \cdot \Delta t \gtrsim \hbar
\end{equation*}

This means that if an atom spends a finite time $\Delta t$ in the upper level $E_2$, its energy cannot be known with arbitrary precision — there is an inherent spread $\Delta E$ in its energy. Said differently: the energy level $E_2$ is not a perfectly sharp value. It is a smeared distribution, with maximum atom density at exactly $E_2$ and a spread of $\Delta E$ around it.

Because the energy is uncertain by $\Delta E$, and since $E = h\nu$, the corresponding photon frequency is also uncertain by:
\begin{equation*}
    \Delta\nu \sim \frac{\Delta E}{h}
\end{equation*}

This is precisely the origin of the line shape function: the atom can interact with photons across this frequency range $\Delta\nu$, not just at a single exact frequency $\nu_0$. The maximum interaction still occurs at $\nu_0$ (when the photon energy exactly matches $E_2 - E_1$), but interaction is possible — with decreasing probability — for photons at frequencies within the spread $\Delta\nu$ around $\nu_0$.

To represent this in our equations, we introduce the notation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $E'$: a specific energy value within the spread $\Delta E_2$ around $E_2$. Similarly for $\Delta E_1$ around $E_1$.
    \item $h\nu$: the energy difference between any two levels within these tolerances.
    \item $h\nu_0 = E_2 - E_1$: the energy difference between the exact central levels.
\end{itemize}

The atom distribution within each level's spread is itself a peaked function — maximum at the exact level energy, falling off symmetrically around it. This is exactly the same shape as the line shape function $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ we derived in Chapter 1.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 2/heisenberg_broadening.jpg}
    \caption{Heisenberg Uncertainty and Broadening: Quantum uncertainty dictates that the upper energy level is not perfectly sharp, creating a blurred energy band ($\Delta E$) and consequently a spread in transition frequencies ($\Delta\nu$).}
    \label{fig:ch2_uncertainty_broadening}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Incorporating the Line Shape Function into the Rate Equations}

The modification is straightforward to state: in each of the three process rate equations, we multiply by $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)\,d\nu$. This factor accounts for the fraction of atoms that can interact with photons in the frequency interval $[\nu, \nu + d\nu]$.

The three modified rate equations are:

\begin{align*}
    \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{sp} &= -A\,N_2\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu)\,d\nu \\[8pt]
    \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{abs} &= B\,\mathcal{E}(\nu)\,N_1\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu)\,d\nu \\[8pt]
    \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{stim} &= -B\,\mathcal{E}(\nu)\,N_2\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu)\,d\nu
\end{align*}

where the factor $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)\,d\nu$ represents the probability that the atom interacts with photons within the frequency slice $d\nu$ around frequency $\nu$. Integrating over all frequencies recovers the original (unbroadened) rates, consistent with the fact that $\int g_{\nu_0}(\nu)\,d\nu = 1$.

\textbf{Limiting case — what if $\Delta\nu \to 0$?} If the linewidth goes to zero, then $g_{\nu_0}(\nu) \to \delta(\nu - \nu_0)/d\nu$, and $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)\,d\nu \to 1$ only at $\nu = \nu_0$ and zero everywhere else. This collapses us back to the original Einstein model with no broadening — the atom interacts only at the exact resonance frequency, and there are no losses beyond the photon energy itself. This is the idealised, unphysical limit. We name the condition: if $\Delta\nu \to 0$, then $\chi \approx \infty$ — the susceptibility blows up, meaning no dissipation and no radiation at all (no losses).

In short, the modification to the Einstein model is to multiply each rate equation by the line shape function $g_{\nu_0}(\nu)\,d\nu$, which distributes the interaction probability across a band of frequencies rather than concentrating it at a single point.

\subsection{Introducing the Energy Density \texorpdfstring{$\rho_\nu$}{rho-nu} and Its Relation to Intensity}

Now that the rate equations are modified, we want to work toward the intensity $I_\nu$ of the light field — which is, ultimately, the quantity we care about (we want to know whether the field grows or decays as it passes through the medium). But the intensity does not appear directly in the rate equations yet. We need to introduce a new parameter that links the energy density to the intensity, and then connect both to the rate equations.

\textbf{Energy density $\rho_\nu$:} The \textbf{energy density} is the energy per unit volume. It is related to the energy spectral density by:
\begin{equation*}
    \rho_\nu = \mathcal{E}(\nu)\,d\nu \qquad \left[\frac{\text{J}}{\text{m}^3}\right]
\end{equation*}

where $d\nu$ is the frequency range of interest (the bandwidth of the photon beam we are considering). Physically, $\rho_\nu$ is the energy stored in the electromagnetic field per unit volume in the frequency interval $d\nu$ around $\nu$.

\textbf{Connecting energy density to intensity:} Consider a small volume element inside the material — a slab of cross-sectional area $A$ and thickness $\Delta z$. The energy stored in this slab is $\rho_\nu \cdot A \cdot \Delta z$. The power carried by the photons passing through the slab is:
\begin{equation*}
    \text{Power} = \frac{\text{Energy}}{\text{time}} = \frac{\rho_\nu \cdot A \cdot \Delta z}{\text{time}}
\end{equation*}

The photons travel at the group velocity $V_g$, so $\Delta z / \text{time} = V_g$. Therefore:
\begin{equation*}
    \text{Power} = \rho_\nu \cdot A \cdot V_g
\end{equation*}

The intensity (power per unit area) is then:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    I_\nu = \rho_\nu\,V_g
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $I_\nu$: intensity of the EM field at frequency $\nu$.
    \item $\rho_\nu$: energy density.
    \item $V_g$: the group velocity of the photons.
\end{itemize}

In a medium of refractive index $n$, the phase velocity (and group velocity for a non-dispersive medium) is $V_g \approx c_0/n$, where $c_0$ is the speed of light in vacuum. Therefore:
\begin{equation*}
    \rho_\nu \approx \frac{I_\nu}{V_g} = \frac{n\,I_\nu}{c_0}
\end{equation*}

So the energy density and intensity are related by a factor of $n/c_0$. This factor of $n$ in the numerator is critical --- it will cancel one power of $n$ from the Einstein $B$ coefficient later, giving $n^2$ (not $n^3$) in the denominator of the final cross-section. We will work with $\rho_\nu$ in the rate equations and convert to $I_\nu$ at the end.

In short, the energy density $\rho_\nu$ is the energy stored per unit volume in a frequency slice, and it maps directly to the measurable intensity via $I_\nu = \rho_\nu V_g$. This bridge lets us convert Einstein's rate equations — which are naturally written in terms of energy density — into statements about optical intensity.

\textbf{Substituting $\rho_\nu = I_\nu/V_g$ into the rate equations:} With this substitution, the three modified rate equations become:

\begin{align*}
    \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{sp} &= -A\,N_2\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu)\,d\nu \\[6pt]
    \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{abs} &= B\,N_1\,\frac{I_\nu}{V_g}\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu) \\[6pt]
    \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{stim} &= -B\,N_2\,\frac{I_\nu}{V_g}\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{align*}

\subsection{The Photon Flux Density}

At this point we can see that we are getting close to the intensity, but we want to express the rate equations in terms of a more immediately physical quantity. We introduce the \textbf{photon flux density} $\Phi$:

\begin{quote}
    $\Phi$: the Photon Flux Density, representing the number of incident photons per unit area per unit time.
\end{quote}

\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    \Phi = \frac{\text{number of incident photons per second}}{\text{area}}
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

The relationship between flux density and intensity is straightforward: each photon carries energy $E_{ph} = h\nu$, so:
\begin{equation*}
    I_\nu = \Phi \cdot E_{ph} = \Phi\,h\nu
\end{equation*}

This is the definition: intensity equals the number of photons per unit area per unit time times the energy per photon. Therefore:
\begin{equation*}
    \Phi = \frac{I_\nu}{h\nu}
\end{equation*}

And substituting $I_\nu = \rho_\nu\,V_g$:
\begin{equation*}
    \rho_\nu\,V_g = \Phi\,h\nu \quad \Rightarrow \quad \rho_\nu = \frac{\Phi\,h\nu}{V_g} = \frac{n\,\Phi\,h\nu}{c_0}
\end{equation*}

Using $V_g = c_0/n$ and the Einstein $B$ coefficient $B = \lambda^3/(8\pi h\,\tau_{sp})$, we substitute $\rho_\nu = n\Phi h\nu / c_0$ into the absorption rate equation:

\begin{align*}
    \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{abs} &= B\,N_1\,\rho_\nu\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu) \\
    &= \frac{\lambda^3}{8\pi h\,\tau_{sp}}\,N_1\,\frac{n\,\Phi\,h\nu}{c_0}\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu) \\
    &= \frac{\lambda^3\,n\,\nu}{8\pi\,c_0\,\tau_{sp}}\,N_1\,\Phi\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{align*}

Now substitute $c_0 = n\lambda\nu$ (since $c_0 = \lambda_0\nu = n\lambda\cdot\nu$). The $n$ and $\nu$ cancel, and one power of $\lambda$ from $\lambda^3$ is consumed:
\begin{align*}
    \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{abs}
    &= \frac{\lambda^3\,n\,\nu}{8\pi\,(n\lambda\nu)\,\tau_{sp}}\,N_1\,\Phi\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu) \\
    &= \frac{\lambda^2}{8\pi\,\tau_{sp}}\,N_1\,\Phi\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{align*}

All factors of $n$ have cancelled completely, and we can immediately identify the factor $\dfrac{\lambda^2}{8\pi\,\tau_{sp}}\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$ as the absorption cross section $\sigma(\nu)$:
\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    \sigma(\nu) = \frac{\lambda^2}{8\pi\,\tau_{sp}}\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{equation}
\end{keyresult}

Therefore, the absorption rate simplifies beautifully to:
\begin{equation*}
    \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{abs} = \sigma(\nu)\,\Phi\,N_1
\end{equation*}

And by identical algebra, the stimulated emission rate becomes:
\begin{equation*}
    \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{stim} = -\sigma(\nu)\,\Phi\,N_2
\end{equation*}

\textbf{Note on the difference between classical and Einstein cross sections:} We have now derived the absorption cross section from the Einstein model in terms of the in-medium wavelength $\lambda$:
\begin{equation*}
    \sigma_{\text{Einstein}}(\nu) = \frac{\lambda^2}{8\pi\,\tau_{sp}}\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{equation*}
Recall that from the classical model we had:
\begin{equation*}
    \sigma_{\text{classical}}(\nu) = \frac{3\lambda^2}{8\pi\,\tau_r}\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu)
\end{equation*}
Both models naturally use the \emph{in-medium} wavelength $\lambda$. Notice that by phrasing the cross-section directly in terms of $\lambda = \lambda_0/n$, we completely eliminate the explicit appearance of the refractive index $n$ from the rule, preventing confusion. The two expressions differ structurally only by a factor of 3 because the quantum (Einstein) derivation correctly accounts for the directional degeneracy of the photon polarisation states, which the classical derivation does not handle with the same accuracy. The Einstein expression is the correct one to use in quantum optics.

In short, replacing $\lambda_0/n$ with the in-medium wavelength $\lambda$ gives the cross section $\sigma(\nu) = \lambda^2/(8\pi \tau_{sp})\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu)$, yielding the compact rate equations $dN_2/dt|_{abs} = \sigma\Phi N_1$ and $dN_2/dt|_{stim} = -\sigma\Phi N_2$.

\subsection{Summary: Rate Equations in Terms of \texorpdfstring{$\sigma$}{sigma} and \texorpdfstring{$\Phi$}{Phi}}

Let us collect the final, compact form of all three rate equations after the modification. To describe the rate of change of $N_2$ due to each process:

\begin{align*}
    &\textbf{Spontaneous emission:} & \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{sp} &= -\frac{N_2}{\tau_{sp}}\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu)\,d\nu = -A\,N_2\,g_{\nu_0}(\nu)\,d\nu \\[6pt]
    &\textbf{Absorption:} & \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{abs} &= \sigma(\nu)\,\Phi\,N_1 \\[6pt]
    &\textbf{Stimulated emission:} & \left.\frac{dN_2}{dt}\right|_{stim} &= -\sigma(\nu)\,\Phi\,N_2
\end{align*}

To further simplify, we define:
\begin{equation*}
    \sigma\,\Phi \equiv \frac{1}{\tau_{st}}
\end{equation*}
where $\tau_{st}$ is the \textbf{stimulated emission lifetime} — the average time it takes for an incident photon flux to stimulate a single atom in $E_2$ to emit. This allows us to write the rates of absorption and stimulated emission symmetrically:

\begin{align*}
    \text{Absorption rate coefficient per atom} &= \sigma\,\Phi = \frac{1}{\tau_{st}} \\
    \text{Stimulated emission rate coefficient per atom} &= \sigma\,\Phi = \frac{1}{\tau_{st}}
\end{align*}

This confirms explicitly that absorption and stimulated emission have the same per-atom rate (since $B_{12} = B_{21}$), and the net direction of population flow depends entirely on whether $N_1 > N_2$ (absorption wins) or $N_2 > N_1$ (stimulated emission wins).

\textbf{Note on spontaneous emission:} We can include spontaneous emission inside the same framework by writing its rate coefficient as $-A = -1/\tau_{sp}$, but note that this term describes emission into all directions and does not contribute to the directed beam — it cannot be collected into the mode we care about (without a cavity). The spontaneous emission into the specific optical mode we are amplifying is a much smaller fraction of the total $A\,N_2$ rate.

In short, the three processes are now fully described by the compact set of rate equations above, where the cross section $\sigma(\nu)$ encodes all the frequency-dependent interaction strength, and the photon flux $\Phi$ encodes the intensity of the driving field.
