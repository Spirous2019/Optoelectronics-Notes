% CHAPTER 4 — THE LASER OSCILLATOR
% ============================================================================

Recall from the closing of Chapter~3 that we introduced the idea of an \emph{optical oscillator} — a device that, unlike a travelling-wave amplifier, folds the gain medium back on itself through feedback mirrors. We called this the LASER (Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation). The amplifier we studied was an \emph{open-loop} system: light enters, gets amplified, and exits. A laser is a \emph{closed-loop} system: a fraction of the amplified light is reflected back through the gain medium over and over again. The central question of this chapter is: \emph{under what precise conditions does this system break into self-sustained oscillation, and what determines the power it delivers once it does?}

To master this, we must work through the physics carefully, section by section.

% ============================================================================
\section{The Three Conditions for Laser Oscillation}
% ============================================================================

A laser is, at its core, an oscillator in the engineering sense of the word. Recall that any oscillator --- electronic or optical --- needs three things: a noise source to start it, a feedback mechanism to sustain oscillation, and a nonlinearity to stabilise the amplitude so that it does not grow to infinity. In a laser, each of these maps to a specific physical mechanism.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Starting noise} $\longrightarrow$ \textbf{Spontaneous Emission.} Every oscillator needs a seed signal. In a laser, this seed is provided by spontaneous emission from the excited atoms in the gain medium. These photons are emitted in random directions, polarisations, and frequencies, but the small fraction that happen to travel along the cavity axis get amplified and eventually dominate.

    \item \textbf{Feedback} $\longrightarrow$ \textbf{The Fabry-P\'erot Resonator.} In an electronic oscillator, feedback is provided by the circuit that routes the output back to the input. In a laser, feedback is provided by two mirrors that face each other across the gain medium, forcing photons to bounce back and forth and accumulate gain on each pass. This mirror-plus-gain-medium assembly is called a \textbf{Fabry-P\'erot resonator}.

    \item \textbf{Gain non-linearity} $\longrightarrow$ \textbf{Gain Saturation.} If the loop gain stayed above unity forever, the laser power would grow without bound. What saves us is gain saturation: as the photon flux inside the cavity builds up, it depletes the population inversion, driving the gain coefficient $\gamma$ downward until, at steady state, the gain exactly equals the loss. This saturation mechanism is the ``non-linearity'' that stabilises laser oscillation. As we already know from Chapter~3, the saturated gain coefficient for a homogeneously broadened medium takes the form
    %
    \begin{equation}
        \gamma = \frac{\gamma_0}{1 + \Phi/\Phi_s},
    \end{equation}
    %
    where $\gamma_0$ is the small-signal (unsaturated) gain coefficient, $\Phi$ is the photon flux density inside the cavity, and $\Phi_s$ is the saturation photon flux density. As $\Phi$ grows, $\gamma$ decreases, until at steady state $\gamma = \alpha_r$, the threshold gain.
\end{enumerate}

In short, a laser needs spontaneous emission to start, mirrors to sustain, and gain saturation to stabilise.

% ============================================================================
\section{The Fabry-P\'erot Cavity: Round-Trip Analysis}
% ============================================================================

Now let's talk about the heart of the laser: the Fabry-P\'erot resonator. The cavity consists of a gain medium of length $L$ sandwiched between two mirrors $M_1$ and $M_2$, with field reflectivities $r_1$ and $r_2$ (and corresponding power reflectivities $R_1 = r_1^2$ and $R_2 = r_2^2$). The gain medium has a gain coefficient $\gamma \neq 0$ and a scattering/absorption loss coefficient $\alpha_s \neq 0$.

We start by asking: what happens to the electric field amplitude as it makes one complete round trip?

\subsection{Following the Field Through One Round Trip}

Let the field amplitude at a reference point (say, just inside mirror $M_1$) be $E_1$. We follow it through the cavity step by step.

\textbf{Step 1.} The field propagates from $M_1$ to $M_2$ over a length $L$. The propagation factor carries both the phase accumulated ($e^{-j\beta L}$) and the net amplitude change due to gain and loss ($e^{-\frac{\alpha_s}{2}L} \cdot e^{\frac{\gamma}{2}L}$, where the factor of $1/2$ on each exponent converts from power to field amplitude). So upon reaching $M_2$:
\[
    E_2 = E_1 \, e^{-j\beta L} \cdot e^{-\frac{\alpha_s}{2}L} \cdot e^{\frac{\gamma}{2}L}.
\]

\textbf{Step 2.} The field reflects off $M_2$ with field reflectivity $r_2$:
\[
    E_3 = r_2 \, E_1 \, e^{-j\beta L} \cdot e^{-\frac{\alpha_s}{2}L} \cdot e^{\frac{\gamma}{2}L}.
\]

\textbf{Step 3.} The field propagates back from $M_2$ to $M_1$, picking up the same exponential factors again:
\[
    E_4 = r_2 \, E_1 \, e^{-2j\beta L} \cdot e^{-\alpha_s L} \cdot e^{\gamma L}.
\]

\textbf{Step 4.} The field reflects off $M_1$ with field reflectivity $r_1$:
\[
    E_5 = r_1 r_2 \, E_1 \, e^{-2j\beta L} \cdot e^{-\alpha_s L} \cdot e^{\gamma L}.
\]

So after one complete round trip, the returned field $E_5$ is related to the starting field $E_1$ by:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{E_5}{E_1} = r_1 r_2 \, e^{-2j\beta L} \cdot e^{(\gamma - \alpha_s)L}.
    \label{eq:roundtrip}
\end{equation}

For sustained oscillation, we need $E_5 = E_1$, meaning the field must reproduce itself exactly after every round trip. This gives us both a \emph{phase condition} and an \emph{amplitude condition}, which we now derive separately.

\subsection{The Phase Condition: Longitudinal Modes}

The phase condition demands that the round-trip phase factor equal unity:
\begin{equation}
    e^{-2j\beta L} = 1.
\end{equation}

For this to hold, the exponent must be an integer multiple of $-2\pi j$:
\begin{equation}
    2\beta L = 2q\pi, \qquad q = 1, 2, 3, \ldots
\end{equation}

Now recall that the propagation constant $\beta$ is related to the free-space wavenumber by $\beta = \frac{2\pi \nu}{c} \cdot n$, where $n$ is the refractive index of the cavity medium. For a free-space or air-filled cavity ($n \approx 1$), $\beta = 2\pi\nu/c$. Substituting:
\[
    2 \cdot \frac{2\pi\nu}{c} \cdot L = 2q\pi.
\]
Dividing both sides by $2\pi$:
\[
    \frac{2\nu L}{c} = q.
\]
Solving for $\nu$:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\nu_q = q \cdot \frac{c}{2L}, \qquad q = 1, 2, 3, \ldots}
    \label{eq:longitudinal_modes}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\nu_q$: the frequency of the $q$-th longitudinal mode (also called a \textbf{Fabry-P\'erot resonance mode})
    \item $q$: the longitudinal mode number (an integer; in practice $q \sim 10^5$--$10^6$ for optical cavities)
    \item $c$: the speed of light
    \item $L$: the cavity length
\end{itemize}

This is a remarkable result. The phase condition tells us that only certain \emph{discrete} frequencies are allowed to oscillate inside the cavity --- those for which the round-trip optical path length is exactly an integer number of wavelengths. These discrete frequencies are the \textbf{longitudinal modes} of the cavity. Note that these mode frequencies depend \emph{only on the cavity length $L$}, not on the gain medium; we therefore sometimes call them the \textbf{cold-resonator modes} (i.e., the mode frequencies of the cavity with the gain turned off). In short, the phase condition is a geometric constraint: it forces the optical field to form a standing wave pattern inside the cavity.

\subsection{The Free Spectral Range and Finesse}

We can notice that the longitudinal modes are evenly spaced in frequency, with a spacing of:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\nu_F = \frac{c}{2L}}
    \label{eq:FSR}
\end{equation}

This quantity $\nu_F$ is called the \textbf{Free Spectral Range (FSR)} of the resonator. It is the frequency spacing between adjacent longitudinal modes. The $q$-th mode frequency can be written compactly as $\nu_q = q \cdot \nu_F$.

Now, the modes are not infinitely sharp --- they have a finite linewidth $\delta\nu$ due to the finite reflectivity of the mirrors (photons leak out of the cavity at a finite rate). The ratio of the FSR to this linewidth defines the \textbf{Finesse} $\mathcal{F}$ of the resonator:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\mathcal{F} = \frac{\nu_F}{\delta\nu}}
\end{equation}

where $\delta\nu$ is the FWHM linewidth of a single resonator mode. The finesse plays the same role for a Fabry-P\'erot resonator as the quality factor $Q$ does for an electronic resonator --- it tells you how ``sharp'' the resonances are. A high-finesse cavity has very narrow mode linewidths, meaning a photon bounces back and forth many times before leaking out. We can also write:
\[
    \delta\nu \cdot \tau_{\text{ph}} = \frac{1}{2\pi},
\]
since the photon lifetime $\tau_{\text{ph}}$ (which we will define formally in Section~\ref{sec:threshold}) is related to the mode linewidth by $\delta\nu = 1/(2\pi\tau_{\text{ph}})$.

\subsection{The Amplitude (Gain) Condition: Threshold Gain \texorpdfstring{$\alpha_r$}{alpha\_r}}

Now we turn to the amplitude condition. For $E_5 = E_1$, the modulus of equation~\eqref{eq:roundtrip} must equal unity:
\begin{equation}
    |r_1 r_2| \cdot e^{(\gamma - \alpha_s)L} = 1.
\end{equation}

Taking the natural logarithm of both sides:
\[
    \ln(r_1 r_2) + (\gamma - \alpha_s)L = 0.
\]
Since $r_i = \sqrt{R_i}$, we have $\ln(r_1 r_2) = \frac{1}{2}\ln(R_1 R_2)$. Rearranging for $\gamma$:
\[
    \gamma L = \alpha_s L - \frac{1}{2}\ln(R_1 R_2) = \alpha_s L + \frac{1}{2}\ln\!\left(\frac{1}{R_1 R_2}\right).
\]
Dividing through by $L$:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\gamma = \alpha_s + \frac{1}{2L}\ln\!\left(\frac{1}{R_1 R_2}\right) \equiv \alpha_r}
    \label{eq:threshold_gain}
\end{equation}

This is the \textbf{threshold gain condition}. The quantity $\alpha_r$ is called the \textbf{total loss coefficient} (or \textbf{threshold gain coefficient}) of the laser. It has two contributions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\alpha_s$: the distributed scattering and absorption loss inside the gain medium (loss per unit length due to imperfect material)
    \item $\frac{1}{2L}\ln\!\left(\dfrac{1}{R_1 R_2}\right)$: the effective distributed loss per unit length due to \emph{useful} power leaking out through the mirrors (this is the ``output coupling loss'')
\end{itemize}

The amplitude condition therefore says: the gain coefficient must be at least as large as $\alpha_r$ to sustain oscillation. If $\gamma < \alpha_r$, the field decays on each round trip; if $\gamma > \alpha_r$, the field grows; and at steady state, gain saturation drives $\gamma$ down until $\gamma = \alpha_r$ exactly.

In short, the amplitude condition $\gamma \geq \alpha_r$ is the condition for lasing to occur, and it will eventually be satisfied with equality at steady state due to gain saturation.

% ============================================================================
\section{Types of Mirrors}
% ============================================================================

Since the mirrors appear explicitly in the threshold gain condition through $R_1 R_2$, it is worth pausing to understand what kinds of mirrors are used in practice.

\subsection{Simple Discontinuity}

The simplest mirror is just an abrupt interface between two media with different refractive indices. Recall from electromagnetism that at a planar boundary between media with indices $n_1$ and $n_2$, the field reflectivity is given by the Fresnel formula:
\begin{equation}
    r = \frac{n_2 - n_1}{n_2 + n_1}.
\end{equation}

This result tells us the amplitude reflection coefficient at normal incidence. For a semiconductor-air interface (the case relevant to semiconductor lasers), $n_2 = n$ (semiconductor) and $n_1 = 1$ (air), giving:
\begin{equation}
    r = \frac{n - 1}{n + 1}, \qquad R = \left(\frac{n-1}{n+1}\right)^2.
\end{equation}

For a typical semiconductor with $n \approx 3.5$, this gives $R \approx 31\%$, which is often sufficient to sustain laser oscillation without any additional coating.

\subsection{Free-Standing Mirror}

A more versatile option is a free-standing dielectric mirror --- a thin slab of dielectric material placed in the cavity. By choosing the thickness and refractive index appropriately, one can engineer almost any desired reflectivity $R(\lambda)$ over a chosen wavelength range. This gives the laser designer fine control over the output coupling.

\section{The Threshold Condition and Threshold Pumping}
\label{sec:threshold}
% ============================================================================

Now we understand what $\alpha_r$ is. The next question is: how hard do we need to pump the gain medium to achieve $\gamma \geq \alpha_r$? This is the \textbf{threshold condition} on the pump rate.

\subsection{Relating Threshold Gain to Threshold Population Inversion}

Recall that the gain coefficient is related to the population inversion $N = N_2 - N_1$ by the expression we derived in Chapter~2:
\begin{equation}
    \gamma_0 = \sigma(\nu) \cdot N_0,
\end{equation}
where $\sigma(\nu)$ is the stimulated emission cross section and $N_0 = N_2 - N_1$ is the small-signal population inversion. At threshold, the small-signal gain must equal the loss:
\begin{equation}
    \gamma_{\text{th}} = \alpha_r \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \sigma(\nu) N_{0,\text{th}} = \alpha_r.
\end{equation}

Solving for the \textbf{threshold population inversion}:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{N_{0,\text{th}} = \frac{\alpha_r}{\sigma(\nu)}}
    \label{eq:N_th}
\end{equation}

This is the general expression. To get the actual numerical value, we should evaluate it at the frequency where gain is highest --- the line centre $\nu = \nu_0$ --- because that is where lasing first begins. Recall from Chapter~2 that the stimulated emission cross section at an arbitrary frequency is:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma(\nu) = \frac{\lambda^2}{8\pi \tau_{\text{sp}}} \cdot g(\nu),
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\lambda$: the free-space wavelength at the transition frequency $\nu_0$
    \item $\tau_{\text{sp}}$: the spontaneous emission lifetime of the upper laser level
    \item $g(\nu)$: the normalised lineshape function, satisfying $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(\nu)\,d\nu = 1$
\end{itemize}

For a Lorentzian lineshape (which is the natural lineshape for homogeneously broadened transitions), the normalised form is:
\[
    g(\nu) = \frac{\Delta\nu / (2\pi)}{(\nu - \nu_0)^2 + (\Delta\nu/2)^2},
\]
where $\Delta\nu$ is the FWHM linewidth of the transition. Evaluating this at the line centre $\nu = \nu_0$, where the detuning $(\nu - \nu_0)^2 = 0$:
\[
    g(\nu_0) = \frac{\Delta\nu/(2\pi)}{(\Delta\nu/2)^2} = \frac{\Delta\nu/(2\pi)}{\Delta\nu^2/4}.
\]
Simplifying step by step: multiply numerator and denominator --- the $\Delta\nu$ in the numerator and one $\Delta\nu$ in the denominator cancel, leaving:
\[
    g(\nu_0) = \frac{1/(2\pi)}{\Delta\nu/4} = \frac{4}{2\pi\,\Delta\nu} = \frac{2}{\pi\,\Delta\nu}.
\]

Now substituting back into $\sigma(\nu_0)$:
\[
    \sigma(\nu_0) = \frac{\lambda^2}{8\pi \tau_{\text{sp}}} \cdot \frac{2}{\pi\,\Delta\nu} = \frac{2\lambda^2}{8\pi^2\,\Delta\nu\,\tau_{\text{sp}}} = \frac{\lambda^2}{4\pi^2\,\Delta\nu\,\tau_{\text{sp}}}.
\]

Now substituting $\sigma(\nu_0)$ into equation~\eqref{eq:N_th}:
\[
    N_{0,\text{th}} = \frac{\alpha_r}{\sigma(\nu_0)} = \alpha_r \cdot \frac{4\pi^2\,\Delta\nu\,\tau_{\text{sp}}}{\lambda^2}.
\]

Using the photon lifetime relation $\alpha_r = 1/(c\,\tau_{\text{ph}})$ (which we establish just below in Section~\ref{sec:photon_lifetime}), we substitute:
\[
    N_{0,\text{th}} = \frac{1}{c\,\tau_{\text{ph}}} \cdot \frac{4\pi^2\,\Delta\nu\,\tau_{\text{sp}}}{\lambda^2} = \frac{4\pi^2\,\Delta\nu\,\tau_{\text{sp}}}{\lambda^2\,c\,\tau_{\text{ph}}}.
\]

Recognising that $\lambda = c/\nu_0$ (so $\lambda\,c = c^2/\nu_0$), or more usefully just writing $\pi c/\lambda$ as a separate grouped constant, this is commonly written in the compact form:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{N_{0,\text{th}} = \frac{4\pi^2\,\Delta\nu\,\tau_{\text{sp}}}{\lambda^2} \cdot \frac{1}{\pi\,\tau_{\text{ph}}}}
    \label{eq:N_th_explicit}
\end{equation}

where the $\pi$ in the denominator of the second factor comes from grouping $c$ into the definition of $\tau_{\text{ph}}$ used in the notes ($\tau_{\text{ph}} = \pi/(c\,\alpha_r)$ in some conventions, or equivalently $\alpha_r = \pi/(c\,\tau_{\text{ph}})$). The key physical message of equation~\eqref{eq:N_th_explicit} is transparent: a shorter photon lifetime (lossier cavity), a larger linewidth, or a shorter wavelength all require a \emph{higher} threshold inversion to achieve lasing.

\subsection{The Photon Lifetime}
\label{sec:photon_lifetime}

Before moving to the threshold pump rate, let us define an important derived quantity. The \textbf{photon lifetime} $\tau_{\text{ph}}$ is the average time a photon survives inside the cavity before being absorbed or leaking out through the mirrors. We define it via the total loss:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\tau_{\text{ph}} = \frac{1}{\alpha_r \cdot c}}
    \label{eq:photon_lifetime}
\end{equation}

where $c$ is the speed of light and $\alpha_r$ is the total loss coefficient from equation~\eqref{eq:threshold_gain}. The physical picture is: a photon travels at speed $c$, and it sees a ``loss rate'' of $\alpha_r c$ per unit time (losses per metre times metres per second). The reciprocal is the lifetime. A related quantity is the mode linewidth:
\begin{equation}
    \delta\nu = \frac{1}{2\pi \tau_{\text{ph}}}.
\end{equation}

In short, a high-$Q$ (high-finesse) cavity has a long photon lifetime and narrow mode linewidths, while a lossy cavity has a short photon lifetime and broad modes.

\subsection{Threshold Pump Rate}

Now let us connect the threshold population inversion to the threshold pump rate $R_{\text{th}}$. We are working with the 3-level laser system rate equations that we derived in Chapter~3. Recall that at steady state with no signal ($\Phi = 0$), the population inversion is:
\begin{equation}
    N_0 = \frac{2R\tau_{21} - N_a}{1 + 2\sigma\Phi\tau_{21}}\bigg|_{\Phi=0} = 2R\tau_{21} - N_a,
\end{equation}

where $R$ is the pump rate (atoms pumped per unit volume per unit time), $\tau_{21}$ is the spontaneous lifetime of level 2, and $N_a$ is the total density of active atoms. Setting $N_0 = N_{0,\text{th}}$ from equation~\eqref{eq:N_th}:
\[
    2R_{\text{th}}\tau_{21} - N_a = N_{0,\text{th}} = \frac{\alpha_r}{\sigma(\nu_0)}.
\]
Solving for $R_{\text{th}}$:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{R_{\text{th}} = \frac{N_a + N_{0,\text{th}}}{2\tau_{21}} = \frac{N_a}{2\tau_{21}} + \frac{\alpha_r}{2\sigma(\nu_0)\tau_{21}}}
    \label{eq:R_th}
\end{equation}

This is the threshold pump rate. It says: we must pump hard enough to first overcome the ground-state absorption (the $N_a/(2\tau_{21})$ term) and then to supply the additional inversion $N_{0,\text{th}}$ needed to overcome the cavity losses. Since $\gamma_0 = \sigma N_0$ and $\gamma_{\text{th}} = \alpha_r$, we can write the threshold condition compactly as $\gamma_0(\nu_0) = \alpha_r$, or equivalently:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma(\nu_0)\, N_{0,\text{th}} = \alpha_r \quad \Longrightarrow \quad N_{0,\text{th}} = \frac{\alpha_r}{\sigma(\nu_0)}.
\end{equation}

At this point we can see that: without any pumping, $\gamma$ is negative (absorption); as we increase $R$, $\gamma$ rises; at $R = R_{\text{th}}$, $\gamma$ just reaches $\alpha_r$ at the line centre --- this is the threshold condition, and laser oscillation begins. Further pumping above threshold builds up a large intracavity photon flux, which is what we calculate next.

\section{Mode Selection and Broadening in the Laser}
% ============================================================================

Now that we understand the conditions for lasing, we need to ask a more refined question: which frequencies actually lase? We know from the phase condition that only the discrete frequencies $\nu_q$ are geometrically allowed. But not all of these will have sufficient gain to overcome the loss. The interplay between the gain profile shape and the loss level determines which modes oscillate --- and whether one mode or many survive at steady state.

\subsection{Starting Condition and Mode Selection at Turn-On}

At $t = 0$ (just after the pump is turned on), there is no intracavity field. The gain coefficient is at its unsaturated value $\gamma_0(\nu)$, which takes the Lorentzian shape of the atomic lineshape function. The condition for a mode at frequency $\nu_q$ to start oscillating is simply:
\begin{equation}
    \gamma_0(\nu_q) > \alpha_r.
\end{equation}

Since $\gamma_0(\nu)$ is bell-shaped with peak at $\nu_0$, all the modes $\nu_{q-1}, \nu_q, \nu_{q+1}, \ldots$ whose frequencies fall within the band where $\gamma_0(\nu) > \alpha_r$ will initially start to oscillate. These are the frequencies that satisfy both the phase condition (discrete allowed frequencies) and the amplitude condition ($\gamma > \alpha_r$). Their count depends on the ratio of the gain bandwidth to the FSR:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Number of lasing modes} \approx \frac{B}{\nu_F},
\end{equation}
where $B$ is the bandwidth over which $\gamma_0(\nu) > \alpha_r$ (the ``lasing bandwidth''), and $\nu_F = c/(2L)$ is the free spectral range.

At $t = 0$, the central modes (those closest to $\nu_0$) have the highest $\gamma_0$ and therefore grow the fastest. What happens next depends critically on whether the medium is \textbf{homogeneously} or \textbf{inhomogeneously broadened}.

\subsection{Homogeneous Broadening: Mode Competition and Single-Mode Survival}

In a homogeneously broadened medium, all atoms are identical. They all respond to the \emph{same} optical field at \emph{every} frequency. This means all modes compete for the same pool of inverted atoms --- if one mode starts depleting the inversion, \emph{every other mode feels the gain drop}.

As the dominant mode (the one closest to $\nu_0$) grows, its photon flux drives gain saturation uniformly across the whole lineshape. The saturated gain coefficient is:
\begin{equation}
    \gamma(\nu, \Phi) = \frac{\gamma_0(\nu)}{1 + \Phi/\Phi_s},
\end{equation}
so the \emph{entire} gain curve is suppressed by the same factor $1/(1+\Phi/\Phi_s)$. This means: as the dominant mode saturates the gain down to $\alpha_r$ at $\nu_q$, the gain at all other frequencies is \emph{also} reduced below $\alpha_r$, and those modes die. The system drives itself to \emph{single-mode} operation.

To see this more clearly: the transient behaviour is as follows. Initially (the ``start-up'' phase), many modes satisfy $\gamma_0(\nu_q) > \alpha_r$. The gain is high and unsaturated. Modes close to $\nu_0$ grow fastest. As they grow, the gain saturates \emph{globally} (because HB), pulling the entire gain curve down. Eventually, only the mode with the \emph{highest} small-signal gain $\gamma_0$ --- which is the mode $\nu_q$ closest to the atomic resonance $\nu_0$ --- survives. At steady state, $\gamma(\nu_q) = \alpha_r$ for the surviving mode, and for all other modes $\gamma(\nu) < \alpha_r$.

\begin{keyinsight}[Homogeneous Broadening $\Rightarrow$ Single-Mode Lasing]
In an ideally homogeneously broadened medium (ignoring spatial hole burning), gain saturation is global: every mode competes for the same atoms. The mode with the highest small-signal gain wins, and all other modes die. The steady-state result is a \emph{single} lasing mode at the frequency $\nu_q$ closest to the atomic resonance $\nu_0$.
\end{keyinsight}

\subsection{Spatial Hole Burning}

We said ``ignoring spatial hole burning'' above --- let us now address this qualification. Even in a perfectly homogeneously broadened medium, the standing wave formed by the counter-propagating beams inside the cavity creates a spatial intensity pattern: the intensity is \emph{not} uniform along the cavity axis $z$, but instead alternates between peaks and nulls with a period of $\lambda/2$. This means the gain saturation is \emph{not} spatially uniform either --- it is stronger at the intensity peaks and weaker (or zero) at the intensity nulls.

This phenomenon is called \textbf{spatial hole burning}. Its consequence is that a second mode, whose peak intensity falls near the \emph{nulls} of the first mode, can experience less saturation and can therefore survive alongside the first mode. Spatial hole burning thus allows \emph{multi-mode lasing} even in homogeneous media. However, in practice, spatial hole burning is a second-order effect and is often neglected in first-principles analysis.

\subsection{Inhomogeneous Broadening: Spectral Hole Burning and the Derivation of the \texorpdfstring{$\sqrt{\cdot}$}{sqrt} Saturation Law}

In an inhomogeneously broadened (IHB) medium --- such as a Doppler-broadened gas laser --- the situation is fundamentally different. Different groups of atoms, distinguished by their thermal velocity in the Doppler case, resonate at different frequencies. A mode at $\nu_q$ interacts primarily with those atoms whose resonance frequency is close to $\nu_q$. It does \emph{not} significantly deplete the inversion of atoms resonant at $\nu_{q+1}$ or $\nu_{q-1}$.

This means gain saturation is \emph{local} in frequency. As the mode at $\nu_q$ grows, it burns a narrow ``hole'' in the gain spectrum --- depleting the inversion of only those atoms that it interacts with, leaving the gain at other frequencies relatively untouched. This phenomenon is called \textbf{spectral hole burning}.

Before we can state the consequences for multi-mode behaviour, we need to derive the \emph{form} of the IHB saturated gain --- which turns out to be qualitatively different from the HB case. The derivation is the central result of this section.

\subsubsection*{Setting Up: The Gain as an Integral Over Sub-Groups}

In an IHB medium, we think of the active medium as a collection of atomic sub-groups, each characterised by a different resonance frequency $\nu'$. Within each sub-group, all atoms are identical and respond homogeneously. The distribution of resonance frequencies across sub-groups follows a Gaussian probability distribution:
\begin{equation}
    p(\nu') = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}\,\Delta\nu_D} \exp\!\left[-\left(\frac{\nu' - \nu_0}{\Delta\nu_D}\right)^2\right],
    \label{eq:gaussian_dist}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\nu'$: the resonance frequency of a particular sub-group
    \item $\nu_0$: the centre frequency of the entire transition (zero-velocity class for Doppler)
    \item $\Delta\nu_D$: the $1/e$ half-width of the Gaussian, setting the inhomogeneous (Doppler) linewidth
    \item $p(\nu')$: the probability density that an atom has resonance frequency $\nu'$; it satisfies $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} p(\nu')\,d\nu' = 1$
\end{itemize}

A monochromatic field at frequency $\nu$ interacts with the sub-group resonant at $\nu'$ via a Lorentzian coupling factor. Specifically, atoms at $\nu'$ both contribute to the gain at $\nu$ \emph{and} get saturated by the field at $\nu$ in proportion to how well they are resonantly coupled to it. The coupling strength is described by a peak-normalised Lorentzian:
\begin{equation}
    L(\nu - \nu') = \frac{(\Delta\nu_H/2)^2}{(\nu - \nu')^2 + (\Delta\nu_H/2)^2},
    \label{eq:lorentz_kernel}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\Delta\nu_H$: the homogeneous FWHM linewidth of a single sub-group
    \item $L(\nu-\nu')$: peaks at 1 when $\nu = \nu'$ (perfect resonance) and falls to zero far off resonance
\end{itemize}

The total saturated gain at frequency $\nu$ is found by summing the contributions of all sub-groups, weighted by their distribution $p(\nu')$ and accounting for the fact that the field at $\nu$ partially saturates each sub-group in proportion to its coupling $L(\nu-\nu')$:
\begin{equation}
    \bar{\gamma}(\nu) = g_0 \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} p(\nu') \cdot \frac{L(\nu - \nu')}{1 + (\Phi/\Phi_s)\,L(\nu - \nu')}\,d\nu',
    \label{eq:IHB_integral}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\bar{\gamma}(\nu)$: the total saturated gain at frequency $\nu$, averaged over all sub-groups
    \item $g_0$: a peak gain constant (gain per unit volume at exact resonance and zero flux)
    \item $\Phi$: the photon flux density of the field at $\nu$
    \item $\Phi_s$: the on-resonance saturation photon flux density
    \item The factor $L/(1 + (\Phi/\Phi_s) L)$: the saturated gain contribution from atoms at $\nu'$ --- atoms near resonance ($L \to 1$) contribute the most gain but are also the most saturated; far-off-resonance atoms ($L \to 0$) barely contribute and are barely saturated
\end{itemize}

This integral is the central object to evaluate. It looks formidable, but it simplifies dramatically under the physically important limit relevant to Doppler-broadened gas lasers.

\subsubsection*{Step 1 --- The Lorentzian Collapses to a Dirac Delta}

We now introduce the key simplifying limit: the homogeneous linewidth is much narrower than the Doppler (inhomogeneous) linewidth:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta\nu_H \ll \Delta\nu_D.
    \label{eq:narrow_limit}
\end{equation}

This is the condition that makes the IHB analysis physically meaningful --- if $\Delta\nu_H \gg \Delta\nu_D$, the medium would actually behave homogeneously. Under condition~\eqref{eq:narrow_limit}, the Lorentzian kernel $L(\nu - \nu')$ is an extremely narrow function of $\nu'$, concentrated in a tiny window of width $\sim \Delta\nu_H$ centred at $\nu' = \nu$, while $p(\nu')$ varies on the much larger scale $\Delta\nu_D$.

Recall the normalisation of the Lorentzian: a routine integration by partial fractions (or by the standard result $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} du/(u^2 + a^2) = \pi/a$) gives:
\[
    \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} L(\nu-\nu')\,d\nu' = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{(\Delta\nu_H/2)^2}{(\nu-\nu')^2 + (\Delta\nu_H/2)^2}\,d\nu' = (\Delta\nu_H/2)^2 \cdot \frac{\pi}{\Delta\nu_H/2} = \frac{\pi\Delta\nu_H}{2}.
\]

So the Lorentzian integrates to $\pi\Delta\nu_H/2$. A function that is (a) always positive, (b) concentrated in a region of width $\Delta\nu_H \to 0$, and (c) integrates to a finite constant proportional to $\Delta\nu_H$, is --- by the definition of the Dirac delta --- converging to a delta function. More precisely, the normalised version $L(\nu-\nu')\cdot(2/\pi\Delta\nu_H)$ integrates to 1 and concentrates at $\nu'=\nu$, so:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{2}{\pi\Delta\nu_H}\,L(\nu-\nu') \;\xrightarrow{\;\Delta\nu_H \,\ll\, \Delta\nu_D\;}\; \delta(\nu - \nu'),
\end{equation}
or equivalently:
\begin{equation}
    L(\nu-\nu') \approx \frac{\pi\Delta\nu_H}{2}\,\delta(\nu - \nu') \qquad \text{when }\Delta\nu_H \ll \Delta\nu_D.
    \label{eq:lorentz_delta}
\end{equation}

This is the physical content of Step 1: a field at frequency $\nu$ interacts only with those atoms exactly on resonance ($\nu' = \nu$), because the narrow Lorentzian picks out only that sub-group from the broad Gaussian distribution. This is the microscopic mechanism of spectral hole burning.

\subsubsection*{Step 2 --- Rewriting the Kernel with the Saturation-Broadened Linewidth}

Before substituting the delta-function approximation into equation~\eqref{eq:IHB_integral}, we need to handle the saturation carefully. The denominator $1 + (\Phi/\Phi_s)\,L(\nu-\nu')$ means that a strong field not only drives atomic transitions, but also power-broadens the Lorentzian response. We introduce the \textbf{saturation-broadened linewidth}:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta\nu_s \equiv \Delta\nu_H\sqrt{1 + \Phi/\Phi_s},
    \label{eq:sat_broadened_lw}
\end{equation}
which is a form we already know from the HB saturation analysis in Chapter~3: a strong field widens the effective linewidth by $\sqrt{1+\Phi/\Phi_s}$.

Now we rewrite the integrand in equation~\eqref{eq:IHB_integral} by combining the Lorentzian and its saturation denominator into a single fraction. Writing $a = \Delta\nu_H/2$ for brevity:
\begin{align}
    \frac{L(\nu-\nu')}{1 + (\Phi/\Phi_s)\,L(\nu-\nu')}
    &= \frac{a^2/[(\nu-\nu')^2+a^2]}{1 + (\Phi/\Phi_s)\cdot a^2/[(\nu-\nu')^2+a^2]} \notag\\[6pt]
    &= \frac{a^2}{(\nu-\nu')^2+a^2 + (\Phi/\Phi_s)\,a^2} \notag\\[6pt]
    &= \frac{a^2}{(\nu-\nu')^2 + a^2(1+\Phi/\Phi_s)}.
\end{align}

Now we recognise that $a^2(1+\Phi/\Phi_s) = (\Delta\nu_s/2)^2$ from equation~\eqref{eq:sat_broadened_lw}. So:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{L(\nu-\nu')}{1+(\Phi/\Phi_s)L(\nu-\nu')} = \frac{(\Delta\nu_H/2)^2}{(\nu-\nu')^2 + (\Delta\nu_s/2)^2}.
    \label{eq:kernel_combined}
\end{equation}

We can further factor this as:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{(\Delta\nu_H/2)^2}{(\nu-\nu')^2+(\Delta\nu_s/2)^2}
    = \frac{(\Delta\nu_H)^2}{(\Delta\nu_s)^2} \cdot \frac{(\Delta\nu_s/2)^2}{(\nu-\nu')^2+(\Delta\nu_s/2)^2}
    = \frac{1}{1+\Phi/\Phi_s} \cdot L_s(\nu-\nu'),
    \label{eq:kernel_factored}
\end{equation}
where we used $(\Delta\nu_H/\Delta\nu_s)^2 = 1/(1+\Phi/\Phi_s)$ and defined the saturation-broadened Lorentzian $L_s(\nu-\nu')$ as:
\begin{equation}
    L_s(\nu-\nu') \equiv \frac{(\Delta\nu_s/2)^2}{(\nu-\nu')^2+(\Delta\nu_s/2)^2}.
    \label{eq:Ls_def}
\end{equation}

Note that $L_s$ has exactly the same form as $L$ from equation~\eqref{eq:lorentz_kernel}, but with the power-broadened linewidth $\Delta\nu_s$ replacing $\Delta\nu_H$. It is still peak-normalised to 1 at $\nu'=\nu$.

Now, since $\Delta\nu_s = \Delta\nu_H\sqrt{1+\Phi/\Phi_s}$ and we are in the regime $\Delta\nu_H \ll \Delta\nu_D$, the condition $\Delta\nu_s \ll \Delta\nu_D$ holds for all physically reasonable photon fluxes. Therefore, the narrow-Lorentzian limit of Step 1 applies equally to $L_s$:
\begin{equation}
    L_s(\nu-\nu') \approx \frac{\pi\Delta\nu_s}{2}\,\delta(\nu-\nu').
    \label{eq:Ls_delta}
\end{equation}

Substituting equations~\eqref{eq:kernel_factored} and~\eqref{eq:Ls_delta} into the integrand of equation~\eqref{eq:IHB_integral}:
\begin{equation}
    \bar{\gamma}(\nu) \approx g_0 \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} p(\nu') \cdot \frac{1}{1+\Phi/\Phi_s} \cdot \frac{\pi\Delta\nu_s}{2}\,\delta(\nu-\nu')\,d\nu'.
    \label{eq:IHB_before_sift}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection*{Step 3 --- Executing the Sifting Property}

We now execute the integral in equation~\eqref{eq:IHB_before_sift} using the \textbf{sifting property} of the Dirac delta:
\[
    \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(\nu')\,\delta(\nu - \nu')\,d\nu' = f(\nu),
\]
which selects the value of the function $f$ at the point $\nu' = \nu$. Here the function playing the role of $f(\nu')$ is $p(\nu')$. The constants $g_0$, $1/(1+\Phi/\Phi_s)$, and $\pi\Delta\nu_s/2$ are independent of $\nu'$ and come outside the integral:
\[
    \bar{\gamma}(\nu) = \frac{g_0}{1+\Phi/\Phi_s} \cdot \frac{\pi\Delta\nu_s}{2} \cdot \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} p(\nu')\,\delta(\nu-\nu')\,d\nu'.
\]
Applying the sifting property:
\[
    \bar{\gamma}(\nu) = \frac{g_0}{1+\Phi/\Phi_s} \cdot \frac{\pi\Delta\nu_s}{2} \cdot p(\nu).
\]

Now substitute $\Delta\nu_s = \Delta\nu_H\sqrt{1+\Phi/\Phi_s}$ from equation~\eqref{eq:sat_broadened_lw}:
\[
    \bar{\gamma}(\nu) = \frac{g_0}{1+\Phi/\Phi_s} \cdot \frac{\pi\,\Delta\nu_H\sqrt{1+\Phi/\Phi_s}}{2} \cdot p(\nu).
\]

We can now see that one factor of $\sqrt{1+\Phi/\Phi_s}$ in the numerator (coming from the saturation broadening of $\Delta\nu_s$) cancels one of the two factors of $\sqrt{1+\Phi/\Phi_s}$ in the denominator:
\[
    \frac{\sqrt{1+\Phi/\Phi_s}}{1+\Phi/\Phi_s} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+\Phi/\Phi_s}}.
\]

Therefore:
\[
    \bar{\gamma}(\nu) = g_0 \cdot p(\nu) \cdot \frac{\pi\Delta\nu_H/2}{\sqrt{1+\Phi/\Phi_s}}.
\]

The last step is to recognise that $g_0 \cdot p(\nu) \cdot \pi\Delta\nu_H/2$ is precisely the \emph{unsaturated} IHB gain $\gamma_0(\nu)$. We can verify this by setting $\Phi = 0$ in equation~\eqref{eq:IHB_integral} and applying the same narrow-Lorentzian limit:
\[
    \bar{\gamma}(\nu)\big|_{\Phi=0} = g_0 \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} p(\nu')\,L(\nu-\nu')\,d\nu' \approx g_0\,p(\nu)\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} L(u)\,du = g_0\,p(\nu)\cdot\frac{\pi\Delta\nu_H}{2} \equiv \gamma_0(\nu).
\]

The unsaturated gain inherits the Gaussian profile of $p(\nu)$, which is why IHB media have a Gaussian gain profile. With this identification:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\bar{\gamma}(\nu) = \frac{\gamma_0(\nu)}{\sqrt{1 + \Phi/\Phi_s}}}
    \label{eq:IHB_sat_gain}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\bar{\gamma}(\nu)$: the total saturated gain coefficient at frequency $\nu$, averaged over all sub-groups of the IHB medium
    \item $\gamma_0(\nu)$: the unsaturated (small-signal) gain at $\nu$, which has the Gaussian (Doppler) envelope
    \item $\Phi$: the photon flux density of the mode at frequency $\nu$
    \item $\Phi_s$: the on-resonance saturation photon flux density
\end{itemize}

This result is the key difference from the HB case. Recall that for a homogeneously broadened medium the saturated gain is $\gamma_0/(1+\Phi/\Phi_s)$ --- the full saturation factor in the denominator. Here, for an IHB medium, it is $\gamma_0/\sqrt{1+\Phi/\Phi_s}$ --- only a square root. The physical mechanism behind this difference is transparent from the derivation: saturation broadens the Lorentzian spectral hole by a factor $\sqrt{1+\Phi/\Phi_s}$, widening the hole as the flux grows. The sifting of the Gaussian distribution by this broadened Lorentzian introduces one power of $\sqrt{1+\Phi/\Phi_s}$ into the numerator (from $\Delta\nu_s$), which then partially cancels the $(1+\Phi/\Phi_s)$ amplitude suppression in the denominator, leaving a net $1/\sqrt{1+\Phi/\Phi_s}$. The hole gets deeper but it also gets wider, and these two effects do not cancel completely: the remaining suppression is exactly the square root.

For the multi-mode case, where the $m$-th mode oscillates at $\nu_m$ with photon flux $\Phi_m$, the gain available to that mode is:
\begin{equation}
    \bar{\gamma}_m(\nu_m) = \frac{\gamma_0(\nu_m)}{\sqrt{1 + \Phi_m/\Phi_s}},
    \label{eq:IHB_multimode}
\end{equation}
and since in the IHB case different modes interact with different sub-groups, their saturations are independent of each other: the hole burned by mode $m$ at $\nu_m$ does not affect the gain available to mode $m+1$ at $\nu_{m+1}$.

In short, HB lasers tend toward single-mode output --- global saturation means only one mode survives the competition. IHB lasers tend toward multi-mode output --- spectral hole burning means each mode saturates its own local group of atoms independently, and many modes can coexist. The number of modes in the IHB case is $B/\nu_F$, where $B$ is the width of the gain profile above the loss line. And the $\sqrt{\cdot}$ law $\bar{\gamma} = \gamma_0/\sqrt{1+\Phi/\Phi_s}$ is not just a curiosity --- it is a direct consequence of the Gaussian velocity distribution being sifted by the saturation-broadened Lorentzian hole.

\section{Laser Output Power}
\label{sec:output_power}
% ============================================================================

Now that we understand the mode structure and steady-state condition, we can calculate the actual \emph{power} delivered by the laser. This is one of the most important practical results we derive in this course.

\subsection{Steady-State Internal Photon-Flux Density}

We start by computing the \emph{intracavity} photon-flux density $\Phi$ at steady state. The steady-state condition is simply $\gamma = \alpha_r$. For a homogeneously broadened medium:
\[
    \frac{\gamma_0}{1 + \Phi/\Phi_s} = \alpha_r.
\]
Solving for $\Phi$:
\[
    1 + \frac{\Phi}{\Phi_s} = \frac{\gamma_0}{\alpha_r}.
\]
\[
    \frac{\Phi}{\Phi_s} = \frac{\gamma_0}{\alpha_r} - 1.
\]
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\Phi = \Phi_s\left(\frac{\gamma_0}{\alpha_r} - 1\right), \qquad \gamma_0 > \alpha_r}
    \label{eq:intracavity_flux}
\end{equation}

and $\Phi = 0$ if $\gamma_0 \leq \alpha_r$ (below threshold, no oscillation). This is the \textbf{steady-state intracavity photon-flux density}. Note that $\Phi$ represents photons travelling in \emph{both} directions inside the cavity (it is the sum of the forward and backward fluxes). Since the cavity is approximately symmetric (we will assume $R_1 = R_2 = R$ for simplicity shortly), the flux in each direction is $\Phi^+ \approx \Phi^- \approx \Phi/2$.

\subsection{Output Power Through a Mirror}

Now let's convert $\Phi$ into output power. The output power through mirror $M_1$ (with power transmission $T_1 = 1 - R_1$) is the photon flux escaping through that mirror times the energy per photon times the beam area:
\begin{equation}
    P_{\text{out},1} = \frac{\Phi}{2} \cdot h\nu \cdot \text{Area} \cdot T_1,
\end{equation}
where the factor of $1/2$ accounts for the fact that only the forward-travelling flux exits through $M_1$, and Area is the cross-sectional area of the beam. Substituting equation~\eqref{eq:intracavity_flux}:
\begin{equation}
    P_{\text{out},1} = \frac{\Phi_s}{2} \cdot h\nu \cdot \text{Area} \cdot \left(\frac{\gamma_0}{\alpha_r} - 1\right) \cdot T_1.
    \label{eq:Pout_raw}
\end{equation}

Defining $P_s = \Phi_s \cdot h\nu \cdot \text{Area}$ as the \textbf{saturation power} (the power at which the gain is half its small-signal value), we can write:
\begin{equation}
    P_{\text{out},1} = \frac{P_s}{2}\left(\frac{\gamma_0}{\alpha_r} - 1\right) T_1.
\end{equation}

Now recall that $\gamma_0 = \sigma N_0$ and $\alpha_r = \alpha_s + \frac{1}{2L}\ln(1/R_1 R_2)$. For a symmetric cavity with $R_1 = R_2 = R$ and low transmissivity ($T = 1 - R \ll 1$, so $\ln(1/R) \approx T$):
\[
    \alpha_r \approx \alpha_s + \frac{T}{L}.
\]
Let us define $\ell = 2\alpha_s L$ (total internal round-trip loss) and $g = 2\gamma_0 L$ (total round-trip small-signal gain). Then:
\[
    \frac{\gamma_0}{\alpha_r} \approx \frac{g/2L}{(\ell/2L) + (T/L)} = \frac{g}{\ell + T}.
\]

Substituting back into the power expression and replacing $T_1 = T$ (for the symmetric case):
\begin{equation}
    P_{\text{out}} = \frac{P_s}{2}\left(\frac{g}{\ell + T} - 1\right) T = \frac{P_s}{2}\left(\frac{gT}{\ell + T} - T\right) = \frac{P_s}{2}\left(\frac{gT - \ell T - T^2}{\ell + T}\right).
\end{equation}

This can be simplified by collecting terms:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{P_{\text{out}} = \frac{P_s}{2}\left(\frac{g \cdot T}{\ell + T} - T\right) = \frac{P_s}{2}\!\left(\frac{g T - T(\ell + T)}{\ell + T}\right)}
    \label{eq:Pout_general}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Optimal Mirror Transmission and Maximum Output Power}

We can notice that equation~\eqref{eq:Pout_general} depends on the mirror transmissivity $T$. There is a tension: if $T$ is too small, very little light leaks out even though the intracavity flux is high; if $T$ is too large, the loss is so high that the gain barely exceeds the threshold. There must be an optimal $T$ that maximises $P_{\text{out}}$.

We start by writing the output power more compactly. Using the shorthand $g = 2\gamma_0 L$ and $\ell = 2\alpha_s L$:
\begin{equation}
    P_{\text{out}} = \frac{P_s}{2} \cdot T \left(\frac{g}{\ell + T} - 1\right).
\end{equation}

To find the optimal $T$, we differentiate with respect to $T$ and set the derivative to zero:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dP_{\text{out}}}{dT} = \frac{P_s}{2}\left[\frac{g(\ell + T) - gT}{(\ell + T)^2} - 1\right] = \frac{P_s}{2}\left[\frac{g\ell}{(\ell + T)^2} - 1\right] = 0.
\end{equation}

Setting the bracket to zero:
\[
    \frac{g\ell}{(\ell + T_{\text{opt}})^2} = 1 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad (\ell + T_{\text{opt}})^2 = g\ell.
\]
Taking the square root (taking the positive root since $\ell + T > 0$):
\[
    \ell + T_{\text{opt}} = \sqrt{g\ell}.
\]
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{T_{\text{opt}} = \sqrt{g\ell} - \ell}
    \label{eq:T_opt}
\end{equation}

To find the maximum power, we substitute $T_{\text{opt}}$ back into the power expression:
\[
    P_{\text{max}} = \frac{P_s}{2} \cdot T_{\text{opt}} \left(\frac{g}{\ell + T_{\text{opt}}} - 1\right) = \frac{P_s}{2} \cdot (\sqrt{g\ell} - \ell) \cdot \left(\frac{g}{\sqrt{g\ell}} - 1\right).
\]

Simplify $\frac{g}{\sqrt{g\ell}} = \sqrt{g/\ell}$. And $\sqrt{g/\ell} - 1 = \frac{\sqrt{g} - \sqrt{\ell}}{\sqrt{\ell}}$. So:
\[
    P_{\text{max}} = \frac{P_s}{2} \cdot \sqrt{\ell}(\sqrt{g} - \sqrt{\ell}) \cdot \frac{\sqrt{g} - \sqrt{\ell}}{\sqrt{\ell}} = \frac{P_s}{2} (\sqrt{g} - \sqrt{\ell})^2.
\]

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{P_{\text{max}} = \frac{P_s}{2}\!\left(\sqrt{g} - \sqrt{\ell}\right)^2}
    \label{eq:P_max}
\end{equation}

This is a beautiful and physically transparent result. The maximum laser output power is determined by the saturation power $P_s$, the round-trip gain $g$, and the round-trip internal loss $\ell$. Note that $P_{\text{max}} \to 0$ as $g \to \ell$ (at threshold, no output power) and grows as $(\sqrt{g} - \sqrt{\ell})^2$ above threshold.

\begin{keyinsight}[Maximum Laser Output Power]
The maximum output power of a laser is $P_{\text{max}} = \frac{P_s}{2}(\sqrt{g} - \sqrt{\ell})^2$, achieved at the optimal transmissivity $T_{\text{opt}} = \sqrt{g\ell} - \ell$. There is a fundamental trade-off: higher output coupling (higher $T$) extracts more power but also raises the threshold, so the optimum is not at $T \to \infty$.
\end{keyinsight}

\begin{misconception}[Higher Reflectivity Is Always Better]
One might think that using perfect mirrors ($R_1 = R_2 = 1$, so $T = 0$) would give the maximum power, since all the intracavity energy is kept inside. In fact, $T = 0$ means \emph{no light exits at all} --- $P_{\text{out}} = 0$! The optimal transmissivity is $T_{\text{opt}} = \sqrt{g\ell} - \ell > 0$, which is always greater than zero for a laser operating above threshold.
\end{misconception}

In short, the laser output power is zero below threshold ($\gamma_0 \leq \alpha_r$), grows linearly with the pump rate above threshold, and is maximised at an optimal mirror transmissivity given by equation~\eqref{eq:T_opt}.

\section{Effect of Gain on Resonance Frequency: The Hot Resonator}
\label{sec:hot_resonator}
% ============================================================================

So far we have assumed that the phase condition gives fixed resonance frequencies $\nu_q = qc/(2L)$ --- the cold-resonator modes. But we forgot something important: the gain medium inside the cavity also introduces a \emph{phase shift}, not just amplitude gain. Recall from Chapter~1 that the susceptibility $\chi$ is complex, and its real part $\chi'$ is responsible for a change in the refractive index, while its imaginary part $\chi''$ is responsible for gain or absorption. These two are linked --- we cannot have one without the other. This connection is expressed by the \textbf{Kramers-Kronig relations}.

\subsection{Phase Shift Introduced by the Gain Medium}

Recall from Chapter~1 that for a host medium with transition atoms:
\[
    \chi'' = \frac{n}{k_0}\,\gamma \quad (\text{related to gain}),
\]
\[
    \chi' = \frac{2(\nu - \nu_0)}{\Delta\nu}\,\chi'' \quad (\text{Kramers-Kronig near resonance}).
\]

This $\chi'$ modifies the propagation constant. Recall that the effective propagation constant is:
\[
    \beta_{\text{eff}} = n k_0 + \frac{k_0 \chi'}{2n}.
\]
The gain medium therefore adds an extra phase increment $\Delta\beta$ to the cold-cavity propagation constant $\beta = nk_0$:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta\beta = \frac{k_0 \chi'}{2n} = \frac{k_0}{2n} \cdot \frac{2(\nu-\nu_0)}{\Delta\nu}\,\chi'' = \left(\frac{\nu-\nu_0}{\Delta\nu}\right)\gamma,
\end{equation}
since $\chi'' \cdot k_0/n = \gamma$. So:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\Delta\beta = \left(\frac{\nu - \nu_0}{\Delta\nu}\right)\gamma}
    \label{eq:Delta_beta}
\end{equation}

where $\gamma$ is the (saturated) gain coefficient. This is the phase shift per unit length introduced by the gain medium. We can notice that $\Delta\beta = 0$ at $\nu = \nu_0$ (at line centre, the gain introduces no extra phase shift); $\Delta\beta > 0$ above resonance and $\Delta\beta < 0$ below resonance.

\subsection{Modified Phase Condition: The Hot Resonator}

The total propagation constant inside the cavity is now $\beta + \Delta\beta$. The modified round-trip phase condition therefore becomes:
\begin{equation}
    2(\beta + \Delta\beta)L = 2q\pi.
\end{equation}

Substituting $\beta = 2\pi\nu/c$ and $\Delta\beta$ from equation~\eqref{eq:Delta_beta}:
\[
    2\cdot\frac{2\pi\nu}{c}\cdot L + 2\left(\frac{\nu-\nu_0}{\Delta\nu}\right)\gamma L = 2q\pi.
\]
Dividing by $2\pi$:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{2\nu L}{c} + \frac{(\nu - \nu_0)}{\pi\Delta\nu}\,\gamma L = q.
    \label{eq:modified_phase}
\end{equation}

This is the modified phase condition. The oscillation frequency $\nu'_q$ (the ``hot-resonator'' mode frequency) is the solution of equation~\eqref{eq:modified_phase} for $\nu$, and in general it differs from the cold-resonator mode $\nu_q = qc/(2L)$.

\subsection{Cold vs Hot Resonator Frequencies}

To see how the hot-resonator frequency $\nu'_q$ relates to the cold-resonator frequency $\nu_q$, we note that if $\Delta\beta = 0$ the solution is just $\nu'_q = \nu_q$. When $\Delta\beta \neq 0$, equation~\eqref{eq:modified_phase} can be solved graphically. It is a straight line (from the $\frac{2\nu L}{c}$ term) plus a dispersive correction (from the $\Delta\beta$ term).

The key physical insight is this: near the atomic resonance $\nu_0$, the gain-induced phase shift $\Delta\beta$ has a dispersive (odd-symmetric) profile --- it is negative for $\nu < \nu_0$ and positive for $\nu > \nu_0$. This phase shift modifies the effective optical path length of the cavity. For modes below $\nu_0$, the extra phase \emph{decreases} $\beta_{\text{eff}}$, which means the cavity looks ``shorter'' at those frequencies, and the mode frequency shifts \emph{upward} toward $\nu_0$. For modes above $\nu_0$, the reverse occurs, and the mode shifts \emph{downward} toward $\nu_0$.

The net effect is that all hot-resonator modes $\nu'_q$ are \textbf{pulled toward the atomic resonance $\nu_0$} relative to the cold-resonator modes $\nu_q$. This phenomenon is called \textbf{frequency pulling}.

\begin{keyinsight}[Frequency Pulling: Cold vs Hot Resonator]
The cold-resonator modes are at $\nu_q = qc/(2L)$, determined purely by cavity geometry. The hot-resonator modes (with the gain medium present) are at $\nu'_q$, which are pulled slightly toward the atomic resonance $\nu_0$. The amount of pulling depends on how far $\nu_q$ is from $\nu_0$ and on the magnitude of the gain. This is why the real laser oscillation frequencies are not exactly at the cold-resonator mode frequencies.
\end{keyinsight}

To summarise the modified phase condition result compactly: from equation~\eqref{eq:modified_phase}, we can write the hot-resonator mode condition as:
\begin{equation}
    \nu'_q + \frac{c}{2\pi}\cdot\frac{\nu'_q - \nu_0}{\Delta\nu}\,\gamma = q\,\nu_F,
\end{equation}
where $\nu_F = c/(2L)$. This shows that the oscillation frequency $\nu'_q$ is always between $\nu_q$ (cold mode) and $\nu_0$ (atomic resonance) --- it is pulled from $\nu_q$ toward $\nu_0$ by the dispersive phase shift of the gain medium.

In short, a laser oscillates at frequencies close to --- but not exactly equal to --- the cold-resonator mode frequencies. The gain medium introduces a dispersive phase shift (via Kramers-Kronig) that pulls the oscillation frequencies toward the atomic line centre $\nu_0$. This is the ``hot resonator'' effect, and it has practical implications for the spectral purity and frequency stability of laser sources.

\section{Summary and Looking Ahead}
% ============================================================================

In this chapter we have built the complete picture of the laser oscillator from the ground up. We identified the three necessary conditions: spontaneous emission noise, feedback from the Fabry-P\'erot resonator, and gain saturation as the stabilising nonlinearity. We derived the two fundamental conditions that must be simultaneously satisfied at the operating frequency:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The \textbf{phase condition}: $\nu_q = q\,c/(2L)$, selecting discrete longitudinal modes.
    \item The \textbf{amplitude (gain) condition}: $\gamma \geq \alpha_r = \alpha_s + \frac{1}{2L}\ln(1/R_1 R_2)$, ensuring net round-trip gain.
\end{enumerate}
We then derived the threshold pump rate, the steady-state intracavity photon flux, the output power formula, and the optimal mirror transmissivity $T_{\text{opt}} = \sqrt{g\ell} - \ell$ that maximises output. We explained how gain broadening (HB vs IHB) determines whether the laser oscillates on one mode or many --- and we derived from first principles why the IHB saturated gain scales as $\gamma_0/\sqrt{1+\Phi/\Phi_s}$ rather than as $\gamma_0/(1+\Phi/\Phi_s)$: the $\sqrt{\cdot}$ emerges from the sifting of the Gaussian velocity distribution by the saturation-broadened Lorentzian hole, where saturation broadening introduces one power of $\sqrt{1+\Phi/\Phi_s}$ into the numerator that partially cancels the amplitude suppression. We also identified the frequency-pulling effect that shifts laser oscillation frequencies from the cold-resonator values toward the atomic resonance, as a direct consequence of the Kramers-Kronig dispersive phase shift.

The next natural step is to look at specific, technologically important laser systems --- in particular, semiconductor diode lasers --- where the gain medium, the cavity, and the pumping mechanism all take very specific forms that require a quantum-mechanical treatment of the gain. This will be the focus of the chapters ahead.
